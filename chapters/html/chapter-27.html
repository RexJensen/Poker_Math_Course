Chips Aren't Cash: Tournaments, Part II

Why maximizing chips and maximizing prize money are fundamentally different goals

Chip Utility Is Concave

In cash games, chips equal money — a linear relationship. In tournaments, the relationship between chips and prize equity is concave: each additional chip is worth less than the last.

This means that gaining 1,000 chips when you have 500 is worth MORE in equity than gaining 1,000 chips when you have 10,000. The mathematical consequence is profound: risk aversion is rational in tournaments.

cEV vs. $EV

cEV (chip EV): Expected value measured in chips. Same as cash game EV.

$EV (dollar EV): Expected value measured in tournament prize equity. What actually matters.

A play can be +cEV but -$EV! This happens when the variance of a marginally +cEV play costs more in equity than it gains.

The Risk Premium

The risk premium is the extra edge needed to justify a tournament gamble beyond what you'd need in a cash game. It varies by situation:

SituationRisk PremiumExplanation
Near the bubble, medium stackHighestMost to lose from busting; short stacks may bust first
Near the bubble, big stackModerateLosing chips hurts less; can still pressure others
Near the bubble, short stackLowerAlready in danger; need chips desperately
Early tournament, deep stacksLowFar from pay jumps; play closer to cash game strategy
Final table, big pay jumpHighLarge $ differences between finishing positions

Adjusting Push/Call Ranges

ICM dramatically affects optimal pushing and calling ranges compared to chip-EV charts:

Bubble Push/Call Example

4 players remain, 3 get paid. You're in the big blind with a medium stack. The button (big stack) pushes all-in. You have AQo.

Cash game (cEV): Easy call against a wide button range. +cEV by a wide margin.

Tournament ($EV): Folding may be correct! If you lose, you bust before the short stack (who folds into the money). The risk premium makes this call -$EV despite being +cEV.

General Adjustments

  • Pushing ranges: Tighter on the bubble (especially medium stacks), wider when very short
  • Calling ranges: Much tighter than cash game — calling risks your tournament life
  • Aggression direction: Prefer being the aggressor (fold equity) over calling (no fold equity)

Prize Pool Equity and Pay Jumps

At the final table, each elimination creates a "pay jump" — everyone remaining moves up at least one pay level. This creates implicit collusion incentives: it's in everyone's interest (except the biggest stack) for short stacks to bust.

Deal-making (chopping) at final tables can be understood through ICM: each player's current chip stack maps to an equity value, and deals distribute the remaining prize pool according to those equities.

Key Concepts

  • Tournament chip utility is concave — each additional chip is worth less in $ equity.
  • Maximize $EV, not cEV. A +cEV play can be -$EV in tournament context.
  • The risk premium is the extra edge needed to justify a tournament gamble; it's highest on the bubble for medium stacks.
  • Calling ranges are much tighter in tournaments than cash games due to ICM pressure.
  • Prefer aggression over calling — being the pusher gives fold equity, calling does not.
  • Pay jumps at final tables create implicit collusion incentives.
