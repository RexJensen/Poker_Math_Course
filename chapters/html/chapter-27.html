Chip Utility Functions

In Chapter 26, we established that tournament chips have diminishing marginal value. Now we formalize this concept with chip utility functions — mathematical descriptions of how chip stacks map to real-dollar equity.

Chip Utility Function

A function U(s) that maps a player’s chip stack s to their tournament equity in dollars. This function is concave: U′′(s) < 0, meaning the marginal value of each additional chip decreases. U(0) = 0 (no chips = no equity) and U(T) = Prize(1st) where T is the total chips in play.

The shape of this utility curve depends on several factors:

  • Prize structure: Top-heavy structures (winner takes 50%+) create a less concave curve. Flat structures (many similar prizes) create more concavity.
  • Number of players remaining: More players = more concavity. Heads-up play has a nearly linear utility function.
  • Position on the pay ladder: Near bubbles and large pay jumps, the curve steepens dramatically at certain stack sizes.
Properties of the Chip Utility Function U(s) 1. U(0) = 0     (zero chips = zero equity)
2. U(T) = Prize(1st)     (all chips = 1st place prize)
3. U′(s) > 0     (more chips = more equity, always)
4. U′′(s) < 0     (each additional chip worth less)
5. U(s) > (s/T) × PrizePool   for   0 < s < T
    (tournament equity exceeds “cash game” equity for non-extreme stacks)

Adjusting Strategy for Tournaments

The distinction between chip-EV (cEV) and dollar-EV ($EV) is the central theme of tournament poker theory. Understanding when and how they diverge is crucial.

cEV vs $EV

cEV (Chip Expected Value): The expected number of chips gained or lost from a decision. This is identical to cash game EV and is the only thing that matters in cash games.

$EV (Dollar Expected Value): The expected change in tournament equity (real dollars) from a decision. This accounts for the concave chip utility and is what should be maximized in tournaments.

In cash games, maximizing cEV and $EV are the same thing. In tournaments, they can diverge significantly:

When +cEV Is −$EV

Situation: 4 players remain in a tournament paying $1000/$600/$300/$100. Blinds: 500/1000.

  • You (Button): 12,000 chips
  • Small Blind: 15,000 chips
  • Big Blind: 20,000 chips
  • UTG (folded): 3,000 chips

You have A♦9♥ on the button. UTG folds. You consider pushing all-in.

cEV analysis: Against the blinds’ calling ranges, pushing is clearly +cEV. You expect to gain approximately 1,800 chips on average.

$EV analysis: But UTG has only 3,000 chips (3 big blinds). They will likely bust soon, promoting everyone else up the pay ladder for free. If you push and get called, you risk:

  • Busting out 4th ($100) instead of waiting for UTG to bust and guaranteeing at least $300
  • The equity gain from winning the pot doesn’t compensate for the risk of missing a $200+ pay jump

The +cEV push is actually −$EV. Correct play: fold A9o and let the short stack bust.

When Do cEV and $EV Align?

  • Early in tournaments: Far from the money, ICM distortions are minimal. Play close to cash game strategy.
  • Heads-up: With only two players, chip utility is nearly linear. All the remaining prize money goes to 1st/2nd, and your equity is roughly proportional to your stack.
  • Overwhelming favorite: When your edge in a spot is so large that even the ICM tax can’t make it negative.

Risk Premium

The risk premium is the extra edge you need in a tournament to justify a gamble that would be profitable in a cash game. It quantifies the “ICM tax” on every decision.

Risk Premium

The additional equity (above break-even) required to make a tournament gamble $EV-positive, beyond what would be needed in a cash game. If a cash game play requires 50% equity to break even, the same play in a tournament might require 55%, 60%, or even more. The extra 5–10%+ is the risk premium.

The medium stack needs the most equity to call profitably. They have the most to lose from a bad outcome (they could ladder up if the short stack busts first). The short stack, paradoxically, can call more liberally — they’re in survival mode and need chips to compete.

Pushing and Calling Ranges in Tournaments

ICM dramatically alters both pushing (shoving all-in) and calling ranges compared to cash games. The adjustments are situation-dependent and can be extreme.

Tournament Pushing Ranges

Your pushing range depends on your stack size, position, and ICM context:

Pushing Range Adjustments

Cash game baseline (10 BB stack, button vs blinds): Push approximately top 40% of hands.

Tournament adjustments:

  • On the bubble with medium stack: Tighten to top 15–25%. Risk of busting before the short stack is very costly.
  • On the bubble with big stack: Widen to top 50%+ against medium stacks. They can’t call without premiums, making your pushes very profitable.
  • Desperately short (2–3 BB): Push wider than cash game ranges (top 50–70%). You need chips to survive, and the blinds will eat you if you wait.
  • At a final table with large pay jump ahead: Tighten ranges, especially with a medium stack.

Tournament Calling Ranges

Calling all-ins is where ICM adjustments are most severe. In cash games, you need the appropriate pot odds equity. In tournaments, you need that plus the risk premium.

  • General rule: Calling ranges are always tighter in tournaments than in cash games.
  • Bubble calling ranges: Can be dramatically tighter. A medium stack on the bubble might only call with the top 5% of hands (TT+, AQs+).
  • Big stack calling a short stack: Can call wider than medium stacks because the risk premium is lower (they won’t bust even if they lose).
  • Short stack calling: Relatively close to cash game ranges, since their risk premium is low (they have little equity to lose).
Calling Decision in Tournaments Call if: Equity(your hand vs push range) > Required Equity

Required Equity = cEV Break-even + Risk Premium

where cEV Break-even = Chips to Call / (Pot + Chips to Call)

Prize Pool Equity Calculations

To make correct tournament decisions, you need to compute the $EV of each option. This means calculating your ICM equity for each possible outcome and weighting by probability.

Complete $EV Calculation

3 players remain. Prizes: $500/$300/$200. Chips: You have 5,000, Villain has 3,000, Bystander has 2,000.

Villain pushes all-in. You estimate your equity vs their range at 60%. Should you call?

Option 1: Fold

Stacks become: You 5,000 / Villain ~4,500 / Bystander ~500 (assuming villain stole your blind).

Your ICM equity: approximately $390.

Option 2: Call and Win (60% of the time)

Stacks: You 8,000 / Villain 0 (bust) / Bystander 2,000.

Your ICM equity: approximately $435. You’re guaranteed at least $300.

Option 3: Call and Lose (40% of the time)

Stacks: You 2,000 / Villain 6,000 / Bystander 2,000.

Your ICM equity: approximately $280.

$EV of calling: 0.60 × $435 + 0.40 × $280 = $261 + $112 = $373

$EV of folding: $390

Folding is better by $17, despite having 60% equity! The risk premium here is approximately 10% — you’d need around 70% equity to justify calling.

Late-Stage Tournament Play

Final table play introduces unique dynamics that go beyond basic ICM calculations.

Final Table Dynamics

  • Pay jumps create “mini-bubbles”: Each elimination means everyone moves up one pay spot. Large jumps between positions create ICM pressure similar to the money bubble.
  • Stack size hierarchy matters: Short stacks are under pressure from blinds. Medium stacks must navigate between accumulation and survival. Big stacks can exert pressure on everyone below them.
  • Heads-up is different: When only two players remain, ICM nearly disappears. The chip utility function becomes approximately linear because only 1st and 2nd place prizes are in play. Play reverts closer to cash-game strategy.

Deal-Making Considerations

ICM provides a mathematical basis for deal-making at final tables:

  • ICM chop: Each player receives their current ICM equity. This is the most common and mathematically fair deal.
  • Chip chop: Prizes distributed proportional to chip stacks. This overvalues big stacks (ignoring concavity) and should be rejected by smaller stacks.
  • Leaving money on the table: Players can agree to lock up ICM equity and play on for the remaining difference. This reduces risk while preserving competition.
ICM Chop

A deal at a final table where the remaining prize pool is divided according to each player’s current ICM equity. This is considered the fairest mathematical split because it accurately reflects each player’s expected share based on stack sizes and remaining prizes. Compare with a “chip chop,” which unfairly benefits larger stacks by ignoring diminishing marginal chip value.

Key Concepts

  • The chip utility function formalizes the concave (diminishing returns) relationship between chips and tournament equity.
  • In cash games, maximize cEV. In tournaments, maximize $EV. These can be very different — a +cEV play can be −$EV.
  • The risk premium is the extra edge needed beyond cash-game break-even to justify a tournament gamble.
  • Risk premium is highest on the bubble for medium stacks, and lowest for big stacks and desperate short stacks.
  • Pushing ranges tighten on the bubble (especially medium stacks) and widen for desperate short stacks.
  • Calling ranges are always tighter in tournaments than cash games due to the ICM tax.
  • At final tables, ICM chop deals are mathematically fairer than chip chops for shorter stacks.
  • Heads-up play is the one stage where chip utility is approximately linear and tournament strategy converges with cash game strategy.
