Part I — Chapter 3

Using All the Information: Estimating Parameters and Bayes' Theorem

From sample data to informed reads — how to update beliefs like a mathematician

The Problem of Incomplete Information

In Chapter 2 we learned to quantify uncertainty with variance and the normal distribution. But those tools assumed we knew the true parameters — the win rate, the standard deviation. In practice, we never know these values exactly. We observe samples and must estimate the underlying truth.

This chapter introduces the statistical machinery for making inferences from data: point estimation, confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and — most powerfully — Bayes' theorem, which gives us a principled way to update our beliefs as new information arrives. For poker players, this is the formal framework behind "reading" opponents.

Population vs. Sample

Population

The complete set of all possible outcomes. In poker, the population is every hand you could ever play under given conditions. Your "true" win rate is a parameter of this population distribution — a fixed but unknown value.

Sample

A finite subset of the population that we actually observe. Your database of 50,000 hands is a sample. The sample mean is a statistic — a value computed from the sample that we use to estimate the population parameter.

The distinction matters enormously. When a player says "I win at 5 BB/100," they are reporting a sample statistic, not their true win rate. The true win rate might be 2 BB/100, 5 BB/100, or even negative — the sample is just one realization from the population, subject to all the variance we studied in Chapter 2.

Estimating Win Rates: Point Estimation

The simplest approach to estimating a parameter is point estimation — producing a single "best guess" value.

Sample Mean as Estimator

Given a sample of n observations x1, x2, …, xn, the sample mean is:

Equation 3.1 — Sample Mean x̄ = (1/n) × Σ xi

The sample mean is the best single estimate of the true mean. It is unbiased (its expected value equals the population mean) and has the smallest variance among all unbiased linear estimators.

Estimating a Poker Win Rate

A player has logged 30,000 hands of $2/$5 NL Hold'em and earned $9,000.

Win rate estimate: x̄ = $9,000 / 30,000 = $0.30/hand (or equivalently, 3 BB/100 hands).

This is our best single guess — but how confident should we be? That's where confidence intervals come in.

Confidence Intervals

A point estimate tells us nothing about precision. A confidence interval quantifies how much our estimate might vary from the truth.

Confidence Interval

A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated this sampling procedure many times, 95% of the constructed intervals would contain the true parameter value.

Equation 3.2 — Confidence Interval CI = x̄ ± zα/2 × (σ / √n)

Where zα/2 = 1.96 for a 95% interval and σ is the standard deviation per observation.

The key insight: the interval width is proportional to σ / √n. This means:

  • Higher variance (σ) makes the interval wider — noisier games require more data.
  • More hands (√n) makes the interval narrower — but only as the square root, so you need four times the hands to cut the interval in half.
How Many Hands Do You Need?

Typical live NL Hold'em: σ ≈ 100 BB/100 hands (or σ = 1 BB/hand).

Hands (n)σ/√n (BB/hand)95% CI Width (BB/100)

At 1,000 hands, the 95% CI is ±6.20 BB/100 — a player running at 5 BB/100 could easily be a loser! You need many tens of thousands of hands before win rate estimates become reliable.

Hypothesis Testing: Am I a Winner?

A related question: given our results, can we conclude that we are a winning player? This is a hypothesis test.

Z-Test for Win Rate

Null hypothesis H0: true win rate = 0 (the player is break-even).

Alternative hypothesis HA: true win rate > 0 (the player is a winner).

Equation 3.3 — Test Statistic z = x̄ / (σ / √n)

If z > 1.96, we reject H0 at the 5% significance level and conclude the player is likely a winner.

Testing a Win Rate Claim

A player has played 20,000 hands at 3 BB/100 with σ = 15 BB/100 hands.

Converting: x̄ = 0.03 BB/hand, σ = 0.15 BB/hand, n = 20,000.

z = 0.03 / (0.15 / √20,000) = 0.03 / 0.001061 = 2.83

Since 2.83 > 1.96, we reject H0 at the 95% confidence level. We can be statistically confident this player is a winner.

Note: if the same results came from only 5,000 hands, z = 0.03 / (0.15 / √5,000) = 1.41 — not significant. Same win rate, not enough data.

Bayes' Theorem: The Crown Jewel

Everything above treats probability as a frequency — how often events occur in repeated trials. Bayes' theorem gives us a different, arguably more powerful perspective: probability as degree of belief, updated with evidence.

Bayes' Theorem
Equation 3.4 — Bayes' Theorem P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / P(B)

Where:

  • P(A) = Prior probability — our belief about A before observing B
  • P(B | A) = Likelihood — how probable the evidence B is, assuming A is true
  • P(B) = Marginal likelihood — the total probability of observing B under all hypotheses
  • P(A | B) = Posterior probability — our updated belief about A after observing B

The Bayesian Update Cycle

Bayes' theorem describes a cycle of learning:

  1. Start with a prior — your initial belief before seeing data.
  2. Observe evidence — something happens (an opponent raises, a card falls).
  3. Compute the posterior — use Bayes' theorem to update your belief.
  4. The posterior becomes the new prior for the next piece of evidence.
The Tight Player's Raise

You know a tight player raises preflop about 8% of the time. You want to estimate the probability they hold A♥A♠ given that they raised.

Prior: P(AA) = 6/1326 ≈ 0.45% (the chance of being dealt pocket aces).

Likelihood: P(raise | AA) = 1.0 (this player always raises AA).

Marginal: P(raise) = 0.08 (they raise 8% of hands overall).

Applying Bayes' Theorem P(AA | raise) = P(raise | AA) × P(AA) / P(raise)              = 1.0 × 0.0045 / 0.08              = 5.6%

The prior probability of AA was 0.45%. After observing a raise from this tight player, it jumps to 5.6% — a 12x increase. The raise is strong evidence, but AA is still unlikely because most of the 8% raising range consists of other hands.

Sequential Bayesian Updating

Now suppose the tight player not only raises preflop but also 4-bets after facing a 3-bet. Assume:

  • P(4-bet | AA) = 0.95 (almost always 4-bets with AA)
  • P(4-bet | raised, not AA) = 0.15 (only 4-bets 15% of non-AA raising hands)

Our prior is now the posterior from step 1: P(AA) = 0.056.

P(4-bet) = P(4-bet | AA) × P(AA) + P(4-bet | not AA) × P(not AA)

            = 0.95 × 0.056 + 0.15 × 0.944 = 0.0532 + 0.1416 = 0.1948

Second Update P(AA | raise, 4-bet) = P(4-bet | AA) × P(AA after raise) / P(4-bet)                    = 0.95 × 0.056 / 0.1948                    = 27.3%

From 0.45% → 5.6% → 27.3%. Each action provides new information, and Bayes' theorem tells us exactly how much to update.

Bayesian Updating in Practice

At the poker table, you are constantly performing informal Bayesian updates. Every time an opponent checks, bets, raises, or folds, you revise your estimate of their likely holdings. The formal framework makes this process explicit:

  1. Assign a prior range based on position, player type, and preflop action. A UTG open from a tight regular might be {AA, KK, QQ, JJ, TT, AKs, AKo, AQs}.
  2. Weight each hand by its combination count. AA has 6 combos, AKs has 4, AKo has 12, etc.
  3. Observe an action and estimate the likelihood of that action for each hand in the range.
  4. Multiply and normalize to get the posterior distribution over hands.

This is precisely what strong players do intuitively. The math ensures consistency and prevents cognitive biases like anchoring on a single hand or ignoring base rates.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Bayes' theorem requires a prior, which introduces subjectivity. An alternative approach is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), which asks: what parameter value makes the observed data most probable?

Maximum Likelihood Estimator

Given observed data D, the MLE is the parameter value θ that maximizes the likelihood function:

Equation 3.5 — Maximum Likelihood θ̂MLE = argmaxθ P(D | θ)

For the simple case of estimating a mean, the MLE equals the sample mean — the same estimator we started with. MLE becomes more interesting for complex models (e.g., estimating an opponent's bluffing frequency from observed showdowns).

In practice, Bayesian and frequentist approaches often agree when sample sizes are large. The Bayesian approach shines when data is scarce and prior knowledge is valuable — exactly the situation at a poker table where you may have only a handful of observations on a new opponent.

Key Concepts

  • Population vs. sample: Your "true" win rate is an unknown population parameter; your observed results are a sample statistic used to estimate it.
  • Sample mean is the best single (point) estimate of the true mean — unbiased and minimum-variance.
  • Confidence intervals (CI = x̄ ± z × σ/√n) quantify estimation precision. Poker win rates require tens of thousands of hands for narrow intervals.
  • Hypothesis testing with z-scores lets us formally test whether observed results are statistically significant.
  • Bayes' theorem — P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B) — is the principled way to update beliefs with new evidence.
  • The Bayesian update cycle: prior → observe evidence → compute posterior → posterior becomes new prior. This is exactly how hand reading works at the table.
  • Maximum likelihood estimation provides an alternative that finds the parameter value making observed data most probable, without requiring a prior.
