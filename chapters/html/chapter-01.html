Why Mathematics?

People play poker for many reasons — social connection, competition, the thrill of gambling. But if our goal is to win, we need a framework for evaluating decisions under uncertainty. That framework is probability and expected value.

Chen and Ankenman acknowledge that utility theory (which accounts for non-financial preferences) is the truly comprehensive framework. However, because each individual has unique utility curves, general analysis becomes extraordinarily difficult. Instead, this book uses money won inside the game as a proxy for utility, with the assumption that players are adequately bankrolled for the game they are playing.

This simplification allows us to use a single, powerful metric: expected value (EV). A mathematical approach to poker is concerned primarily with the maximization of expected value.

Probability: The Language of Uncertainty

Probability

If n trials of an experiment produce nx occurrences of event x, the probability p(x) is defined as:

Equation 1.1 p(x) = lim(n→∞) nx / n

In plain English: the probability of an outcome is the fraction of times it occurs over an arbitrarily large number of trials.

Counting: The Pocket Aces Example

What is the probability of being dealt pocket aces in Texas Hold'em? We break this down step by step:

  • There are 52 cards in a standard deck, each equally likely
  • Probability of any specific card (e.g., A♠): 1/52
  • Probability the first card is any ace: 4/52 = 1/13 (four aces, mutually exclusive)
  • Given the first card is an ace, probability the second is also an ace: 3/51 = 1/17 (dependent event)
  • Joint probability: (1/13) × (1/17) = 1/221 ≈ 0.45%
Flopping a Flush

You hold two suited cards. What is the probability the flop comes all in your suit?

There are 11 remaining cards of your suit in a 50-card deck:

p(flush flop) = (11/50) × (10/49) × (9/48) = 990/117,600 = 33/3,920 ≈ 0.84%

This uses the dependent events formula (Equation 1.5) applied three times, since each card dealt removes one from the deck.

The Rules of Probability

Mutually Exclusive Events

Events that cannot both occur simultaneously. If events A and B are mutually exclusive:

Equation 1.2 — Addition Rule (Mutually Exclusive) p(A ∪ B) = p(A) + p(B)

Example: The probability of being dealt AA, KK, or QQ is simply 1/221 + 1/221 + 1/221 = 3/221.

Independent Events

Events where one does not affect the other (e.g., two separate dice rolls):

Equation 1.3 — Multiplication Rule (Independent) p(A ∩ B) = p(A) × p(B)

Example: Probability of rolling double sixes = (1/6) × (1/6) = 1/36.

Dependent Events

Events where one affects the probability of the other (like cards dealt from one deck):

Equation 1.5 — Conditional Probability p(A ∩ B) = p(A) × p(B|A)

where p(B|A) is the probability of B given that A has occurred.

General Union (Non-Exclusive Events)

Equation 1.4 — General Addition Rule p(A ∪ B) = p(A) + p(B) − p(A ∩ B)

Example: Probability a card is a heart OR an ace = 13/52 + 4/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13. We subtract the ace of hearts to avoid double-counting.

Complements

Equations 1.6–1.9 0 ≤ p(A) ≤ 1   |   p(certain) = 1   |   p(impossible) = 0   |   p(not A) = 1 − p(A)

Probability Distributions

A probability distribution is a complete, mutually exclusive pairing of all possible outcomes of an event with their corresponding probabilities.

Coin Flip Distribution

C = {(heads, 1/2), (tails, 1/2)}

Die Roll: D = {(1, 1/6), (2, 1/6), (3, 1/6), (4, 1/6), (5, 1/6), (6, 1/6)}

In poker, we represent an opponent's possible holdings as a probability distribution. At the start of a hand, each player's distribution is identical (all possible two-card combos). As the hand progresses, we incorporate new information — betting patterns, board cards, our own hand — to refine the probability estimate for each possible hand.

Hand Distribution Notation

When probabilities are omitted from a distribution, it implies the relative probabilities are unchanged from the deal. For example, a tight player who raises only with premium hands:

H = {AA, KK, QQ, AKs, AKo}

The relative frequency of each hand is as it was when cards were dealt.

Expected Value: The Core of Poker Strategy

Expected Value (EV)

For a probability distribution P where each of the n outcomes has a value vi and a probability pi:

Equation 1.11 <P> = Σ pi × vi   (summed over all outcomes i=1 to n)

The expected value is the sum of each outcome's value multiplied by its probability. It represents the long-run average result.

Example: The Fair Coin Flip

Your friend offers a $10 coin flip. Win $10 or lose $10 with equal probability:

B = {(+$10, 1/2), (-$10, 1/2)}
<B> = (1/2)(+$10) + (1/2)(-$10) = $5 - $5 = $0

This is a fair bet — you break even on average.

Example: The Favorable Flip

Now your friend offers $11 when you win, but you pay $10 when you lose:

<Bn> = (1/2)(+$11) + (1/2)(-$10) = $5.50 - $5.00 = +$0.50 per flip

You can't win exactly 50 cents on any single flip, but over many flips, you'll average +$0.50 per flip.

Example: The Dice Roll Gamble

Win $30 on double sixes, lose $1 otherwise:

<Bd> = (+$30)(1/36) + (-$1)(35/36) = $0.833 - $0.972 = -$0.139

This is a negative EV proposition — you lose about 14 cents per roll on average.

The Additive Property

Perhaps the most important property: expected value is additive. The EV of six bets in a row is the sum of each individual bet's EV. This is the engine of profitable poker — a skilled player accumulates positive EV over thousands of small decisions.

As Chen writes: "The free drinks and neon lights of Las Vegas are financed by the summation of millions of little coin flips, on each of which the house has a tiny edge."

Odds vs. Probability

Poker players often express likelihood as odds rather than probabilities. The relationship:

If probability = p, then odds against = (1-p) : p
If odds against = X : Y, then probability = Y / (X + Y)

For example, pocket aces have a probability of 1/221. The odds against being dealt aces are 220:1. The authors prefer probabilities because they convert directly to expected value calculations.

Key Concepts

  • The probability of an outcome is the ratio of that outcome's occurrence over an arbitrarily large number of trials.
  • A probability distribution pairs a complete, mutually exclusive list of outcomes with their corresponding probabilities.
  • The expected value of a valued probability distribution is the sum of each outcome's value multiplied by its probability.
  • Expected value is additive — the EV of multiple independent bets is the sum of the individual EVs.
  • A mathematical approach to poker is concerned primarily with the maximization of expected value.
  • In poker, we represent opponents' hands as probability distributions that we refine as new information becomes available.
