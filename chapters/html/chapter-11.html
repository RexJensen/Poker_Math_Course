One Side of the Street: Half-Street Games

The simplest poker model that still captures the essence of betting, bluffing, and calling

📄 Download Chapter PDF

What Is a Half-Street Game?

A half-street game is the simplest meaningful model of poker. It strips the game down to a single decision point: one player acts, the other responds, and then the hand is over. Despite this simplicity, half-street games reveal deep truths about optimal bluffing, value betting, and calling strategy.

In a half-street game, there are exactly two players:

  • Player Y (the bettor) — acts first. Can bet or check.
  • Player X (the caller) — responds if Y bets. Can call or fold.

If Y checks, or if Y bets and X calls, there is a showdown — the best hand wins. There are no further streets of action. This “one side of the street” structure is what gives the model its name.

Half-Street Game

A two-player poker game where each player holds a hand drawn from some distribution. There is money already in the pot (the ante). Player Y may bet a fixed amount b or check. If Y bets, Player X may call b or fold. After this single action, the hand ends.

Game Structure

The basic setup for any half-street game:

  • Both players contribute an ante to the pot, creating a pot of size P.
  • Each player receives a hand drawn from some distribution (could be a single card from a deck, a number from [0,1], etc.).
  • Y decides: bet b or check.
  • If Y checks → showdown. Best hand wins the pot.
  • If Y bets → X decides: call b or fold.
  • If X folds → Y wins the pot P (without showdown).
  • If X calls → showdown for pot of P + 2b.

The key insight is that Y has a positional advantage — Y gets to decide whether to put more money into the pot. This advantage turns out to be quantifiable.

Three Key Half-Street Games

Game 1: The Clairvoyance Game

X knows Y’s hand, but Y does not know X’s hand.

This gives X a massive informational advantage. X can make perfect decisions — calling when ahead and folding when behind. You might think Y should never bet in this scenario, since X will always make the right choice.

The Clairvoyance Game Lesson

Surprisingly, Y should still bet some hands. Even though X plays perfectly, Y can profit by betting strong hands because X must still pay to see if Y is bluffing. The key insight: X’s perfect information about Y’s hand doesn’t eliminate the value of betting — it just constrains it.

Y’s strategy: bet all hands that X cannot profitably call against (value betting), and check everything else. Y cannot bluff profitably in this game because X would always catch the bluffs.

Game 2: The Reverse Clairvoyance Game

Y knows X’s hand, but X does not know Y’s hand.

Now Y has both the positional and informational advantage. Y can construct a devastating strategy:

  • Value bet when Y’s hand beats X’s hand
  • Bluff optimally when Y’s hand is weak, choosing the right frequency
  • Check when Y’s hand is moderate and showdown value is preferable

X, lacking information, is at a severe disadvantage. This game demonstrates the enormous value of information in poker.

Game 3: The Symmetric Information Game

Neither player sees the other’s cards.

This is the standard half-street game — the most relevant model for real poker. Neither player has extra information, and the optimal strategies for both sides emerge from the mathematics of the game itself.

The Symmetric Half-Street Game

Both players draw hands from identical distributions without knowledge of the other’s hand. This is where the optimal bluffing ratio emerges — the most celebrated result of half-street game theory.

The Optimal Bluffing Ratio

This is the central result of Chapter 11 and one of the most important formulas in the entire book. In a half-street game with pot size P and bet size b:

Optimal Bluff-to-Value Ratio Bluffs / Value Bets = b / (b + P)

This formula tells us exactly how often to bluff relative to our value bets to make our opponent indifferent between calling and folding.

Deriving the Intuition

When X faces a bet of b into a pot of P, X must call b to win P + b. X needs to win at least:

Minimum Win Frequency for a Profitable Call b / (P + 2b)

If Y bluffs at exactly the right frequency, X’s EV from calling equals X’s EV from folding (both zero against the bluffing component). This is the indifference principle at work.

Pot-Size Bet Example

With a pot-size bet (b = P), the optimal bluff ratio is:

b / (b + P) = P / (P + P) = P / 2P = 1/2

This means for every two value bets, you should include one bluff. Your betting range should be 2/3 value bets and 1/3 bluffs.

At this ratio, your opponent’s calling and folding both have the same EV. They literally cannot exploit you regardless of how they respond to your bets.

The Value of Position

The half-street model reveals why position matters in poker. Player Y (the bettor) has a structural advantage because:

  • Y chooses when to put money in the pot — betting strong hands and checking weak ones
  • Y can bluff, forcing X to make difficult decisions
  • X is reactive, limited to calling or folding when Y bets
  • When Y checks, the hand goes to showdown with no further risk

Even in the clairvoyance game where X has perfect information, Y still captures value from the positional advantage. In the symmetric game, the positional player’s edge is even more pronounced.

Positional Advantage

In half-street games, the bettor’s ability to choose between betting and checking creates an inherent edge. This edge exists independent of card advantage and is a pure product of the game’s structure. It is quantifiable and grows with bet size relative to the pot.

Key Concepts

  • A half-street game is the simplest poker model: one player bets or checks, the other calls or folds, then showdown.
  • The optimal bluff-to-value ratio is b/(b+P), where b is the bet size and P is the pot size.
  • With a pot-size bet, you should bluff once for every two value bets (1/3 of your bets are bluffs).
  • Optimal bluffing makes the opponent indifferent between calling and folding — they cannot exploit you.
  • The bettor has a positional advantage that exists even when the caller has informational advantages.
  • Three variants (Clairvoyance, Reverse Clairvoyance, Symmetric) reveal different aspects of the interplay between information and position.
