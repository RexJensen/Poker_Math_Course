Part III — Chapter 21

A Case Study: Using Game Theory

Applying the theoretical framework from Chapters 10–20 to a complete, multi-street hand analysis

Applying Theory to Practice

The preceding chapters developed a rich theoretical framework: half-street and full-street games, clairvoyance results, bluffing frequencies, protection, semi-bluffs, and multi-street dynamics. This chapter takes all of that theory and applies it to a detailed, realistic hand analysis.

The goal is not merely to find the "correct" answer for one hand. Rather, it is to demonstrate the process of applying game-theoretic reasoning at each decision point — a process you can replicate in your own play.

The Case Study Method

Walk through a complete poker hand from preflop to river. At each street, analyze the situation through the lens of game theory: What does your range look like? What does your opponent's range look like? What is the game-theoretically optimal action, and when should you deviate from it?

Preflop Analysis

Every hand begins with preflop decisions. Game theory informs these decisions through range construction:

Range Construction by Position

A player's opening range depends on their position at the table. Earlier positions require tighter ranges because more players remain to act. Later positions can open wider because fewer players remain and you gain positional advantage postflop.

Typical Opening Ranges (6-max) UTG: ~15% of hands   |   MP: ~18%   |   CO: ~27%   |   BTN: ~40%+
3-Betting and Defense Ranges

When facing an open raise, the game-theoretic framework tells us we need both value 3-bets and bluff 3-bets to remain balanced:

  • Value 3-bets: Hands strong enough to 3-bet for value (e.g., QQ+, AK).
  • Bluff 3-bets: Hands that benefit from fold equity and have some playability if called (e.g., suited aces like A5s, A4s). These are the semi-bluff principle applied preflop.
  • Flat calls: Hands too strong to fold but not ideal for 3-betting (e.g., suited connectors, medium pairs).

The ratio of value to bluff 3-bets should roughly match the pot odds you offer the opponent. If a 3-bet to 3x offers the opener about 2:1, your value-to-bluff ratio should be approximately 2:1.

Stack Depth and Preflop Strategy

Stack depth fundamentally alters preflop play:

  • Deep stacks (150+ BB): Speculative hands (suited connectors, small pairs) gain value because implied odds are large. Positional advantage is magnified.
  • Short stacks (30–50 BB): Implied odds shrink. High-card hands increase in value. 3-bet/fold replaces flat calling. Push-fold dynamics begin to dominate.

Flop Play

The flop is where the most complex range interactions begin. Game theory guides us through several critical decisions:

Range vs. Range Analysis

Rather than thinking "I have top pair," the game-theoretic approach asks: "How does my entire range interact with this flop, and how does my opponent's range interact with it?" The player whose range connects better with the flop has the range advantage and can bet more frequently.

Continuation Betting Strategy

The preflop raiser has the uncapped range (contains the strongest hands) and typically has range advantage on most flops. This justifies continuation betting, but the frequency and size depend on the flop texture:

Out of Position: Check-Raise vs. Call vs. Fold

The out-of-position defender needs a balanced response to continuation bets:

  • Check-raise: A mix of strong hands (sets, two pair) and semi-bluffs (flush draws, open-ended straight draws). This is the [0,1] game in action — a polarized range.
  • Check-call: Medium-strength hands that are too good to fold but not strong enough to raise (top pair weak kicker, middle pair, gutshots).
  • Check-fold: Hands with insufficient equity to continue at the price offered.

Turn Play

The turn introduces a new card that reshapes ranges and equities. Key considerations:

Barrel Selection

After continuation betting the flop, which hands should bet again ("double barrel") on the turn? Game theory tells us to continue with:

  • Value hands that are still ahead of the calling range.
  • Strong semi-bluffs that picked up equity or have fold equity against the opponent's flop calling range.
  • Bluffs that block the opponent's continuing range (blockers).
Turn Card Impact on Ranges

The turn card can dramatically shift the dynamics:

  • Draw-completing cards (third flush card, straight-completing card): Dramatically changes equities. The flop caller's range now contains many strong hands. The flop bettor must proceed cautiously.
  • Brick turns (cards that change little): Equities remain similar to the flop. The flop bettor can continue aggression with a similar rationale.
  • Overcard turns (e.g., an Ace on a low board): May improve the preflop raiser's range more than the caller's. Can be good barrel cards.

Semi-bluffs on the turn are the final opportunity to bet with a draw before the river, where the game becomes static. The turn semi-bluff must be evaluated with the knowledge that only one card remains to improve.

River Play

The river is where the theoretical framework from Part III culminates. The game is now static — no more cards to come — and we are in [0,1] game territory:

Polarized Betting Ranges

On the river, optimal betting ranges are polarized: they contain the very best hands (value) and the very worst hands (bluffs), with medium-strength hands checking. This is the direct application of the [0,1] game from Chapter 12.

To make the defender indifferent: bluff frequency = 1/(1 + pot odds) = 1/3
Therefore: value-to-bluff ratio = 2:1 (2 value combos for every 1 bluff combo)

Larger bets allow more bluffs in the range while keeping the opponent indifferent to calling. This is why overbets can be optimal with very polarized ranges.

Call-Down Decisions vs. Polarized Ranges

When facing a river bet from a polarized range, the game-theoretic solution for the defender is to call with the top portion of their range such that the bettor's bluffs are made exactly indifferent. You should call with any hand that beats at least some of the betting range's value hands, and the breakeven frequency for calling is determined by the pot odds offered.

Synthesizing the Framework

The complete case study reveals how all the theoretical pieces fit together:

The GTO-Then-Exploit Approach
  1. Start from game theory. Build your baseline strategy using the optimal frequencies derived from [0,1] games, half-street games, and full-street games. This strategy is unexploitable — no opponent can profit by deviating against it.
  2. Observe opponent tendencies. Does this opponent fold too much? Too little? Do they overvalue certain hands? Do they bet too small or too large?
  3. Make exploitive adjustments. Deviate from GTO to profit from specific opponent mistakes:
    • If they fold too much → bluff more
    • If they call too much → bluff less, value bet thinner
    • If they bet too much → call wider, trap more
    • If they are passive → bet more, deny equity
  4. Monitor and adjust. Opponents adapt. Return to baseline when your exploitation is being counter-exploited.

Chen and Ankenman emphasize that the complete poker player uses both game theory and exploitation. Game theory is the foundation; exploitation is the superstructure built upon it. Without the foundation, the superstructure collapses. Without the superstructure, you leave money on the table.

Key Concepts

  • Range-based thinking replaces hand-based thinking: analyze how your entire range interacts with the board, not just your specific hand.
  • Preflop ranges are constructed by position, with 3-bet ranges containing both value and bluff components (like semi-bluffs applied preflop).
  • Continuation betting frequency and sizing should be calibrated to your range advantage on the specific flop texture.
  • Turn barrel selection depends on how the turn card changes equities and ranges relative to the flop action.
  • River betting ranges should be polarized (strong hands and bluffs), with a value-to-bluff ratio determined by the bet sizing and pot odds.
  • The GTO-then-exploit approach uses game theory as a baseline and layers exploitive adjustments on top when opponent tendencies are identified.
  • The complete player integrates both game-theoretic foundations (unexploitable baseline) and exploitive adjustments (profiting from opponent mistakes).
