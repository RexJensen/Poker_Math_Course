Part IV — Chapter 23

Adding Uncertainty: Risk of Ruin with Uncertain Win Rates

What happens when you don't even know if you're a winning player?

📄 Download Chapter PDF

The Uncertainty Problem

In Chapter 22, we developed the risk of ruin formula under a critical assumption: that we know our exact win rate. We plugged in a precise value for μ (our expected win rate) and computed the probability of going broke.

But here is the uncomfortable truth: we don't know our true win rate. All we have is a sample estimate — a noisy measurement derived from a finite number of hands played. The win rate we calculate from our database is not our true win rate; it is merely our best guess.

This distinction matters enormously. A player who has won at 3 BB/100 over 50,000 hands might actually be a 5 BB/100 winner, a 1 BB/100 winner, or even a losing player who ran well. The uncertainty about the true win rate introduces an entirely new layer of risk that must be accounted for.

Sample Win Rate vs. True Win Rate

The sample win rate (μ̂) is the win rate observed over a finite number of hands. The true win rate (μ) is the theoretical long-run average that we would observe over an infinite number of hands. We can never know μ exactly; we can only estimate it from μ̂.

Compound Uncertainty: Two Layers of Risk

When we account for win rate uncertainty, we must deal with two distinct sources of randomness simultaneously:

Layer 1: Session Variance (Normal Poker Variance)

Even if your true win rate is exactly 3 BB/100, you will experience wide swings session to session. This is the variance we addressed in Chapter 22 — the natural randomness of poker outcomes. Your results fluctuate around your true win rate with a standard deviation determined by your playing style and game type.

Layer 2: Win Rate Uncertainty

On top of session variance, you don't know where the "center" of your distribution actually is. Maybe your true win rate is 3 BB/100, or maybe it's 1 BB/100, or maybe it's −0.5 BB/100. This uncertainty about the mean itself creates an additional layer of risk that compounds with the session variance.

The combination of these two layers makes the actual risk of ruin substantially higher than the naive calculation would suggest. You are uncertain not only about whether you will run bad (Layer 1) but also about whether "running normally" is even good enough (Layer 2).

Bayesian Win Rate Estimation

To handle win rate uncertainty rigorously, we use Bayesian inference. Instead of treating the win rate as a single number, we treat it as a probability distribution — a range of possible values, each with an associated likelihood.

The Prior Distribution

Before playing any hands, we start with a prior belief about our win rate. This might be based on:

  • General population statistics (most players lose)
  • Self-assessment of skill level
  • Results at similar stakes or games
  • Coaching or study background

A reasonable prior for a player new to a stake might be a normal distribution centered at 0 BB/100 with a standard deviation of 5 BB/100, reflecting genuine uncertainty about whether they are a winner or loser at this level.

Updating with Observed Data

As we accumulate hands, we update our belief. If we observe a win rate of μ̂ over n hands with a per-hand standard deviation of σ, the standard error of our sample mean is:

Standard Error of the Sample Win Rate SE = σ / √n

The more hands we play, the smaller SE becomes, and the more confident we can be that our sample win rate is close to our true win rate.

The Posterior Distribution

Using Bayes' theorem, we combine the prior with the observed data to produce the posterior distribution — our refined belief about the true win rate after seeing our results.

Posterior Win Rate Distribution (Normal-Normal Model)

If the prior is Normal(μ0, σ0²) and the data yields sample mean μ̂ with variance σ²/n, the posterior is:

Posterior Mean and Variance μpost = (μ0/σ0² + n·μ̂/σ²) / (1/σ0² + n/σ²)

σpost² = 1 / (1/σ0² + n/σ²)

The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the sample mean. With more data, the posterior pulls toward the sample mean; with less data, the prior dominates.

Example: Updating Your Win Rate Belief

Suppose your prior is μ0 = 0 BB/100, σ0 = 5 BB/100. You play 50,000 hands with σ = 80 BB/100 and observe μ̂ = 3 BB/100.

Standard error: SE = 80/√50,000 = 80/223.6 ≈ 0.358 BB/100

The posterior mean will be pulled toward 3 BB/100, and the posterior standard deviation will be much smaller than the prior — but still meaningfully nonzero. There remains genuine uncertainty about whether your true win rate is 2, 3, or 4 BB/100.

Effective Risk of Ruin

Now we arrive at the key result. Instead of computing RoR at a single point estimate, we integrate the risk of ruin over all possible true win rates, weighted by their posterior probability:

Effective Risk of Ruin
The Effective RoR Formula Effective RoR = ∫ RoR(μ) × p(μ) dμ

Where RoR(μ) is the risk of ruin formula from Chapter 22 evaluated at win rate μ, and p(μ) is the posterior probability density of μ being the true win rate.

Why Effective RoR Is Always Higher

This is the central insight of the chapter: the effective RoR is always greater than or equal to the RoR computed from the point estimate alone.

The reason is mathematical: the risk of ruin function RoR(μ) is convex in μ. By Jensen's inequality:

Jensen's Inequality Applied E[RoR(μ)] ≥ RoR(E[μ])

In practical terms: the scenarios where your true win rate is worse than estimated contribute disproportionately to the effective RoR. A small probability of being a losing player can dramatically increase your overall ruin risk.

The Asymmetry of Uncertainty

Consider a player with estimated μ = 3 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100 with a 30 buy-in bankroll (3,000 BB).

  • Naive RoR (at μ=3): approximately 5%
  • If true μ = 4: RoR drops to ~2%
  • If true μ = 2: RoR rises to ~14%
  • If true μ = 0: RoR = 100% (certain ruin!)

Notice the asymmetry: being 1 BB/100 worse than estimated hurts much more than being 1 BB/100 better helps. This is why uncertainty always increases effective risk.

The "Am I a Winner?" Problem

Perhaps the most sobering aspect of win rate uncertainty is this: even after a substantial sample of hands, there may be a meaningful probability that your true win rate is negative.

Sample Size and Confidence

When the probability of being a loser is nontrivial, the effective RoR increases dramatically because RoR(μ) = 100% for any μ ≤ 0. Even a 5% chance of being a break-even or losing player can push the effective RoR well above what the naive estimate suggests.

The Confidence Trap

Many players fall into a dangerous pattern: they observe a positive sample win rate, compute the naive RoR, and conclude they are safe. But if they haven't accounted for the possibility that their true win rate might be lower (or negative), they are systematically underestimating their risk.

Practical Implications

New Players: Be Conservative

A player who has played only 20,000–50,000 hands has very wide confidence intervals around their true win rate. The uncertainty-adjusted bankroll requirements are substantially higher than the naive calculation suggests.

Rule of Thumb With limited data (<50k hands), consider adding 50–100% to your naive bankroll requirement to account for win rate uncertainty.

Established Players: More Confidence, Less Padding

A player with 500,000+ hands at a stake has narrow confidence intervals. Their effective RoR will be much closer to the naive RoR, and they can be more aggressive with bankroll decisions.

Even Winners Can Go Broke

If you underestimate your uncertainty, you might be playing at stakes that are too high for your actual edge. The risk of ruin formulas from Chapter 22 are only as reliable as the win rate estimate you feed into them. Garbage in, garbage out.

Moving Between Stakes

When you move to a new stake, your uncertainty about your win rate at that level resets. You might be a proven 4 BB/100 winner at $1/$2, but you have very little data at $2/$5. The higher uncertainty at the new stake demands a larger bankroll cushion than the naive RoR would indicate.

Moving Up in Stakes

A $1/$2 regular moves to $2/$5. They have:

  • 200,000 hands at $1/$2 with 4 BB/100 win rate (high confidence)
  • 5,000 hands at $2/$5 with 2 BB/100 win rate (very low confidence)

At $2/$5, the standard error is 80/√5,000 ≈ 1.13 BB/100. The 95% confidence interval for the $2/$5 win rate is roughly [−0.2, 4.2] — they might not even be a winner at this level! The bankroll requirement should reflect this massive uncertainty.

Key Concepts

  • We never know our true win rate — we only have a sample estimate with an associated standard error.
  • Risk of ruin must account for two layers of uncertainty: normal session variance and uncertainty about the true win rate itself.
  • Bayesian inference provides a principled way to express our uncertainty as a posterior distribution over possible win rates.
  • The effective RoR integrates over all possible win rates: Effective RoR = ∫ RoR(μ) × p(μ) dμ.
  • Effective RoR is always higher than naive RoR due to the convexity of the RoR function (Jensen's inequality).
  • Players with limited data should maintain larger bankrolls to compensate for win rate uncertainty.
  • Moving to a new stake resets your uncertainty, requiring more conservative bankroll management.
