Part IV — Chapter 22

Staying in Action: Risk of Ruin

When expected value alone is not enough — bankroll management, variance, and the mathematics of going broke

Suspending the EV-Only Assumption

Throughout the first three parts of the book, Chen and Ankenman operated under a simplifying assumption: players are "adequately bankrolled." This means they assumed players could absorb any short-term losses without risk to their continued play.

This chapter confronts the reality: what does "adequately bankrolled" actually mean? How much money do you need to play a game with a positive expectation without a significant risk of going broke?

The Bankroll Paradox

Consider two scenarios:

  • Player A: Has a 5 BB/100 win rate (excellent), but only 10 buy-ins (1,000 BB) in their bankroll.
  • Player B: Has a 2 BB/100 win rate (modest), but has 50 buy-ins (5,000 BB) in their bankroll.

Which player is more likely to go broke? Surprisingly, Player A with the higher win rate may have a greater risk of ruin if variance is high. The answer depends on the relationship between win rate, variance, and bankroll — which is exactly what Risk of Ruin quantifies.

The Risk of Ruin Model

Risk of Ruin (RoR) is the probability that a player loses their entire bankroll before achieving a target (or, in the infinite-horizon case, ever). The model makes the following assumptions:

Risk of Ruin Assumptions
  • The player repeatedly plays the same game (same stakes, same opponents, same conditions).
  • The player has a fixed, constant win rate (μ) measured in BB per hand or BB per 100 hands.
  • Results follow a distribution with fixed variance (σ²).
  • The player stops only if their bankroll reaches zero (ruin).
  • No reloading — if the bankroll hits zero, the player is done.

Under these assumptions, the probability of ruin has a closed-form solution, originally worked out by Bill Chen and Tom Weideman using results from random walk theory and the theory of Brownian motion.

The Risk of Ruin Formula

Risk of Ruin Formula RoR = e−2μB / σ²

Where:

Key Insight: Exponential Decay

The formula reveals that Risk of Ruin decreases exponentially with bankroll size. This has profound implications:

  • Doubling your bankroll does not merely halve your RoR — it squares it (dramatically reduces it).
  • If RoR = 20% with bankroll B, then RoR ≈ 4% with bankroll 2B, and RoR ≈ 0.16% with bankroll 4B.
  • This exponential relationship means that the difference between 20 buy-ins and 40 buy-ins is much more significant than the difference between 100 and 120 buy-ins.
Worked Example

A player has a win rate of μ = 3 BB/100 hands and standard deviation σ = 15 BB/100 hands. What is their Risk of Ruin with a 3,000 BB (30 buy-in) bankroll?

RoR = e−2(3)(3000) / (15)²
= e−18000 / 225
= e−80
≈ 1.8 × 10−35
≈ Effectively zero

With these parameters, 30 buy-ins is more than enough. But what if the win rate drops to 1 BB/100?

RoR = e−2(1)(3000) / 225
= e−26.67
≈ 2.6 × 10−12
≈ Still essentially zero

Now with only 1,000 BB (10 buy-ins) and μ = 1 BB/100:

RoR = e−2(1)(1000) / 225
= e−8.89
≈ 0.014% (very low)

The formula shows that even modest win rates provide excellent protection against ruin with reasonable bankrolls.

Properties of Risk of Ruin

Property 1: Non-Positive EV Means Certain Ruin

If μ ≤ 0 (break-even or losing player), then RoR = 100%. A player with zero or negative expected value will eventually go broke with certainty, regardless of bankroll size. It is only a matter of time. This is mathematically guaranteed by the properties of random walks with non-positive drift.

Property 2: Win Rate and Variance Are Substitutes

The exponent −2μB/σ² shows that RoR depends on the ratio of win rate to variance. A player with μ = 2, σ = 10 has the same RoR as a player with μ = 8, σ = 20 (both have μ/σ² = 0.02), assuming the same bankroll.

Bankroll Requirements

Given the RoR formula, we can solve for the bankroll needed to achieve a target Risk of Ruin:

Required Bankroll for Target RoR B = −σ² × ln(RoRtarget) / (2μ)

Practical Implications

Should You Play the Big Game?

You have $10,000 in your bankroll. A lucrative $5/$10 game is running (buy-in $1,000, so you have 10 buy-ins). Your estimated win rate is 3 BB/100 with σ = 15 BB/100.

RoR = e−2(3)(1000) / 225 = e−26.67 ≈ 0.000000000003

Wait — that seems great. But consider: can you really sustain a 3 BB/100 win rate at a higher stake? If your true win rate at $5/$10 is only 0.5 BB/100:

RoR = e−2(0.5)(1000) / 225 = e−4.44 ≈ 1.2%

And if you are actually a small loser at this stake (μ = −0.5 BB/100), your RoR is 100%. The key question is not just how many buy-ins you have, but whether your win rate at the higher stake is truly positive.

Moving Up in Stakes

The game-theoretically sound approach to moving up:

  1. Estimate your likely win rate at the higher stake (usually lower than your current win rate).
  2. Calculate RoR at the higher stake with your current bankroll.
  3. Move up only when your RoR at the higher stake is acceptably low (typically < 5%).
  4. Move back down if your bankroll drops below the threshold. This is not failure; it is mathematically sound bankroll management.
Going Broke Does Not Mean You Are a Bad Player

A critical insight from Chen and Ankenman: going broke indicates only that you were underbankrolled for the game you were playing, given your win rate and the game's variance. Many skilled players have gone broke simply because they played too high for their bankroll. Risk of Ruin is not a skill metric — it is a bankroll adequacy metric.

Key Concepts

  • Risk of Ruin (RoR) is the probability of losing your entire bankroll. It depends on three factors: win rate (μ), variance (σ²), and bankroll size (B).
  • The RoR formula is e−2μB/σ², derived from random walk theory by Bill Chen and Tom Weideman.
  • If your expected value is zero or negative (μ ≤ 0), your Risk of Ruin is 100% — guaranteed eventual ruin.
  • RoR decreases exponentially with bankroll: doubling your bankroll squares (not halves) your risk of ruin.
  • 20 buy-ins is often insufficient for typical winning players. Most need 30–50+ buy-ins for an acceptable (<5%) RoR.
  • Going broke means you were underbankrolled, not necessarily that you are a bad player. Bankroll management is a separate skill from poker strategy.
  • Move up in stakes only when your RoR at the higher stake is acceptably low, and move back down if your bankroll drops.
