Small Bets, Big Pots: No-Fold [0,1] Games

A continuous hand distribution reveals the pure mathematics of when to bet — even when the opponent always calls

📄 Download Chapter PDF

Beyond Discrete Hands

The AKQ game gave us powerful insights, but it has only three possible hands. Real poker has many more, and the discrete nature of three cards limits the depth of our analysis. To go deeper, Chen and Ankenman introduce a brilliant simplification: the [0,1] game.

Instead of dealing cards from a deck, each player is dealt a real number uniformly distributed on the interval [0,1]. Higher numbers are better hands. This continuous distribution eliminates the combinatorial complexity of real poker while preserving its essential mathematical structure: uncertainty about the opponent's hand, and the decision of whether and how much to bet.

In this chapter, we study the simplest version: the no-fold [0,1] game, where players cannot fold. This constraint might seem to eliminate all the interesting strategic decisions, but as we will see, the question of when to bet remains rich and surprising.

The [0,1] Game Framework

The [0,1] Game

Each player is dealt a "hand" — a real number drawn uniformly from the interval [0,1]. Higher numbers are stronger hands. The hand value 0 is the worst possible hand, and 1 is the best possible hand.

Hand value x ~ Uniform[0, 1]
P(hand ≤ x) = x     for all x ∈ [0, 1]

The probability of being dealt a hand less than or equal to any value x is simply x itself. This elegant property makes the mathematics tractable.

Key Properties of the Uniform Distribution

  • Median hand: 0.5 (exactly half the possible hands are better, half are worse)
  • Percentile interpretation: A hand of 0.75 beats 75% of all possible hands
  • Symmetry: Both players draw from the same distribution, so neither has an inherent advantage from the deal
  • No ties: The probability of both players having exactly the same hand is zero (a property of continuous distributions)
Thinking in [0,1] Terms

Consider mapping real poker hands to the [0,1] interval. If you hold a hand that beats 80% of all possible hands, your [0,1] value is approximately 0.80. A hand that beats only 30% of hands maps to 0.30.

The beauty of this framework is that we do not need to worry about suits, card combinations, or board textures. All of that complexity is abstracted into a single number representing hand strength.

The No-Fold Condition

In this variant of the [0,1] game, players cannot fold. They can only bet or check (as the first player), and call or check (as the second player). If a bet is made, the opponent must call.

No-Fold [0,1] Game Setup
  • Both players ante, creating a pot of size P
  • Each player receives a hand from Uniform[0,1]
  • Player 1 can bet b or check
  • If Player 1 bets, Player 2 must call (no folding allowed)
  • If Player 1 checks, Player 2 can bet b or check
  • If Player 2 bets after a check, Player 1 must call
  • Highest hand wins the pot (plus any bets)

Why Study No-Fold Games?

This might seem unrealistic — who plays poker without the option to fold? But the no-fold game models important real situations:

  • Huge pot, tiny bet: When the pot is very large relative to the bet size, folding is almost always wrong because the pot odds are so favorable. The no-fold game is the limiting case.
  • Last street with committed stacks: When players have already committed most of their stacks, the remaining bet is small relative to the pot, making folding incorrect.
  • Isolating the betting decision: By removing the fold option, we can study the pure question of when betting adds value compared to checking.

When to Bet: The Core Question

Even though the opponent always calls, betting is not always correct. This is a crucial insight. Let us examine why.

Expected Value of Betting vs. Checking

Suppose you hold hand x and the pot is P. If you check, your EV depends only on whether your hand is better than the opponent's (which happens with probability x, since your opponent's hand is uniform on [0,1]):

EV of Checking EV(check | hand = x) = x × P − (1 − x) × 0 = x × P

(You win the pot P with probability x, and win nothing otherwise.
We measure EV relative to the current pot contribution.)

If you bet b and the opponent must call:

EV of Betting (opponent always calls) EV(bet | hand = x) = x × (P + b) − (1 − x) × b
                     = x × P + x × b − b + x × b
                     = x × P + (2x − 1) × b

The difference between betting and checking is:

EV(bet) − EV(check) ΔEV = (2x − 1) × b

This is the key equation. The sign of ΔEV depends entirely on whether x is above or below 0.5:

The Betting Threshold
  • If x > 0.5 (above median): ΔEV > 0, so betting is better than checking
  • If x = 0.5 (exactly median): ΔEV = 0, so betting and checking are equivalent
  • If x < 0.5 (below median): ΔEV < 0, so checking is better than betting

In the simple no-fold game where only one player can bet, the optimal threshold is x = 0.5. Bet with the top half of your range, check with the bottom half.

Intuition Behind the Threshold

When you hold hand 0.7 and bet, you win the extra bet b from the 70% of hands you beat, but lose the extra bet b to the 30% of hands that beat you. Net gain: (0.7 − 0.3) × b = +0.4b. Betting is clearly profitable.

When you hold hand 0.3 and bet, you win the extra bet from only 30% of hands but lose it to 70%. Net: (0.3 − 0.7) × b = −0.4b. Betting costs you money even though the opponent cannot fold.

The median hand (0.5) is the exact break-even point: you beat as many hands as beat you, so the extra bet neither helps nor hurts.

The Full Game: Both Players Can Bet

When both players can act (Player 1 bets or checks, then Player 2 responds), the analysis becomes more complex. The key insight is that the strategies interact.

Sequential Decision Making

If Player 1 bets, Player 2 must call (no-fold condition). But if Player 1 checks, Player 2 faces the same decision: bet or check? Player 2's optimal threshold may differ from Player 1's because the check from Player 1 provides information.

Information from Checking

If Player 1 uses the threshold strategy (bet with x > 0.5, check with x ≤ 0.5), then when Player 1 checks, Player 2 knows that Player 1's hand is at most 0.5. This information shifts Player 2's optimal strategy.

Against a range capped at 0.5, Player 2's threshold for betting profitably drops below 0.5. Player 2 can bet thinner because the opponent's range is weaker.

Equilibrium Considerations

Finding the full equilibrium requires both players to optimize simultaneously. Player 1 must account for the fact that checking reveals information (which Player 2 exploits), while Player 2 must account for the information content of Player 1's check.

The mathematical solution involves solving difference equations (covered in the appendix to this chapter). The key qualitative results are:

  • Player 1 (out of position) bets with slightly more than just the top half of hands
  • Player 2 (in position) exploits checks by betting with a wider range
  • The equilibrium strategies depend on the ratio of bet size to pot size (b/P)

The Mathematical Solution

The optimal betting threshold can be derived precisely. For the simplest case where only one player can bet (half-street no-fold game):

Half-Street No-Fold Threshold Bet if x > 1/2
Check if x ≤ 1/2

This threshold is independent of bet size in the no-fold game.

This remarkable result means that regardless of whether the bet is tiny or enormous, you should bet exactly when your hand is above the median. The reason: the no-fold condition means the bet size only scales the magnitude of the gain or loss, not its sign.

Value of Betting Optimally

The expected gain from following the optimal strategy (compared to always checking) can be computed by integrating the gain over all hands above the threshold:

Expected Gain from Optimal Betting ∫1/21 (2x − 1) × b × dx = b × [x² − x]1/21 = b × (0 − (−1/4)) = b/4

On average, the ability to selectively bet (rather than always checking) is worth b/4 to the bettor per hand. This quantifies the value of having the option to bet, even when the opponent always calls.

Difference Equations

For the full two-player game, the solution involves difference equations rather than simple algebra. The appendix to this chapter develops the mathematical machinery. The key concept: each player's optimal strategy is a function (not just a single number), mapping hand values to betting probabilities. These functions are determined by a system of equations that encode the optimality conditions.

Difference Equations in Game Theory

A difference equation relates the value of a function at one point to its value at neighboring points. In the [0,1] game, the optimal strategy at hand value x depends on the strategies at nearby hand values, creating a system that must be solved simultaneously.

The solutions to these equations yield the equilibrium strategies and the game's value (expected payoff under optimal play).

Connection to Real Poker

The no-fold [0,1] game, despite its simplicity, teaches several lessons that apply directly to real poker:

Lesson 1: Selective Betting Has Value

Even when the opponent always calls, you should not bet every hand. Below-median hands lose more by betting than by checking. This contradicts the naive intuition that "if they always call, I should always bet my good hands." The subtlety is that betting bad hands is actively harmful.

Lesson 2: The Threshold is at the Median

The optimal betting threshold is where your hand beats exactly half the opponent's range. In real poker, this translates to: bet when you are more likely than not to have the best hand against the opponent's calling range.

Lesson 3: Information Leaks from Checking

When you check, the opponent learns something about your hand (it is probably not very strong). In the [0,1] game, this information is precise and exploitable. In real poker, the same principle applies: checking reveals weakness, which the in-position player can exploit.

Lesson 4: Large Pot, Small Bet Situations

The no-fold game perfectly models situations where the pot is enormous relative to the remaining bet. In these spots, folding is essentially never correct, and the entire strategic question reduces to: should you bet or check? The answer: bet with the top portion of your range.

Real Poker Application

The pot is $500 on the river and both players have only $25 remaining. Neither player should fold — the pot odds of $525 to $25 (21:1) mean you need less than 5% equity to call. This is essentially a no-fold game.

In this spot, bet your $25 when you believe your hand is above the median of your opponent's range. Check otherwise. Do not bet "because they'll call anyway" with a below-median hand — that costs you EV.

Key Concepts

  • The [0,1] game uses continuously distributed hand values on [0,1], simplifying analysis while preserving poker's essential structure.
  • In the no-fold variant, players cannot fold — they can only bet or check/call. This models large-pot, small-bet situations.
  • Even when the opponent always calls, selective betting is optimal. Below-median hands lose EV by betting.
  • The EV gain from betting is ΔEV = (2x − 1) × b, which is positive only when x > 0.5.
  • The betting threshold of x = 0.5 is independent of bet size in the no-fold game — only the magnitude of the gain scales with b.
  • The optimal betting strategy has value b/4 compared to always checking, quantifying the value of selective aggression.
  • The full two-player solution requires difference equations, but the qualitative lesson is clear: bet strong hands, check weak ones, even if the opponent never folds.
