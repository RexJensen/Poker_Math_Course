The Road to Poker: Static Multi-Street Games

Extending toy games across multiple betting rounds — where stack depth, SPR, and multi-barrel strategy emerge

From Single Street to Multi-Street

Everything we have analyzed so far — the AKQ game, the [0,1] game with and without folding — has been a single-street model. One player bets (or checks), the other responds, and the hand is over. This simplification isolated fundamental concepts like value betting, bluffing, and the indifference principle.

But real poker has multiple betting rounds: preflop, flop, turn, and river. Each additional street adds exponential complexity to the game tree. A strategy that works perfectly on one street might be suboptimal when considered in the context of future streets. This chapter begins the critical transition from single-street models to multi-street thinking.

The key insight: decisions on early streets must account for what happens on later streets. A flop bet is not just about the flop — it sets up the turn and river. This forward-looking perspective is what separates strong players from weak ones.

Static Multi-Street Games

Static Multi-Street Game

A game where hand values are fixed — they do not change between streets. There are no draws, no community cards that improve or worsen a hand. The only thing that changes across streets is the betting: players have multiple opportunities to bet, raise, call, or fold.

Key Simplification Static game: hand values fixed at deal time
Dynamic game: hand values change with new cards (draws, board cards)
This chapter: static games only

Why study static multi-street games first? Because they isolate the effect of multiple betting rounds without the additional complexity of changing hand values. This lets us understand how stack depth, bet sizing across streets, and multi-barrel bluffing work in their purest form. Dynamic games (with draws and changing equity) will come later.

Why Multiple Streets Matter

Multiple betting rounds transform poker strategy in several fundamental ways that single-street models cannot capture.

The Four Dimensions of Multi-Street Play
DimensionSingle-StreetMulti-Street
Value Extraction One chance to bet for value Multiple bets = more money from strong hands
Bluffing Bluff once, opponent calls or folds Multi-barrel bluffs — increasing pressure across streets
Stack Depth Irrelevant (only one bet) Critical — how much can be wagered over all streets
Planning Decide bet or check, done Must plan entire sequences: bet-bet-bet, bet-check-bet, etc.

The most important new variable that emerges is stack depth. In a single-street game, you bet b and that is it. In a multi-street game, the total amount you can wager is limited by your stack. How you distribute bets across streets — and how your stack size relates to the pot — becomes a central strategic consideration.

SPR: Stack-to-Pot Ratio

Stack-to-Pot Ratio (SPR)

The SPR is the ratio of the effective stack size to the current pot size. It is the single most important number for multi-street decision-making.

Equation 19.1 — SPR SPR = Effective Stack / Pot Size

The effective stack is the smaller of the two players' stacks (since you can never bet more than your opponent can call).

SPR determines the commitment threshold — how strong your hand needs to be to justify putting all your chips in the pot across the remaining streets.

SPR Guidelines
SPR RangeRequired Hand StrengthTypical SituationStrategic Implication
Low (< 4) Top pair / overpair Short stacks, 3-bet pots, tournaments Can commit with one-pair hands. Many hands become "all-in or fold." Less room for maneuvering.
Medium (4–13) Strong top pair / two pair Standard single-raised pots, 100bb deep Need stronger hands to stack off. Room for multi-street play, bluffing, and position exploitation.
High (> 13) Two pair+ / sets / straights Deep stacks, limped pots One-pair hands are rarely strong enough to commit. Premium hands needed. Implied odds become huge.
SPR in Practice

Scenario 1: You open to $15, opponent 3-bets to $50, you call. Pot = $100, effective stacks = $250 remaining.

Calculation SPR = $250 / $100 = 2.5 (Low SPR)

With SPR = 2.5, you can commit with overpairs and top pair top kicker. A single pot-sized bet on the flop essentially commits you to the hand.

Scenario 2: You open to $10, opponent calls. Pot = $20, effective stacks = $490 remaining.

Calculation SPR = $490 / $20 = 24.5 (High SPR)

With SPR = 24.5, top pair is far too weak to stack off. You need two pair, sets, or better to commit all your chips. This is why set-mining and playing speculative hands becomes profitable at deep stacks — the implied odds justify the investment.

Multi-Street Bluffing

In a single-street game, a bluff either works or it does not. In a multi-street game, bluffing becomes far more nuanced. You must decide: if I bluff the flop, do I continue on the turn? And the river?

Multi-Barrel Bluffing

A multi-barrel bluff is a bluff that continues across multiple streets. A "double barrel" is a bluff on the flop followed by a bluff on the turn. A "triple barrel" continues through all three postflop streets.

The Escalating Cost of Multi-Barrel Bluffs

Consider a pot of $100 with a 75%-pot bet sizing on each street:

StreetPot Before BetBet Size (75%)Cumulative RiskPot if Called
Flop $100 $75 $75 $250
Turn $250 $187.50 $262.50 $625
River $625 $468.75 $731.25 $1,562.50

The total risk of a triple-barrel bluff is $731.25 to win the original $100 pot. This enormous cost is why most bluffs should not continue on all three streets. However, those that do continue are extremely credible precisely because they are so expensive — most players assume nobody would risk that much without a strong hand.

Optimal Multi-Street Bluffing Strategy

The game-theoretically optimal approach to multi-street bluffing involves a key principle:

Bluff Attrition

At equilibrium, some bluffs from earlier streets should be abandoned on later streets, while others continue. The fraction that continues is determined by the same indifference principle we saw in the [0,1] game, applied street by street.

Equation 19.2 — Multi-Street Bluff Attrition On each street, maintain the ratio:
Bluffs / Total Bets = b / (b + Current Pot)

As the pot grows, the allowed bluff ratio shrinks.
Some flop bluffs must be given up on the turn.

This creates a natural "funnel" effect: you start with more bluffs on the flop, discard some on the turn, and arrive at the river with a smaller but precisely calibrated number of bluffs relative to your value bets.

Geometric Bet Sizing

If your goal is to get all the money in by the river, how should you size your bets? Betting too large on early streets leaves you with an awkward stack on later streets. Betting too small means you cannot get it all in. The solution is geometric bet sizing.

Geometric Bet Sizing

Geometric bet sizing means choosing a consistent fraction of the pot to bet on each street such that the cumulative bets exactly exhaust the effective stack by the final street. Each bet is the same percentage of the current pot.

Equation 19.3 — Geometric Sizing Formula Given: Pot = P, Effective Stack = S, Streets remaining = n

Let r = bet as fraction of pot on each street.
After n streets of betting r × pot (and getting called):
P × (1 + 2r)n = P + 2S

Solving for r:
r = [(P + 2S)/P]1/n − 1) / 2
Geometric Sizing: Worked Examples

Example 1: Pot = $100, Stack = $400, Streets = 3 (flop, turn, river)

Calculation (P + 2S) / P = (100 + 800) / 100 = 9
91/3 = 2.08
r = (2.08 − 1) / 2 = 0.54 = 54% pot

Bet 54% of the pot on each street. This gets all $400 in by the river.

  • Flop: Pot $100, bet $54. Called → Pot $208
  • Turn: Pot $208, bet $112. Called → Pot $432
  • River: Pot $432, bet $234. All-in (total bet across streets: $54 + $112 + $234 = $400)

Example 2: Pot = $100, Stack = $150, Streets = 2 (turn and river only)

Calculation (100 + 300) / 100 = 4
41/2 = 2.0
r = (2.0 − 1) / 2 = 0.50 = 50% pot

Bet 50% of the pot on each remaining street to get the money in by the river.

Geometric sizing has an elegant property: it keeps the SPR constant across streets. If the SPR is 4 on the flop and you bet geometrically, the SPR going into the turn and river is the same. This means your hand-strength commitment threshold does not change from street to street — the same hands that were worth betting on the flop remain worth betting on the turn.

Lessons for Real Poker

Multi-street thinking fundamentally changes how you approach every hand. Here are the key lessons:

Single-Street vs. Multi-Street Mindset
Single-Street ThinkingMulti-Street Thinking
"Should I bet or check?" "What is my plan for all remaining streets?"
"I'll bet $75 into $100" "How does this bet size set up my turn and river actions?"
"I'll bluff here" "Am I prepared to continue this bluff on the turn if called? What about the river?"
"My hand is strong" "My hand is strong relative to the SPR — is it strong enough to commit my stack?"
"I have top pair" "I have top pair with SPR 3 — this is a stacking hand. With SPR 15 — this is a one-bet hand."

The transition from single-street to multi-street analysis is the bridge between toy games and real poker. Every concept from Chapters 15-18 — value betting, bluffing, the three-region strategy, equity denial — still applies, but now it must be evaluated across the full trajectory of the hand.

Why is geometric bet sizing considered optimal for getting all the money in across multiple streets?

Key Concepts

  • Multiple streets add exponential complexity. Each additional betting round multiplies the game tree. Decisions on early streets must account for the consequences on later streets.
  • Static multi-street games isolate the effect of multiple betting rounds without changing hand values. They reveal how stack depth and bet sizing across streets shape strategy.
  • SPR (Stack-to-Pot Ratio) is the most important metric for multi-street play. Low SPR (< 4) = commit with top pair. Medium SPR (4-13) = need strong hands. High SPR (> 13) = need premium holdings.
  • Multi-barrel bluffing is expensive and must be used selectively. Some bluffs should be abandoned on later streets; the bluff-to-value ratio narrows as the pot grows.
  • Geometric bet sizing distributes bets evenly across streets to exactly exhaust the stack by the river. It keeps SPR constant, maintaining consistent commitment thresholds.
  • Plan ahead. Every bet should be part of a multi-street plan. Ask: "What is my plan if called? If raised? On the next street?" Single-street thinking is a hallmark of weak play.
  • Stack depth determines hand value. The same hand (e.g., top pair) can be a monster at low SPR or a marginal holding at high SPR. Always evaluate hand strength relative to SPR.
