The Road to Poker: Static Multi-Street Games

Extending the [0,1] game to multiple betting rounds — SPR, geometric sizing, and multi-street bluffing

📄 Download Chapter PDF

From One Street to Many

In previous chapters, we analyzed games with a single betting round. Real poker, however, has multiple streets — preflop, flop, turn, and river in Hold'em. Each street offers another opportunity to bet, raise, or fold, creating a vastly more complex strategic landscape.

This chapter studies static multi-street games: games where hand values do not change between streets. This "static" assumption strips away the complexity of draws and improving hands, letting us isolate the pure effect of having multiple opportunities to bet. The insights are powerful and directly applicable to real poker.

The key questions we address:

  • How does the stack-to-pot ratio (SPR) shape strategy?
  • What is the optimal bet size across multiple streets?
  • How do bluffs work when there are multiple betting rounds?

Stack-to-Pot Ratio (SPR)

SPR — Stack-to-Pot Ratio

The SPR is the ratio of the effective stack size to the current pot size. It is the single most important number for strategic decision-making at the start of any post-flop situation.

Definition SPR = Effective Stack / Pot Size

The effective stack is the smaller of the two players' stacks (the maximum amount that can be wagered).

SPR Ranges and Their Meaning
SPRDescriptionStrategic Implication
< 3 Very low Commit with top pair or better. All-in is viable. Bluffing is expensive relative to pot. Focus on hand strength.
3 – 7 Low One pair hands can commit. Two streets of betting typically gets you all-in. Position less important.
7 – 15 Medium Need two pair+ to comfortably stack off. Multi-street planning crucial. Draws have good implied odds.
> 15 High Very deep play. Position extremely valuable. Sets, straights, flushes needed to stack off. Complex multi-street games.

Why SPR Matters

SPR determines the "depth" of the game — how many decisions remain and how significant each one is. At low SPR, the game collapses toward a single decision (go or no-go). At high SPR, there are many decision points, each adding complexity and rewarding skill.

Key Insight Low SPR → Simpler decisions, hand strength dominates
High SPR → Complex decisions, skill (position, reads) dominates

Multi-Street Bluffing

When there are multiple streets, bluffing becomes more nuanced. A bluff does not need to succeed on a single street — it can be deployed as a multi-street plan.

Multi-Street Bluff (Barrel)

A multi-street bluff (or "barreling") involves betting on consecutive streets with a hand that has no showdown value. Each bet represents another "barrel" of the bluff. The threat of continued aggression puts maximum pressure on the opponent.

Multi-Street Bluff Probability P(opponent folds over N streets) = 1 − P(calls street 1) × P(calls street 2) × ... × P(calls street N)
The Compounding Power of Barreling

Suppose your opponent calls one bet about 60% of the time. On a single street, your bluff folds out 40%.

But over three streets of betting:

Three Barrel Success Rate P(opponent folds at least once) = 1 − 0.6³ = 1 − 0.216 = 78.4%

Your bluff succeeds nearly 80% of the time! This is the power of multi-street aggression. Each additional barrel compounds the fold equity. Of course, each barrel also risks more chips, so the sizing and selection of bluffing hands must be calibrated carefully.

When to Continue Barreling

Not every bluff should be continued on the next street. The decision depends on:

  • Remaining streets: More streets remaining means more future fold equity.
  • Board texture: Scare cards that arrive can strengthen your bluffing story.
  • Opponent tendencies: Calling stations should be barreled less; fit-or-fold players more.
  • Commitment threshold: As the pot grows relative to remaining stacks, opponents become pot-committed.

Geometric Bet Sizing

One of the most elegant results in multi-street poker theory: to get all-in over N streets, there is an optimal geometric bet size that creates maximum pressure while maintaining balanced bluff ratios on each street.

Geometric Sizing

Geometric bet sizing means betting the same fraction of the pot on each street, chosen so that the cumulative bets exactly equal the remaining stack by the final street.

Equation 19.1 — Geometric Bet Fraction Let r = bet / pot (fraction of pot bet each street)
After N streets of betting fraction r:
Final pot = Initial pot × (1 + 2r)N

To get all-in: Stack + Pot = Pot × (1 + 2r)N
⇒ (1 + 2r)N = (Stack + Pot) / Pot = SPR + 1
⇒ r = [(SPR + 1)1/N − 1] / 2
Geometric Sizing in Practice

Pot = $100, Stack = $400 (SPR = 4). You want to get all-in over 3 streets.

Calculation (1 + 2r)³ = 5
1 + 2r = 51/3 ≈ 1.71
2r ≈ 0.71
r ≈ 0.355 (bet ~35.5% of pot each street)

Street 1: Pot = $100. Bet $35.50. Pot becomes $171.

Street 2: Pot = $171. Bet $60.70. Pot becomes $292.40.

Street 3: Pot = $292.40. Bet $103.80 (remaining stack). All-in.

Total wagered across 3 streets: $200 (half the original stack per player), for a final pot of $500.

Why Geometric Sizing Is Optimal

Geometric sizing has several desirable properties:

  • Consistent pressure: Each bet is the same fraction of the pot, keeping the pot odds the same on every street.
  • Balanced bluff ratios: Constant pot odds mean the optimal bluff-to-value ratio is the same on every street.
  • Efficient stacking: You get all-in by the final street without having to overbet or underbet on any particular street.
Common Geometric Sizes SPR = 3, N = 2 streets: r ≈ 50% pot
SPR = 4, N = 3 streets: r ≈ 35.5% pot
SPR = 8, N = 3 streets: r ≈ 55% pot
SPR = 13, N = 3 streets: r ≈ 72% pot
SPR = 20, N = 3 streets: r ≈ 88% pot

Equilibrium in Multi-Street Static Games

In a multi-street static game, the equilibrium structure extends the single-street [0,1] game results. Each street has its own three-region strategy, and the regions are interconnected across streets.

Backward Induction

Multi-street games are solved by backward induction: start from the last street (where the game reduces to a single-street problem), solve it, then work backwards to earlier streets. Each earlier-street decision accounts for the future game tree.

Two-Street Static Game

In a two-street [0,1] game with geometric sizing:

  • Street 2 (river): Standard three-region strategy. Value bet top, check middle, bluff bottom.
  • Street 1 (turn): Similar structure, but now "value betting" means planning to continue on street 2. Some street-1 bets are semi-bluffs that become value bets or pure bluffs on street 2.

The overall bluff frequency across both streets combined is what matters for equilibrium.

Practical Implications for Real Poker

Plan Ahead, Not Street by Street

The biggest lesson: do not make isolated street-by-street decisions. Before you bet the flop, have a plan for the turn and river.

Planning a Three-Barrel Bluff

You hold 7♠6♠ and the board is A♦9♣2♥. You decide to c-bet as a bluff. Before betting, plan:

  • Turn plan: Barrel on spade turns (picking up a flush draw), on 8 or 5 (picking up a straight draw), and on Kings or other scare cards. Check-fold on blanks.
  • River plan: If you picked up equity, evaluate. If you hit, bet for value. If missed, consider a river bluff if the runout looks scary.

SPR Drives Strategy

Before any post-flop action, check the SPR. It tells you:

  • How many streets of betting it takes to get all-in
  • How strong your hand needs to be to commit
  • Whether you have room to maneuver (high SPR) or are in a go/no-go situation (low SPR)

Key Concepts

  • SPR = Effective Stack / Pot. The most important number for post-flop planning. Low SPR = simple, commit-or-fold. High SPR = complex, multi-street maneuvering.
  • Geometric bet sizing: r = [(SPR+1)1/N − 1] / 2. Bet this fraction of the pot each street to get all-in by the final street with consistent pot odds.
  • Multi-street bluffing compounds fold equity. A 40% per-street fold rate yields ~78% cumulative fold equity over three streets.
  • Backward induction solves multi-street games: solve the last street first, then work backward.
  • Plan ahead. Before betting any street, know your plan for future streets. Which cards will you barrel? When will you give up?
  • Static models isolate multi-street effects. Holding hand values constant reveals that multiple betting rounds alone create enormous strategic complexity.
