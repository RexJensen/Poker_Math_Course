Beyond Expected Value

Expected value tells us our long-run average result. But poker isn't played in the long run — it's played one hand, one session, one month at a time. Variance measures how far your actual results may stray from expectation.

Chen and Ankenman note that poker players tend to view variance as a one-sided phenomenon, only noticing downswings while assuming upswings represent their "true" win rate. This perception bias leads to overestimating one's actual edge. Understanding variance mathematically corrects this bias.

Variance: Measuring Dispersion

Variance (VP)

For a probability distribution P where each of the n outcomes has a value xi and probability pi:

Equation 2.1 VP = Σ pi × (xi − <P>)²

Variance is always positive (since each term is squared). It measures the average squared distance from the mean.

Comparing Two Games

Both games below have EV = 0, but very different variances:

Coin Flip: Win or lose 1 unit

VC = (1/2)(1 − 0)² + (1/2)(-1 − 0)² = (1/2)(1) + (1/2)(1) = 1

Die Roll: Win 5 on a six, lose 1 otherwise

VD = (5/6)(-1 − 0)² + (1/6)(5 − 0)² = (5/6)(1) + (1/6)(25) = 5

Five times the variance of the coin flip! The die roll game has much more "swinginess" even though both games are fair.

Standard Deviation

Standard Deviation (σ)

The square root of variance: σ = √V

Standard deviation is in the same units as the outcomes (dollars, big bets) making it easier to compare directly to expected value.

In poker, a loose-wild game has much higher variance than a tight-passive one. Your style also affects variance — thin value bets and semi-bluff raises are higher-variance plays that may increase EV, both, or neither.

The Square Root Law

Variance is additive across trials. This gives rise to a crucial relationship:

Equation 2.4 — Standard Deviation Scales with √N σN = σ × √N

If one coin flip has σ = 0.5, then 100 flips have σ100 = 0.5 × √100 = 5 (not 50!).

This square root relationship is profound: as you play more hands, the standard deviation grows, but much more slowly than the expected value. Your EV grows linearly (proportional to N), while your standard deviation grows only as √N. This is why a positive edge eventually dominates — the law of large numbers.

The Normal Distribution & Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the most powerful results in statistics: regardless of the underlying distribution, the distribution of sample sums approaches a normal (bell) distribution as sample size increases.

Equation 2.5 — Normal Distribution N(x, μ, σ) = (1/(σ√2π)) × exp(-(x-μ)² / (2σ²))

Z-Scores

Equation 2.6 z = (x − μ) / σ

A z-score tells us how many standard deviations a result is from the mean. We can use the cumulative normal distribution Φ(z) to find the probability of being above or below any given point.

Worked Examples from the Book

Die Roll Game (D2)

Lose 1 unit on rolls 1-5, win 6 units on a 6.

EV per trial: (5/6)(-1) + (1/6)(6) = 1/6 units

Variance: (1/6)(6 − 1/6)² + (5/6)(-1 − 1/6)² ≈ 6.806

σ = √6.806 ≈ 2.61

After 200 rolls: μ200 = 33.33, σ200 = 36.89

The Winning Session Problem

A player wins 0.015 BB/hand with σ = 2 BB/hand, playing 300-hand sessions.

μ300 = 0.015 × 300 = 4.5 BB

σ300 = 2 × √300 = 34.6 BB

z0 = (0 − 4.5) / 34.6 = -0.1299

Φ(-0.1299) = 44.83%

Winning sessions: 55.17% — even a solid winner has losing sessions nearly 45% of the time!

The AK vs AQ Claim

A player claimed that in 2,000 observed AK vs AQ all-in confrontations online, they ran 50-50. AK should win ~73.5%.

μ2000 = 2000 × 0.735 = 1470 wins for AK

σ2000 = 0.4413 × √2000 = 19.74

z1000 = (1000 − 1470) / 19.74 = -23.8

This is 23.8 standard deviations from the mean — a staggeringly impossible result if the cards were fair. The player was almost certainly exaggerating, lying, or affected by perception bias.

Key Concepts

  • Variance, the weighted sum of squared distances from the mean, measures dispersion from expectation.
  • Standard deviation (σ = √V) puts variance in the same units as outcomes, making comparison easier.
  • Variance is additive across independent trials, giving rise to the σN = σ√N relationship.
  • The Central Limit Theorem tells us sample distributions approach normality as sample size grows, enabling z-score analysis.
  • The law of large numbers means relative closeness to the mean increases with sample size, even as absolute spread grows.
  • Even winning poker players have losing sessions frequently — understanding variance prevents perception bias and tilt.
