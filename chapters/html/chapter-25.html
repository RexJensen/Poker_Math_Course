Poker Finance: Portfolio Theory and Backing Agreements

Applying modern finance theory to poker investments, staking, and risk sharing

📄 Download Chapter PDF

Poker as Investment

In the previous chapters, we analyzed a single player's bankroll management using risk of ruin and the Kelly Criterion. Now we expand the frame: what happens when we treat poker not as a game, but as an investment asset class?

This perspective opens the door to powerful tools from modern finance theory. A poker player's hourly earn rate is analogous to an investment's expected return. Their variance is analogous to investment risk. And just as investors diversify across assets, poker players can diversify through staking, backing, and swapping arrangements.

The mathematics is remarkably similar, and the insights are profound: risk can be shared, reduced, and optimized through financial agreements between players.

Portfolio Theory (Markowitz)

Harry Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), which won the Nobel Prize in Economics, provides the foundation for understanding how diversification reduces risk. The core ideas translate directly to poker.

The Power of Diversification

The central insight of MPT is that combining multiple risky assets can produce a portfolio with less risk than any individual asset, provided the assets are not perfectly correlated.

Portfolio Variance

For a portfolio of two assets with weights w1 and w2, returns with variances σ1² and σ2², and correlation ρ:

Two-Asset Portfolio Variance σp² = w1²σ1² + w2²σ2² + 2w1w2ρσ1σ2

When ρ < 1 (less than perfect correlation), the portfolio variance is less than the weighted average of individual variances. When ρ = 0 (uncorrelated), the reduction is maximized for a given set of weights.

The Efficient Frontier

For any set of available investments, there exists an efficient frontier — the set of portfolios that offer the maximum expected return for each level of risk. Any portfolio below the efficient frontier is suboptimal: you could get more return for the same risk, or less risk for the same return.

Poker Portfolio: Two Uncorrelated Players

Consider two players who each have μ = 4 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100. If a backer invests equally in both (buying 50% of each player's action):

Portfolio μ = 0.5 × 4 + 0.5 × 4 = 4 BB/100 (unchanged)

If ρ = 0 (different games, uncorrelated):
σp² = 0.5² × 80² + 0.5² × 80² = 0.25 × 6400 + 0.25 × 6400 = 3200
σp = √3200 = 56.6 BB/100

The expected return stays the same at 4 BB/100, but the standard deviation drops from 80 to 56.6 BB/100 — a 29% reduction in risk with no sacrifice in expected return. This is the magic of diversification.

With more uncorrelated players, the risk reduction compounds. Backing n identical, uncorrelated players reduces portfolio standard deviation by a factor of √n.

Staking and Backing Agreements

A backing arrangement (or "staking deal") is one of the most common financial arrangements in poker. Understanding the mathematics helps both parties evaluate whether a deal is fair and beneficial.

The Basic Structure

Standard Backing Agreement

In a typical backing deal:

  • The backer provides the entire bankroll and absorbs all losses
  • The player provides the skill and time at the table
  • Profits are split according to an agreed ratio (commonly 50/50)
  • The player must clear any makeup (accumulated losses) before profit splitting resumes

This arrangement is essentially an equity investment: the backer is buying a share of the player's future earnings in exchange for providing capital and absorbing downside risk.

The Backer's Perspective

For the backer, each player in their stable is an investment asset. The backer's goal is to construct a portfolio of players that maximizes their risk-adjusted return.

Optimal Player Portfolio

The backer should evaluate each potential player using the Kelly ratio framework from Chapter 24:

Player Quality Metric (from Backer's Perspective) Player Kelly Ratio = (split × μ) / σ²

Higher Kelly ratios mean better risk-adjusted returns. The backer should prioritize backing players with the highest Kelly ratios, not necessarily the highest raw win rates.

Correlation Matters

A critical factor the backer must consider is correlation between players' results:

  • Same table: Highly correlated (when one wins a big pot, another may lose it). This provides minimal diversification benefit.
  • Same game type, different tables: Moderately correlated (similar game dynamics, but outcomes are mostly independent).
  • Different game types: Low correlation (cash vs. tournaments, NLH vs. PLO). Maximum diversification benefit.
  • Different sites/locations: Very low correlation. Ideal for portfolio construction.
Backer's Portfolio: Correlated vs. Uncorrelated

A backer stakes two players at 50/50 split, each with μ = 6 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100.

ScenarioCorrelation (ρ)Portfolio σRisk Reduction
Same table0.875.9 BB/1005%
Same game, different tables0.364.5 BB/10019%
Different game types0.056.6 BB/10029%

The backer's expected return is the same in all cases (3 BB/100 after the 50% split), but the risk varies dramatically depending on how correlated the players' results are.

The Player's Perspective

Why would a winning player voluntarily give up 50% of their profits? The answer lies in the Kelly Criterion and the concept of utility.

Risk of Ruin: Eliminated

The most immediate benefit of being backed is that your personal risk of ruin drops to zero. The backer absorbs all losses. Even in the worst downswing of your career, you cannot go personally broke (assuming you are not investing your own money alongside).

Access to Higher Stakes

This is the primary financial argument for being backed. Consider this analysis:

Self-Funded vs. Backed: A Comparison

A player has $10,000 in personal bankroll. They can beat $1/$2 (200BB buy-in) for 5 BB/100, and they estimate they could beat $5/$10 (200BB buy-in) for 4 BB/100.

ScenarioEffective Earn RateAnnual Income (30 hrs/wk)
Self-funded at $1/$2 5 BB/100 × $2 = $10/100 hands ~$23,400
Backed at $5/$10 (50/50 split) 4 BB/100 × $10 × 50% = $20/100 hands ~$46,800

Despite giving up 50% of profits, the backed player earns twice as much because they can play at stakes 5x higher. The backer provides the bankroll that makes this possible.

When Being Backed Makes Sense

Being backed is rational when:

  • You are underbankrolled for the games where your edge is largest
  • The certainty equivalent of playing self-funded is lower than your backed earn rate
  • You value the elimination of ruin risk highly (risk-averse players)
  • You are trying to build a bankroll for eventual self-funding at higher stakes

When Being Backed Does NOT Make Sense

  • You are already well-bankrolled for the games you want to play
  • The split is unfavorable (e.g., 60/40 in backer's favor at low stakes)
  • You are a high-volume, proven winner with enough data to be confident in your edge

Swapping Percentages

Another common arrangement is percentage swapping, where two players exchange portions of their action. For example, Player A sells 10% of their action to Player B, and Player B sells 10% to Player A.

Percentage Swap

When Player A and Player B swap s% of each other's action:

Post-Swap Returns Player A's result = (1−s) × A_result + s × B_result
Player B's result = s × A_result + (1−s) × B_result

Each player's expected value changes (up or down) depending on the relative win rates, but the variance of each player's results decreases as long as ρ < 1.

When Swaps Are Beneficial

Swaps are most beneficial when:

  • Players have similar edges: If win rates are comparable, neither player sacrifices much EV.
  • Results are uncorrelated: Different game types or locations maximize the diversification benefit.
  • Both players are variance-sensitive: Players on shorter bankrolls benefit more from variance reduction.
The Math of a 10% Swap

Two players, each with μ = 5 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100, swap 10% of their action. Their results are uncorrelated (ρ = 0).

Each player's new expected value = 0.9 × 5 + 0.1 × 5 = 5 BB/100 (unchanged)

Each player's new variance:
σnew² = 0.9² × 80² + 0.1² × 80² = 0.81 × 6400 + 0.01 × 6400
= 5184 + 64 = 5248
σnew = √5248 = 72.4 BB/100

A 10% swap with an equal player reduces standard deviation from 80 to 72.4 BB/100 — a 9.5% reduction in risk with zero sacrifice in expected value.

Beware of Unequal Swaps

If you swap with a player whose win rate is significantly lower than yours, you are effectively subsidizing their variance reduction at the cost of your own expected value. A swap is only fair when the players have reasonably similar edges, or when the better player receives compensation (a smaller swap percentage or cash payment).

Makeup and Obligations

One of the most important (and often misunderstood) aspects of backing agreements is the concept of makeup.

Makeup

Makeup is the accumulated deficit that a backed player owes before profit splitting resumes. If a player loses $5,000 of the backer's money, they are "$5,000 in makeup." They must win back that $5,000 before any subsequent profits are split.

The Makeup Trap

Makeup creates a significant psychological and mathematical burden on the player:

  • Effective earn rate drops: While in makeup, the player earns $0/hour since all profits go to clearing the deficit. This can persist for hundreds of hours.
  • Tilt risk increases: The psychological weight of a large makeup balance can affect play quality.
  • Quitting incentive: A player deep in makeup may rationally prefer to walk away from the deal, since the expected time to clear makeup may be very long.

Makeup from a Financial Perspective

Mathematically, makeup is equivalent to a call option on the player's future results. The backer has given the player the right to play with the backer's money. The player's position is like a long call: unlimited upside (after clearing makeup), zero downside (the backer absorbs losses).

The backer's position is like selling a call: they capture losses, but their profit is limited to their split percentage. This asymmetry explains why backers demand a significant share of profits.

The Expected Duration of Makeup

A player is $10,000 in makeup. Their earn rate at the current stake is $20/hr. The expected time to clear makeup is:

Expected hours = $10,000 / $20/hr = 500 hours

At 30 hours per week, that's roughly 4 months of working for free before the player sees any income. However, due to variance, the actual time could be significantly shorter or longer. There's also a meaningful probability the makeup grows larger before it is cleared.

Key Concepts

  • Poker earnings can be analyzed using Modern Portfolio Theory — treating each player or game as an investment asset with expected return and variance.
  • Diversification reduces risk without sacrificing expected return, provided the assets (players) are not perfectly correlated.
  • A backing arrangement is an equity investment: the backer provides capital and absorbs risk; the player provides skill; profits are split.
  • The backer should construct a portfolio of players with high Kelly ratios and low correlation to maximize risk-adjusted returns.
  • Being backed is rational when the player is underbankrolled and the backed earn rate (after split) exceeds the self-funded earn rate.
  • Percentage swaps between players with similar edges reduce individual variance through mutual diversification.
  • Makeup is the accumulated deficit in a backing deal; it functions like a call option and creates significant financial and psychological pressure on the player.
  • The efficient frontier represents the optimal trade-off between risk and return across all possible portfolio allocations.
