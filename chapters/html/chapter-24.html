Growing Bankrolls: The Kelly Criterion and Rational Game Selection

How to maximize long-run bankroll growth by choosing the right games and stakes

The Kelly Criterion

The Kelly Criterion, developed by John L. Kelly Jr. at Bell Labs in 1956, is a money management strategy that answers a deceptively simple question: given a favorable bet, how much should you wager?

The answer is the fraction of your bankroll that maximizes the long-run growth rate of your wealth. Bet too little, and you leave growth on the table. Bet too much, and you actually decrease your long-run growth rate — or even go broke.

The Kelly Fraction

For a simple bet with probability p of winning and odds of b to 1:

Classic Kelly Formula f* = (bp − q) / b = (bp − (1−p)) / b

where f* is the optimal fraction of bankroll to wager, p is the probability of winning, q = 1−p is the probability of losing, and b is the net odds received.

Kelly for Poker (Simplified)

In poker, the situation is more complex because we don't face discrete win/lose bets. Instead, we have a continuous distribution of outcomes characterized by our win rate (μ) and standard deviation (σ). The simplified Kelly criterion for poker becomes:

Poker Kelly Formula
Optimal Stake Level f* = μ / σ²

This tells us the fraction of our bankroll that should constitute one "unit" at the table. Equivalently, the optimal number of buy-ins for our bankroll is 1/f* = σ²/μ.

Kelly Bankroll for a Typical Player

A $1/$2 NL Hold'em player with μ = 5 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100:

f* = 5 / (80²) = 5 / 6400 = 0.000781

Required bankroll = 1/f* = 6400/5 = 1,280 BB = 12.8 buy-ins

This means the Kelly-optimal strategy is to play with roughly 13 buy-ins. However, this is the full Kelly recommendation, which most practitioners consider too aggressive in practice.

Log Utility: The Foundation of Kelly

The Kelly Criterion is mathematically equivalent to maximizing the expected logarithm of your bankroll. This is not an arbitrary choice — it has several remarkable properties:

Why Logarithmic Utility?

  • Diminishing marginal value: The log function captures the intuition that the 100th dollar matters less than the 1st dollar. Doubling your bankroll from $1,000 to $2,000 feels as significant as doubling from $10,000 to $20,000.
  • Ruin avoidance: Because log(0) = −∞, the Kelly bettor will never risk their entire bankroll on a single bet (theoretically), preventing total ruin.
  • Long-run growth maximization: Kelly maximizes the geometric growth rate. Over time, the Kelly bettor will almost surely end up with more money than any other strategy.
  • Fraction invariance: The optimal bet is always a fraction of current bankroll, which naturally scales with your wealth.
Growth Rate Under Kelly

The expected log growth rate per period under Kelly betting is:

Kelly Growth Rate G* = μ² / (2σ²)

This is the maximum achievable growth rate. Any other betting fraction yields a lower growth rate.

Growth Rate Comparison

For a player with μ = 5 BB/100 and σ = 80 BB/100:

G* = 5² / (2 × 80²) = 25 / 12,800 = 0.00195 per 100 hands

This means the bankroll grows by approximately 0.195% per 100 hands at the Kelly-optimal level. Over 100,000 hands, the bankroll would grow by a factor of e(0.00195 × 1000) ≈ e1.95 ≈ 7.0 times its initial value.

Fractional Kelly: Trading Growth for Safety

While full Kelly maximizes long-run growth, the ride can be extremely volatile. In practice, most professional players and investors use a fractional Kelly approach — betting some fraction of the Kelly-optimal amount.

Kelly Fraction Growth Rate (% of full Kelly) Variance (% of full Kelly) Practical Notes
Full Kelly (1.0)100%100%Maximum growth, but very volatile
3/4 Kelly (0.75)93.75%75%Slightly conservative; good balance
Half Kelly (0.50)75%50%Most popular among professionals
Quarter Kelly (0.25)43.75%25%Very conservative; minimal risk
The Half-Kelly Trade-off

Half Kelly is particularly popular because of a remarkable trade-off:

Half Kelly Properties Growth rate = 75% of full Kelly
Variance = 50% of full Kelly
Drawdown severity = roughly halved

You sacrifice only 25% of your growth rate but reduce your variance by 50%. This is a very favorable trade-off for risk-averse practitioners.

The relationship between Kelly fraction f (as a proportion of full Kelly) and growth rate G follows a parabolic curve:

Growth Rate as Function of Kelly Fraction G(f) = G* × f × (2 − f)

This formula shows that at f = 1 (full Kelly), G = G*. At f = 2 (double Kelly), G = 0 — you grow not at all! And beyond f = 2, growth becomes negative.

Over-betting Kelly: The Danger Zone

One of the most important insights from Kelly theory is that betting more than the Kelly fraction actually decreases your long-run growth rate. This is counterintuitive: surely more aggressive betting should lead to faster growth?

The Parabola of Growth

Think of growth rate as a hill. Kelly sits at the peak. Moving in either direction — betting too little or too much — reduces growth. But the consequences are asymmetric:

  • Under-betting (f < 1): Growth is reduced, but you are still growing. The player is safe but leaving money on the table.
  • Over-betting (1 < f < 2): Growth is reduced AND variance increases. The player is taking on more risk for less reward. This is strictly dominated.
  • Extreme over-betting (f > 2): Expected growth rate becomes negative. The player is expected to lose money over time despite having a positive edge!

This is why the Kelly Criterion is so important: it identifies the inflection point where more aggressive play starts hurting rather than helping. Many aggressive poker players unwittingly over-bet Kelly by playing at stakes too high for their bankroll and edge, destroying their long-run growth rate even though they have a genuine advantage.

Application to Poker: Rational Game Selection

The Kelly Criterion provides a rigorous framework for one of poker's most important decisions: which game should you play?

The Kelly Ratio

When comparing games, the key metric is not your win rate alone, nor your variance alone, but the Kelly ratio:

Kelly Ratio (Game Quality Metric) Kelly Ratio = μ / σ²

The game with the highest Kelly ratio is the best game for maximizing bankroll growth, regardless of the absolute stakes involved.

Comparing Two Games

Suppose you have access to two games:

MetricGame A ($1/$2)Game B ($2/$5)
Win Rate (μ)3 BB/1005 BB/100
Std Dev (σ)12 BB/10025 BB/100
σ²144625
Kelly Ratio3/144 = 0.02085/625 = 0.008
Optimal Bankroll48 BB125 BB

Despite Game B having a higher win rate in BB/100, Game A has a Kelly ratio 2.6 times higher. The lower variance of Game A more than compensates for its lower edge. Game A is the superior game for bankroll growth.

When Higher Stakes Aren't Better

This analysis reveals an important truth: moving to higher stakes is not always beneficial, even if you have a larger edge in absolute dollar terms. If the higher stakes come with proportionally greater variance (due to tougher opponents, deeper stacks, or higher-variance strategies), the Kelly ratio may actually decrease.

Rational game selection requires estimating both your edge and your variance at each available game, then comparing Kelly ratios.

Certainty Equivalent

The certainty equivalent is the guaranteed amount of money you would accept instead of playing the uncertain game. For a Kelly bettor, this has a clean mathematical form.

Certainty Equivalent

For a Kelly bettor facing a game with win rate μ and variance σ², the certainty equivalent per unit of play is:

Certainty Equivalent CE = μ − σ² / (2B)

where B is the current bankroll. As the bankroll grows, the certainty equivalent approaches the raw expected value μ, because the player can better absorb variance.

The certainty equivalent framework helps explain why a small-bankrolled player might rationally accept a staking deal that sacrifices 50% of their EV — the certainty equivalent of playing high-variance games on a short bankroll can be very low or even negative.

Key Concepts

  • The Kelly Criterion determines the bet size that maximizes long-run bankroll growth rate: f* = μ/σ².
  • Kelly is equivalent to maximizing the expected logarithm of bankroll, which naturally accounts for diminishing marginal value and prevents ruin.
  • Fractional Kelly (especially half Kelly) sacrifices modest growth for substantial variance reduction — a trade-off most practitioners favor.
  • Over-betting Kelly (f > 1) decreases growth rate while increasing variance. Beyond 2x Kelly, expected growth becomes negative.
  • The Kelly ratio (μ/σ²) is the correct metric for comparing games — a lower-variance game can be superior despite a lower win rate.
  • The certainty equivalent quantifies the guaranteed amount a player would accept instead of playing, incorporating both edge and risk.
  • Rational game selection means choosing the game with the highest Kelly ratio, not necessarily the highest edge or highest stakes.
