Part III — Chapter 10

Facing The Nemesis: Game Theory

When exploitive play isn't enough — finding strategies that are fundamentally sound against any opponent

Why Exploitive Play Has Limits

In Part II we developed the tools of exploitive play: observe an opponent's tendencies, model their strategy, and choose the maximally exploitive counter-strategy. Against weak, predictable opponents this approach is devastatingly effective. But real poker presents situations where pure exploitive play breaks down:

  • Strong opponents who exploit back. If you adapt to them, they adapt to you, creating an infinite regress of adjustments.
  • Unknown opponents. You have no reads, no history — what strategy do you default to?
  • Tournament play. You can't choose your table or your opponents. You need a strategy that holds up against the entire field.

Chen and Ankenman argue that we need strategies that are "fundamentally sound" — strategies that perform well regardless of what our opponent does. This is the domain of game theory.

The Nemesis Concept

To evaluate how robust a strategy truly is, imagine the worst possible opponent: a nemesis. This nemesis knows your exact strategy — every frequency, every threshold, every tendency — and plays the maximally exploitive counter-strategy against you.

The Nemesis

A theoretical opponent who knows your complete strategy and plays the best possible response against it. Your expected value against the nemesis represents the worst-case scenario for your strategy.

Here is the key insight: if you choose a strategy that maximizes your EV against the nemesis, you have found the most robust strategy possible. No matter who you actually face, you cannot do worse than your nemesis EV. This is the foundation of game theory optimal (GTO) play.

Nemesis in Action

Suppose you bet the river with a strategy of bluffing 50% of the time when you miss. Your nemesis, knowing this, will call every time (since you bluff too often for folding to be profitable). Your EV against the nemesis is terrible.

Now suppose you never bluff. Your nemesis simply folds every time you bet, denying you any value from your strong hands. Again, poor EV.

The GTO solution lies somewhere in between — a bluffing frequency that makes the nemesis indifferent between calling and folding.

The Minimax Theorem

The mathematical foundation for GTO play comes from John von Neumann's Minimax Theorem (1928), one of the landmark results in mathematics:

Minimax Theorem (von Neumann, 1928)

In any finite, two-player, zero-sum game, there exists a pair of strategies (one for each player) such that neither player can improve their expected value by unilaterally changing their strategy. Furthermore:

The Minimax Equation maxA minB EV(A, B) = minB maxA EV(A, B)

The left side reads: "Player A maximizes (over their own strategies) the minimum EV (that Player B can impose)." The right side reads: "Player B minimizes (over their own strategies) the maximum EV (that Player A can achieve)." These two quantities are equal, and this common value is called the value of the game.

What this means in poker terms:

  • There exists a strategy that guarantees you at least a certain EV, no matter what your opponent does.
  • Your opponent also has a strategy that limits your EV to that same value.
  • These two strategies meet at the Nash equilibrium.

Interpreting max-min and min-max

Think of it this way: if you move first (commit to a strategy) and your opponent gets to see it and respond optimally, the best you can guarantee is maxA minB EV(A,B). If your opponent commits first and you get to respond, the best they can guarantee (for themselves) is minB maxA EV(A,B). The minimax theorem says these are equal — the order doesn't matter. This is remarkable and is precisely what makes the equilibrium concept so powerful.

Nash Equilibrium Properties

Nash Equilibrium

A strategy profile where neither player has an incentive to unilaterally deviate. Each player's strategy is a best response to the other's.

The Nash equilibrium has several crucial properties for poker players:

PropertyMeaning for Poker
EV Floor Playing the equilibrium strategy guarantees you at least the game value. You cannot do worse than this, regardless of your opponent's strategy.
No incentive to deviate If both players are at equilibrium, neither gains by changing strategy. The game is "solved."
Opponent deviations help you If your opponent deviates from equilibrium while you stay at equilibrium, your EV can only increase (or stay the same). You never do worse.
Safety GTO is the "safe" strategy. It may not maximize against a specific weak opponent, but it never exposes you to exploitation.
The EV Floor in Practice

Consider a symmetric poker game where neither player has a positional advantage. At Nash equilibrium, the game value is 0 (since it's symmetric). This means:

  • Playing GTO guarantees you break even in the long run.
  • If your opponent deviates from GTO, you profit.
  • You can never lose money in expectation by playing GTO.

In asymmetric games (like those with positional advantage), the game value may not be zero, but the same principle holds — GTO sets the floor.

The Indifference Principle

Perhaps the most practically useful concept from game theory is the indifference principle: at equilibrium, the opponent is indifferent between the options they mix over.

Indifference Principle

At a Nash equilibrium, if a player mixes between two or more actions with positive probability, they must be indifferent between those actions — each yields the same expected value. If one action were strictly better, the player would play it 100% of the time, contradicting the mix.

This principle is incredibly powerful because it lets us calculate optimal frequencies. Here is how:

Calculating Optimal Bluff Frequency

Suppose Player A bets the pot (bet = pot) and can either have a value hand or be bluffing. Player B must decide whether to call or fold.

To find A's optimal bluff frequency, we set B to be indifferent between calling and folding:

EV(fold) = 0 (B gives up, losing nothing additional)

EV(call) = P(A is bluffing) × (pot + bet) − P(A has value) × bet

Setting EV(call) = EV(fold) = 0 with a pot-sized bet:

P(bluff) × (2 × pot) − (1 − P(bluff)) × pot = 0

2 × P(bluff) × pot − pot + P(bluff) × pot = 0

3 × P(bluff) × pot = pot

P(bluff) = 1/3

So at equilibrium, Player A should bluff 1 out of every 3 bets (i.e., for every 2 value bets, include 1 bluff). This makes the opponent's call or fold decision completely irrelevant — both yield EV = 0.

The beauty of this approach: we don't need to know what our opponent will do. By making them indifferent, we guarantee that no matter how they respond, our EV is the same. The optimal player makes the opponent's decision irrelevant.

Preview: The [0,1] Game

To study game theory in a tractable way, Chen and Ankenman introduce toy poker games where each player is dealt a hand drawn uniformly at random from the continuous interval [0, 1]. Higher numbers beat lower numbers with certainty.

These "half-street" and "full-street" [0,1] games strip away the complexity of real poker while preserving the essential strategic features: bluffing, calling, raising, and information asymmetry. They allow us to derive closed-form equilibrium solutions and build deep intuition about optimal play.

We will explore these games in detail in the following chapters, but the key takeaway is: game theory doesn't just tell us that we should bluff — it tells us exactly how often and with which hands.

Exploitive vs. GTO: The Trade-off

AspectExploitive PlayGTO Play
Goal Maximize EV against a specific opponent Maximize worst-case EV (against the nemesis)
Strengths Extracts maximum value from weak opponents Unexploitable; safe baseline; no reads required
Weaknesses Vulnerable to counter-exploitation; requires accurate reads Leaves value on the table against predictable opponents
Information needed Detailed opponent model Only the game structure
Risk High (if reads are wrong, you get exploited) Zero (you can't do worse than game value)

The Recommended Approach

Chen and Ankenman advocate a practical synthesis:

  1. Start with GTO-like play as your baseline — this keeps you safe while you gather information.
  2. Identify reliable opponent patterns through observation. Look for consistent, large deviations from equilibrium.
  3. Deviate exploitively when you have high confidence in your reads. The larger and more consistent the opponent's deviation, the more you should deviate in response.
  4. Return to baseline when facing strong, adaptive opponents or when your reads become uncertain.

This approach treats GTO as the "home base" that you depart from strategically and return to when conditions warrant.

Why Study Game Theory for Poker?

Even if you never intend to play a pure GTO strategy, understanding game theory is essential for several reasons:

  • Provides a baseline strategy. GTO gives you a solid default to fall back on in any situation.
  • Reveals correct frequencies. Game theory tells us the "right" bluffing frequency, calling frequency, and bet sizing in any spot. Without this reference point, you're guessing.
  • Shows which exploitive deviations are most valuable. By understanding equilibrium, you can identify which opponent mistakes are most costly and prioritize your exploitation accordingly.
  • Identifies opponent deviations. You can't know your opponent is "calling too much" unless you know what the right calling frequency is. GTO provides that benchmark.

Key Concepts

  • Exploitive play breaks down against strong opponents who counter-exploit, against unknowns, and in tournaments where you can't choose opponents.
  • The nemesis is a theoretical worst-case opponent who knows your exact strategy. Your EV against the nemesis is the floor for your strategy.
  • The minimax theorem guarantees that in two-player zero-sum games, an equilibrium exists where maxA minB EV = minB maxA EV.
  • At Nash equilibrium, neither player can improve by deviating unilaterally. It sets an EV floor — opponents who deviate can only help you.
  • The indifference principle: at equilibrium, the opponent is indifferent between mixed actions. This lets us calculate optimal bluffing and calling frequencies.
  • GTO play is the safe baseline; exploitive play extracts more value but requires accurate reads. The best approach is to start GTO and deviate exploitively when patterns are reliable.
  • Understanding game theory reveals correct frequencies, identifies opponent deviations, and shows which exploitive adjustments are most profitable.
