Playing Accurately, Part I: Cards Exposed Situations

When both hands are face up, the correct play still isn't always obvious

The Two-Step Exploitive Process

In Part I, we built the mathematical foundations: probability, expected value, variance, Bayes’ theorem, and pot odds. Now we apply them to actual poker play. The exploitive approach to poker can be distilled into two steps:

  1. Read hands — Determine what your opponent is likely to hold (Chapter 5).
  2. Calculate EV — For each possible action (fold, call, raise), compute the expected value and select the action with the highest EV.

This chapter examines Step 2 in the simplest possible scenario: what happens when both players’ cards are exposed? By removing the information asymmetry, we can focus purely on the mathematical interactions between pot size, stack size, position, and drawing odds.

Cards Exposed Analysis

Cards Exposed Game

A theoretical construct where both players’ hole cards are turned face up. There is no hidden information — each player knows exactly where they stand. Despite this, non-trivial strategic decisions remain because of pot odds, remaining cards to come, and stack-size constraints.

In a cards-exposed situation, the analysis is straightforward in principle:

  • The player with the made hand (currently ahead) wants to charge the draw as much as possible.
  • The player with the drawing hand wants to see cards as cheaply as possible.
  • The draw should chase if the pot odds are favorable; fold otherwise.

This seems simple, but Chen and Ankenman show that several counter-intuitive situations arise even in this fully transparent setting.

Example 7.1: When the Made Hand Should Not Bet

7-Card Stud — $30-60 Limit

Consider the following scenario on 5th street (three cards dealt, two to come):

  • Player X: A-K-Q-J-T (open-ended straight draw needing a 9 or an ace-high straight card)
  • Player Y: Four spades (flush draw)
  • Pot: $400
  • Y’s remaining stack: $120

Player X currently has the best hand (ace high vs. no made hand). The intuitive play is for X to bet — after all, X is ahead! But the analysis reveals something surprising.

Why Betting Is Wrong for X

If X bets $60 on 5th street, Y has to call $60 into a $460 pot — getting roughly 7.7:1. With approximately 9 flush outs from 37 remaining unknown cards, Y’s probability of hitting on the next card alone is about 24.3%. The pot odds easily justify a call.

But it gets worse for X. Y can actually raise to $120 (all-in). Now X faces a raise, with the pot offering good enough odds that X must call. The net result:

X Bets → Y Raises All-In All $120 of Y’s stack goes into the pot under conditions favorable to Y.
Y gets all remaining money in with correct pot odds.

Contrast this with X checking. If X checks, Y must decide whether to bet with a draw. If Y bets, X can raise. The dynamic shifts. By checking, X avoids creating a situation where Y can profitably commit all remaining chips.

Key Insight

Even when you have the best hand, betting can be incorrect if it allows your opponent to get the rest of their money in under favorable conditions. The interaction between pot size, remaining stack, and drawing odds determines the correct action — not simply who is currently ahead.

The Semi-Bluff in Exposed Cards

One of the most surprising results in exposed-card analysis is that the semi-bluff can be correct even when your opponent can see your hand.

Semi-Bluff (Cards Exposed)

A bet made with a drawing hand (not currently best) that has positive expected value because the pot is large relative to remaining bets. Even though the opponent knows you are drawing, they may not be able to profitably call.

When the Draw Should Bet

Suppose the pot is very large ($500) and both players have $60 left. The draw has a 35% chance of winning. If the draw bets $60:

If called: Draw wins $620 × 35% = $217, loses $60 × 65% = $39
EV(bet, if called) = $217 − $39 = +$178

The made hand must call (getting good odds), but the draw profits from getting money in because the pot is already so large. The draw’s bet has positive EV regardless of whether it’s called.

Free Card Play

Another counter-intuitive exposed-card tactic: the draw raises on one street to get a “free card” on the next. In limit poker, a player who raises often gets checked to on the following street (the opponent fears another raise). The draw can then check behind, effectively seeing an additional card without paying an extra bet.

Free Card Economics Cost of raising on Street N: 1 extra small bet
Savings on Street N+1: 1 big bet (if opponent checks to you)
Net savings: 1 big bet − 1 small bet = positive (in limit games where big bet = 2 × small bet)

The Key Lesson

Even in the simplest possible poker scenario — cards face up, no hidden information, no deception — the correct play is not always obvious. Three factors interact in complex ways:

The Three Interacting Factors
  1. Position: Acting last gives you information about your opponent’s action before you commit chips.
  2. Stack sizes: The ratio of remaining stacks to the pot determines whether draws can profitably commit and whether made hands can effectively charge draws.
  3. Pot size: A large pot relative to stacks changes the dynamics entirely, sometimes making aggressive play correct for draws and passive play correct for made hands.

If the correct play can be non-obvious with cards exposed, imagine how much more complex it becomes when hands are hidden. This motivates the analysis in the next chapter, where we examine how to play accurately against a distribution of possible hands.

Key Concepts

  • The exploitive process has two steps: read hands, then calculate EV for each action and choose the highest.
  • In cards-exposed analysis, we remove hidden information to study the pure mathematical interactions of pot size, stacks, and drawing odds.
  • A made hand should not always bet — when the stack-to-pot ratio is low and the draw has correct odds, betting can be worse than checking.
  • The semi-bluff can have positive EV even with cards exposed when the pot is large relative to remaining bets.
  • The free card play (raising to check behind on the next street) can save the drawing hand money in limit games.
  • Position, stack sizes, and pot size all interact to determine correct play — being ahead is not sufficient justification for betting.
