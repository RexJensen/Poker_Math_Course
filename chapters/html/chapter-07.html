Part II — Chapter 7

Playing Accurately, Part I: Cards Exposed Situations

When the cards are face up, the math still isn't obvious

The Two-Step Exploitive Process

Exploitive play has two steps: (1) read your opponent's hand/strategy (Chapter 5), and (2) calculate the EV of each action and choose the highest. This chapter focuses on step two in the simplest setting — where both players' cards are exposed.

You might think face-up poker is trivial: made hands bet, draws call or fold based on pot odds. But Chen and Ankenman show that some interesting, non-intuitive situations arise even with perfect information.

Example: 7-Card Stud with Exposed Hands

Example 7.1 — Straight Draw vs. Flush Draw

$30-$60 7-card stud. Player X has A♠K♦Q♥J♠T♣ (straight draw). Player Y has A♠T♠8♠5♠2♣ (flush draw).

Pot: $400. Player Y has only $120 left. Player X covers.

Key Insight: X should NOT bet on 5th street despite being ahead! Y has the correct odds to call AND raise. Betting creates a worse situation for X because it allows Y to get all remaining chips in with correct odds.

The Semi-Bluff with Exposed Cards

Even with cards face up, the semi-bluff can be correct when the pot is large relative to remaining bets. The draw can bet because the made hand cannot profitably call in certain stack configurations.

This counterintuitive result demonstrates that poker's complexity goes beyond hidden information — the game tree structure itself (bet sizes, stack depths, pot size) creates strategic richness.

Free Card Play

Sometimes a draw should raise to get a "free card" on the next street. The idea: if you raise the flop, the opponent often checks to you on the turn, allowing you to see the river without putting in another bet.

Free Card Play

Raising with a drawing hand to induce a check on the next street, effectively getting to see an additional card without paying a full bet. Works because the opponent, having been raised, fears betting into a potential made hand.

Position, Stack Size, and Pot Size Interactions

The correct play in exposed-card games depends heavily on three interacting factors:

  • Position: Acting last provides information advantage even with exposed cards
  • Stack sizes: Short stacks limit the game tree, changing optimal decisions
  • Pot size: Large pots relative to remaining bets favor the draw (better odds)

The lesson: even in the simplest possible poker scenario, correct play requires careful analysis of all these factors together.

Key Concepts

  • Even with cards face up, poker decisions can be non-trivial due to game tree structure.
  • The correct play depends on the interaction of position, stack sizes, and pot size.
  • Semi-bluffs can be correct even when the opponent can see your cards, in certain configurations.
  • The free card play — raising with a draw to get a cheaper path to showdown — exploits opponent's predictable check-to-raiser tendency.
  • Exposed-card games teach us about the structural elements of poker strategy independent of hidden information.
