The No-Limit AKQ Game

Complete solution to the full-street no-limit AKQ game

📄 Download Appendix PDF

Overview

Chapter 15 introduced the full-street AKQ game, where both players can bet. This appendix provides the complete derivation of the no-limit solution — a game where bet sizes are unrestricted. The solution turns out to be surprisingly close to the spread-limit game analyzed in Chapter 17.

The AKQ game uses a three-card deck (Ace, King, Queen) with each player dealt one card. Players post antes of size 1 into the pot, and the game proceeds with one full street of betting.

Solution Strategy Outline

The derivation proceeds through a series of increasingly refined strategic steps:

Step 1: Constrain X from Betting Kings

First, solve the game where Player X may not bet with kings. X bets amount r (the “Golden Mean of Poker,” √2 − 1 ≈ 0.4142) with some aces and checks the remainder. Y bets a different, larger amount s0 when checked to. Both players balance their strategies with aces and queens appropriately.

Step 2: X Bets Kings for a Small Amount

Relaxing the constraint, X can improve by betting a smaller amount t with some kings. This is a “blocking bet” that reduces the value Y extracts.

Step 3: Y Responds with Raises

Y raises X’s king-bets to amount s(t) and raise-bluffs with queens at frequency β. This makes X at most indifferent between checking and betting t with kings. The bluffing frequency β is related to s0, the amount Y bets when X checks.

Step 4: X Mixes Aces into Bet-t Range

X responds by also betting t with some aces (in addition to kings), gaining value by either picking off raise-bluffs or getting called by Y’s kings. Y cannot prevent X from gaining value in one of these two ways.

Step 5: X Cannot Bet All Aces

Crucially, in the no-limit case X cannot bet all aces for amount t. If he does, his checking range contains only kings, and Y exploits this by betting very large with aces and queens. X must retain some aces in his checking range to prevent this exploitation.

Player X’s Optimal Strategy

With Aces

Bet r ≈ 0.4142 with some aces. Bet τ = 0.2521 with some aces (mixed in with kings). Check remaining aces. If checked and Y bets, raise all-in.

With Kings

Bet τ = 0.2521 with some kings. Check remaining kings. Call enough to make total calling frequency equal to 1/(1 + s) against Y’s bets.

With Queens

Bet r with some queens (as bluffs, balanced against ace bets). Bet τ with 7.81% of queens (fold to any raise). Check and fold remaining queens.

Player Y’s Optimal Strategy

If X Checks

Bet s0 = 0.5422 with all aces and 35.16% of queens. If X check-raises, reraise all-in with aces, fold queens.

If X Bets Amount t

Raise to t2 with all aces and β of queens. Call with enough kings to make total calling frequency with hands that beat a queen equal to 1/(1 + t). If X re-raises, Y re-raises all-in with aces and folds queens.

The Value Function A(t)

The analysis constructs a function A(t) representing the value to X of betting amount t with aces. This function is the sum of:

  • Value from being called by Y’s kings
  • Value from picking off Y’s raise-bluffs with queens

The optimal t maximizes this function, yielding the equilibrium blocking bet size of τ = 0.2521.

Why X Can’t Bet All Aces

If X bets all aces (leaving only kings when checking), Y bets very large with aces and some queens. X must call with kings at a loss, which exceeds the gain from betting aces. The equilibrium requires X to keep some aces in reserve for his checking range to protect against Y’s large bets.

Key Concepts

  • Blocking bets reduce opponents’ ability to extract value with large bets
  • The Golden Mean r = √2 − 1 appears again as X’s primary bet size
  • Players must protect their checking ranges by keeping strong hands in them
  • Y’s raise-bluff frequency β is calibrated to make X indifferent about betting kings
  • The solution closely mirrors the spread-limit game from Chapter 17
  • Even in no-limit, balance and mixed strategies are essential at equilibrium
