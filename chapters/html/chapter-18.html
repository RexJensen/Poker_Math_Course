Part III — Chapter 18

Lessons and Values: The [0,1] Game Redux

Extracting deep strategic lessons from the continuous distribution model — position, bluffing value, information, and equity denial

Synthesizing [0,1] Game Lessons

Having solved the [0,1] game in Chapter 17, we now step back and ask: what does this model teach us? The [0,1] game is elegant not just because it is solvable, but because the lessons it produces transfer directly to real poker. In this chapter, we quantify several of the most important strategic concepts that emerge from the model.

We will measure the value of position, the value of being able to bluff, the value of information, and the concept of equity denial. Each of these is a cornerstone of modern poker strategy, and the [0,1] framework lets us put precise numbers on ideas that are usually discussed only qualitatively.

The Value of Position

Every poker player knows that position matters. But how much does it matter? The [0,1] game lets us compute the answer exactly.

Positional Advantage

In the [0,1] game, the in-position player (the one who acts last) has a measurable EV advantage over the out-of-position player. This advantage arises because the in-position player gets to see the opponent's action before deciding.

Equation 18.1 — Value of Position EVIP − EVOOP = f(P, b) > 0

The exact formula depends on the pot and bet sizes, but the key result is that this difference is always positive. Position is always worth something, and its value increases as the pot-to-bet ratio changes.

Why Position Matters Mathematically

The in-position player's advantage stems from two sources:

  • Informational advantage: IP sees OOP's action (bet or check) before deciding. A check from OOP reveals weakness. A bet from OOP narrows their range.
  • Strategic flexibility: IP can choose to bet or check after seeing OOP's action, allowing them to tailor their strategy to the situation. OOP must act "blind" — without knowing IP's intention.

In the [0,1] model with P = 10 and b = 5, the in-position player's EV is typically 5-15% higher than the out-of-position player's EV. This advantage compounds across every hand played, which is why seat selection is one of the most underrated skills in poker.

The Value of the Bluff

What if you could never bluff? How much would that cost you? The [0,1] game provides a precise answer.

A World Without Bluffing

If the bettor is restricted to only value betting (no bluffs allowed), the opponent can adopt a trivially simple counter-strategy: always fold when bet into. Since the bettor only bets with strong hands, calling is a losing proposition. The bettor's value bets never get paid off.

Equation 18.2 — No-Bluff EV EVno bluff = EV from winning uncontested pots only
EVwith bluff = EVno bluff + Value from getting calls on value bets

This reveals a profound truth: bluffing is not just about winning pots with bad hands. The primary value of bluffing is that it makes your value bets profitable. If opponents know you never bluff, they never call, and your strong hands earn nothing beyond the existing pot. Bluffing creates uncertainty, which forces opponents to call, which allows your value bets to extract additional money.

The Symbiosis of Bluffs and Value Bets

Think of bluffs and value bets as symbiotic partners:

  • Bluffs need value bets: If you only bluffed, opponents would always call, and your bluffs would lose money.
  • Value bets need bluffs: If you only value bet, opponents would always fold, and your value bets would earn no extra money.
  • Together: Bluffs force opponents to call, making value bets profitable. Value bets force opponents to be cautious, making bluffs profitable. The equilibrium balances these forces exactly.

The Value of Information

How much would you pay to see your opponent's hand? In the [0,1] model, this question has a concrete answer.

Perfect Information Value

The value of seeing the opponent's hand is the difference between your EV with perfect information and your EV without it.

Equation 18.3 — Information Value Vinfo = EVperfect − EVequilibrium

With perfect information, you would: bet for value only when ahead, bluff only when the opponent will fold, and call only when you are ahead. Every decision would be optimal. The gap between this ideal and equilibrium play is the price of uncertainty.

In practice, this concept explains why tracking software, hand histories, and opponent profiling have value. Each piece of information moves you closer to the "perfect information" EV, and the difference is measurable.

Equity Denial

Equity Denial

Equity denial occurs when you bet and your opponent folds a hand that had positive equity in the pot. By betting, you prevent them from freely realizing their share of the pot at showdown. You "deny" them the equity they would have earned by seeing the hand to completion.

Equation 18.4 — Equity Denied Equity Denied = Opponent's showdown equity × Pot × P(opponent folds)
Equity Denial in Action

In a [0,1] game with pot P = 10:

You hold hand = 0.7. If you check and go to showdown, your opponent wins whenever they hold a hand above 0.7 (30% of the time). Your expected showdown value is 0.7 × 10 = 7.

Now suppose you bet b = 5 and your opponent folds all hands below 0.6. Those folded hands (the interval [0, 0.6]) had total equity of roughly 30% of the pot in aggregate. By betting, you denied that equity — they surrendered their claim to the pot rather than pay to see the showdown.

Importantly, some of those folded hands (in the range [0, 0.3] or so) had very little equity. But hands near 0.5 or 0.6 had significant equity that was denied. This is the essence of why betting with a good-but-vulnerable hand is valuable: it prevents the opponent from freely realizing their share.

Equity denial is closely related to the concept of protection.

Protection

Protection Betting

A protection bet is a bet made with a hand that is currently ahead but vulnerable to being overtaken. The bet aims to fold out hands that could improve to beat you, or (in the [0,1] static model) hands that have some showdown equity against you.

In the [0,1] game, "protection" and "equity denial" are essentially the same concept — your hand is ahead right now, and by betting you prevent opponents from freely winning their share of the pot at showdown. In real poker with community cards, protection has the additional dimension of folding out draws: hands that could improve on future streets to beat you.

Protection in Real Poker

You hold A♠K♥ on a board of A♦ 8♣ 7♣. You have top pair, top kicker — currently the best hand most of the time. But there are many draws:

  • Flush draws (two clubs) — ~35% to complete by the river
  • Straight draws (9-6, T-9, 6-5) — various percentages
  • Overcards with backdoor equity

By betting, you deny these draws the right to see free cards. You either collect the pot immediately (they fold) or charge them a price to continue (they call). Either way, you protect your equity advantage.

The Continuum of Hand Strength

One of the deepest conceptual lessons of the [0,1] game is that hand strength is a continuous spectrum, not a set of discrete categories.

Relative Hand Strength

In the [0,1] model, your hand value (say 0.72) has no absolute meaning. Its strength is entirely defined by where it falls relative to your opponent's distribution. If the opponent's range is concentrated in [0.8, 1.0], then 0.72 is weak. If their range is spread across [0, 0.6], then 0.72 is dominant.

This challenges the common poker habit of thinking in rigid categories: "I have top pair, that's a medium-strength hand." In reality, top pair could be anything from a monster (against a wide range) to nearly worthless (against a tight, strong range). The [0,1] model trains you to think about hand strength as a position on a continuum, defined relative to the opponent's likely distribution.

Application to Real Poker

The [0,1] game distills poker into its essential structure. The strategies that emerge from this model form a practical framework applicable to almost every real poker situation.

The Three-Tier Framework
Tier[0,1] ModelReal Poker AnalogAction
Strong Hands Top of [0,1] range Sets, two pair, overpairs, top pair top kicker Bet for value — build the pot, get called by worse
Medium Hands Middle of [0,1] range Middle pairs, weak top pairs, second pair Check — too good to bluff, not strong enough to value bet (the "condensed range")
Weak Hands Bottom of [0,1] range Missed draws, no pair/no draw, pure air Bluff — no showdown value, might win the pot with a bet

This three-tier framework — bet the top, check the middle, bluff the bottom — is the foundation of modern poker strategy. It applies on the flop, on the turn, on the river, and (with modifications) preflop as well. Whenever you are unsure what to do with a hand, ask yourself: "Where does this hand fall on the continuum? Is it a value bet, a check, or a bluff?"

The Fundamental Strategic Question For any hand in any situation, determine:
1. Am I ahead of my opponent's calling range? → Value bet
2. Am I behind their calling range but ahead of some checking range? → Check
3. Am I behind nearly everything? → Bluff or fold

Key Concepts

  • Position has quantifiable value. The in-position player always has a higher EV in the [0,1] game. This advantage comes from acting with more information.
  • Bluffs enable value bets. Without the threat of bluffs, opponents always fold to bets, and strong hands earn nothing extra. Bluffing creates the uncertainty that makes value bets profitable.
  • Information has a price. The gap between perfect-information EV and equilibrium EV measures the value of seeing your opponent's hand. Every piece of information you gather (tells, patterns, history) narrows this gap.
  • Equity denial is a core reason to bet. Betting forces opponents to fold hands that had positive showdown equity, preventing them from freely realizing their share of the pot.
  • Protection is equity denial in action. Betting with strong-but-vulnerable hands charges draws and marginal hands a price to continue.
  • Hand strength is a continuum. Your hand's value depends entirely on where it falls relative to the opponent's distribution — not on absolute categories.
  • Three-tier framework: Bet the top (value), check the middle (showdown value), bluff the bottom (no showdown value). This structure applies across nearly all poker situations.
