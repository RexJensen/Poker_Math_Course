Part III — Chapter 15

Player X Strikes Back: Full-Street Games

Extending the model to two-player action — check-raises, donk bets, and the true value of position

From Half-Street to Full-Street

In Chapters 11 through 14, we studied half-street games: one player bets or checks, and the other player calls or folds. This is a powerful but incomplete model. In real poker, both players can act. The first player might check, the second player might bet, and then the first player can call, fold, or raise.

The full-street game allows both players to act, creating a richer set of strategic possibilities. The most important new element is the check-raise — a weapon that was entirely absent from half-street analysis.

Full-Street Game

A game in which both players have the opportunity to act. Player X (first to act) can check or bet. If X checks, Player Y can check or bet. If Y bets after X checks, X can fold, call, or raise. If X bets initially, Y can fold, call, or raise.

This dramatically increases the complexity. In a half-street game, the game tree has a handful of terminal nodes. In a full-street game, the tree branches much more extensively, and the equilibrium strategies become correspondingly more complex.

The Check-Raise: A Fundamental Weapon

The check-raise is the defining strategic element of full-street games. It occurs when Player X checks, Player Y bets, and then Player X raises.

Check-Raise

The act of checking with the intention of raising after the opponent bets. It is a deceptive play that disguises hand strength — the checker appeared weak but is actually strong (or bluffing).

Why Check-Raising Matters

Without the check-raise, the half-street model gives the bettor enormous power. The bettor can bet with value hands and bluffs freely, knowing the checker can only call or fold. The check-raise changes this dynamic fundamentally:

  • It punishes loose betting. If Player Y bets too frequently after X checks, X can check-raise and force Y to put in more money with weak hands.
  • It extracts extra value. By checking a strong hand, X can induce a bet from Y and then raise, winning more than a simple bet would have.
  • It creates a trapping threat. The mere possibility of a check-raise constrains the opponent's betting strategy, even when the check-raise does not actually occur.

Optimal Check-Raise Frequency

Like bluffing, check-raising has an optimal frequency. Check-raise too often, and the opponent adapts by checking behind more frequently (denying you the chance to raise). Check-raise too rarely, and the opponent bets freely after your checks, exploiting your passivity.

Check-Raise Balance Principle The optimal check-raise frequency makes the opponent indifferent
between betting and checking after a check.

If X check-raises too often → Y checks behind more → X loses value
If X check-raises too rarely → Y bets freely → X is exploited

The exact frequency depends on the bet size, pot size, and the hand distributions of both players. In the simplified AKQ game (discussed below), we can solve for it precisely.

The Full-Street AKQ Game

To illustrate full-street concepts, we extend the AKQ game from Chapter 13. Recall the setup: a three-card deck (A, K, Q), each player is dealt one card, there is an ante in the pot, and the bet size is fixed.

Half-Street Review

In the half-street version, Player 1 could only bet or check, and Player 2 could only call or fold. The optimal strategy was:

  • Player 1 bets the Ace (value) and sometimes bluffs with the Queen
  • Player 1 always checks the King (medium hand)
  • Player 2 calls with the Ace always, and calls with the King at the right frequency

Full-Street Extension

Now Player 1 can also check-raise. After Player 1 checks and Player 2 bets, Player 1 can raise instead of just calling or folding. This adds several new strategic elements:

New Strategic Possibilities

Check-raise with the Ace (trapping): Player 1 checks the Ace, hoping Player 2 bets so Player 1 can raise. This wins more than simply betting, but risks Player 2 checking behind and winning nothing extra.

Check-call with the King: The King is too strong to fold but not strong enough to raise. Player 1 checks and calls a bet, playing the middle-strength hand passively.

Check-raise bluff with the Queen: Player 1 can check, wait for Player 2 to bet, and then raise as a bluff. This is risky but creates balance in the check-raising range.

The Optimal Solution

The full-street AKQ game solution involves mixed strategies (randomizing between actions at certain frequencies). Key results include:

  • Player 1 sometimes bets the Ace directly and sometimes check-raises it
  • Player 1 checks the King and calls if Player 2 bets
  • Player 1 bluffs with the Queen at a lower frequency than in the half-street game (because the check-raise creates additional bluffing opportunities)
  • Player 2 must bet less aggressively after Player 1 checks (because of the check-raise threat)
Key Result: Full-Street AKQ Equilibrium Player 1's strategy with Ace: mix between betting and check-raising
Player 1's strategy with King: check, then call if Y bets
Player 1's strategy with Queen: mix between bluff-betting and checking

The check-raise threat constrains Player 2's betting frequency after a check.

Donk Betting

A donk bet is a bet made into the previous aggressor — that is, when a player who was passive on the previous street (calling or checking) suddenly leads out with a bet on the next street.

Donk Bet

A bet made by a player who was not the aggressor on the previous street, into the player who was. In standard play, the non-aggressor checks to the aggressor. A donk bet deviates from this convention.

Is Donk Betting Optimal?

In most optimal strategies derived from game theory, donk betting is rare or entirely absent. The reasoning:

  • Information disadvantage: The caller from the previous street typically has a weaker, less defined range. Betting into a stronger, better-defined range is usually suboptimal.
  • Check-raise option: By checking, the out-of-position player preserves the option to check-raise, which is often more profitable than donk betting.
  • Range asymmetry: The previous aggressor's range usually contains more strong hands. Donk betting into this range risks being raised frequently.

However, there are specific situations where donk betting can appear in optimal play — typically when the board changes dramatically and the caller's range benefits more from the new card than the previous bettor's range.

The Value of Position in Full-Street Games

One of the most important results from full-street analysis is the quantification of positional advantage. The player who acts last (in position) has a significant strategic edge.

Why Position Matters

  • Information advantage: The in-position player sees the opponent's action before deciding. A check reveals weakness; a bet reveals strength (or bluff). This information is valuable.
  • Pot control: The in-position player can check behind to keep the pot small with medium-strength hands, or bet to grow it with strong hands.
  • Bluff efficiency: The in-position player's bluffs are more efficient because they occur after the opponent has shown weakness (by checking).
  • Closing the action: When in position on the last street, you get the final say. You cannot be check-raised if you check, and your bet closes the action if the opponent just calls or folds.
Position Advantage Quantified

In the full-street AKQ game with a pot of P and bet of b, the in-position player's expected value is strictly higher than the out-of-position player's. The difference comes from:

  • Ability to bet or check behind after the opponent checks (free showdown or value bet)
  • No risk of being check-raised when checking behind
  • Better information for calling/folding decisions when facing a bet

The check-raise partially compensates the out-of-position player, but does not fully close the gap. Position remains a significant advantage.

Key Result

Position Value Theorem EV(in-position) > EV(out-of-position) in full-street games

The difference in EV represents the mathematical value of position.
The check-raise partially but incompletely compensates the OOP player.

This result explains why experienced poker players fight for position and why being on the button is universally considered the most profitable seat at the table.

Key Concepts

  • Full-street games allow both players to act, creating richer strategy spaces than half-street games.
  • The check-raise is the key new element: checking strong hands to induce a bet, then raising. It constrains the opponent's betting frequency.
  • Optimal check-raise frequency makes the opponent indifferent between betting and checking after a check.
  • In the full-street AKQ game, Player 1 mixes between betting and check-raising the Ace, check-calls the King, and adjusts bluff frequency accordingly.
  • Donk betting (betting into the previous aggressor) is generally rare in optimal strategies because checking preserves the more profitable check-raise option.
  • The in-position player has higher EV than the out-of-position player. The check-raise partially but incompletely compensates for this disadvantage.
