Online Poker Data Mining

Online poker provides a fundamentally different information environment than live poker. Every hand played online generates a hand history — a complete, machine-readable record of every action taken by every player. These histories can be downloaded, stored, and analyzed at scale.

Software tools such as tracking programs automate the collection process, aggregating thousands or even millions of hands into searchable databases. This gives players access to detailed statistics on their opponents — far more data than any human could gather through observation alone at a live table.

Chen and Ankenman highlight that this data abundance transforms the nature of "reading" opponents. In live poker, reads are qualitative and subjective. Online, they become quantitative and statistical.

Statistical Tells

In live poker, tells are physical — a trembling hand, a change in breathing, eye contact or avoidance. Online, tells come from data patterns. The key statistics that serve as proxies for reading opponent tendencies include:

VPIP — Voluntarily Put Money in Pot

The percentage of hands in which a player voluntarily puts money into the pot (excluding posting blinds). A high VPIP indicates a loose player who plays many hands; a low VPIP indicates a tight player.

VPIP = (Hands voluntarily entered / Total hands dealt) × 100%

Typical range: 15–25% for solid players; 35%+ for loose players; under 12% for very tight players.

PFR — Pre-Flop Raise Percentage

How often a player raises before the flop. The gap between VPIP and PFR reveals passivity: a player with VPIP=40% and PFR=8% is loose-passive (calling station), while VPIP=22% and PFR=18% is tight-aggressive.

PFR = (Hands raised preflop / Total hands dealt) × 100%
Aggression Factor (AF)

The ratio of aggressive actions (bets + raises) to passive actions (calls). An AF of 1.0 means the player bets/raises as often as they call; above 2.0 is aggressive; below 1.0 is passive.

AF = (Bets + Raises) / Calls
Went to Showdown % (WTSD)

How often a player who sees the flop goes all the way to showdown. A high WTSD (above 30%) suggests a player who is difficult to bluff; a low WTSD (below 22%) suggests a player who folds to pressure on later streets.

These statistics are mathematical proxies for the qualitative reads that experienced live players develop through observation. Together, they form a statistical profile that can classify opponents into archetypes and guide strategic adjustments.

Timing Tells

Even without physical presence, the speed of an opponent's actions can provide information in online poker:

Long pause before bettingStrong hand (thinking about bet sizing, not whether to bet)Low–Moderate
Instant checkWeakness (used auto-check or gave up quickly)Moderate
Long pause then checkConsidered betting but decided against (medium strength)Low

Timing tells are less reliable than pattern-based statistical tells. Players may be multi-tabling, distracted, or deliberately varying their timing. Use them as supplementary information, not primary reads.

Bet Sizing Tells

In online poker, players often develop unconscious bet sizing patterns that correlate with hand strength:

Common Bet Sizing Patterns
  • Larger bets (75–100%+ pot) — Often correlated with strong hands or polarized ranges
  • Min-bets or small bets (25–33% pot) — Frequently used as blocking bets with medium-strength hands
  • Specific recurring sizes — A player who always bets exactly $12 with strong hands and $6 with bluffs reveals exploitable information

With a large enough sample, you can identify whether a specific opponent's bet sizing is informative (correlated with hand strength) or balanced (uncorrelated).

Multi-Tabling and Volume

Online play enables much higher volume than live poker. A live player might see 25–35 hands per hour; an online player can play 60–80 hands per hour per table, and many players run 4–16 tables simultaneously.

This has important statistical implications:

The Volume vs. Quality Trade-off
  • More hands = tighter confidence intervals on your win rate. Playing 100,000 hands gives a much more reliable estimate of your true edge than 10,000 hands.
  • Multi-tabling may reduce decision quality. Complex spots requiring deep analysis may be handled suboptimally when attention is divided across many tables.
  • The optimal number of tables balances total EV: if your win rate per table drops from 3 BB/100 (1 table) to 2 BB/100 (4 tables), your total hourly EV still increases — but only up to a point.
Total Hourly EV EVtotal = (Tables) × (Hands/hr/table) × (Win rate per hand)

The key question: does adding another table reduce your per-table win rate enough to offset the added volume?

Sample Size and Data Reliability

This section connects directly to the variance and confidence interval concepts from Chapters 2 and 3. The central problem: how much data do you need on an opponent before their statistics are reliable?

VPIP Confidence Interval

If you observe a player with VPIP = 30% over n hands, the 95% confidence interval for their true VPIP is approximately:

CI95 = VPIP ± 1.96 × √(VPIP × (1 − VPIP) / n)

With 50 hands: 30% ± 12.7% → true VPIP could be anywhere from 17.3% to 42.7%

With 500 hands: 30% ± 4.0% → true VPIP is likely between 26.0% and 34.0%

With 5,000 hands: 30% ± 1.3% → true VPIP is very likely between 28.7% and 31.3%

The takeaway: small samples on individual opponents may be wildly unreliable. You need large samples before adjusting significantly based on observed statistics.

Bayesian Approach to Opponent Reads

Rather than relying solely on observed data, the optimal approach is Bayesian: combine your prior beliefs (based on population tendencies, game type, and stakes) with the observed data to form a posterior estimate.

With few hands, lean heavily on priors. As your sample grows, let the data dominate. This mirrors the Bayesian framework introduced in Chapter 3.

Key Concepts

  • Online hand histories provide far more data than live observation, transforming opponent reads from qualitative to quantitative.
  • VPIP, PFR, Aggression Factor, and WTSD are the core statistical tells that classify opponents into player types (tight/loose, passive/aggressive).
  • Timing tells (speed of actions) and bet sizing tells (patterns in bet amounts) provide supplementary but less reliable information.
  • Multi-tabling increases volume but may decrease per-table decision quality — the optimal number of tables maximizes total hourly EV.
  • Small sample sizes produce unreliable statistics — use confidence intervals to assess how much to trust observed data on an opponent.
  • A Bayesian approach combines prior beliefs with observed data, leaning on priors when samples are small and letting data dominate as samples grow.
