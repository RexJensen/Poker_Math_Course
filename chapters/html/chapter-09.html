Part II — Chapter 9

Adaptive Play: Distribution vs. Distribution

When both players hold ranges, the game becomes a dynamic battle of strategy adjustments

Both Players Have Distributions

In Chapter 7, we analyzed hand vs. hand (cards exposed). In Chapter 8, we advanced to hand vs. distribution — we know our cards but estimate the opponent’s range. Now we take the final step: distribution vs. distribution, where both players hold ranges of possible hands.

This is the most realistic model of poker. Neither player knows the other’s exact hand. Each player’s strategy must account for the full range of hands the other might hold — and the other player is doing the same thing simultaneously.

Distribution vs. Distribution

A framework where both Player A and Player B hold distributions (ranges) of possible hands, and each player’s optimal strategy depends on the other’s distribution. This creates a strategic interdependence that is the hallmark of poker as a game of skill.

EV Calculation: The Double Summation

When both players hold distributions, the expected value calculation requires summing over all possible matchups of hands from both ranges:

Equation 9.1 — Distribution vs. Distribution EV <Adist, Bdist> = Σi Σj p(Ai) × p(Bj) × EV(Ai vs Bj)

This is a double summation: for every hand i that Player A might hold, and every hand j that Player B might hold, we compute the EV of the matchup and weight it by the joint probability of both hands occurring.

Simplified Example

Suppose Player A’s range is {AA (30%), KK (70%)} and Player B’s range is {QQ (50%), AK (50%)}.

We evaluate all four matchups:

A’s HandB’s HandJoint ProbA’s EquityWeighted EV
AA (30%)QQ (50%)15%81%0.15 × 0.81 = 0.122
AA (30%)AK (50%)15%93%0.15 × 0.93 = 0.140
KK (70%)QQ (50%)35%81%0.35 × 0.81 = 0.284
KK (70%)AK (50%)35%69%0.35 × 0.69 = 0.242
A’s total equity = 0.122 + 0.140 + 0.284 + 0.242 = 78.7%

Player A’s range has 78.7% equity against Player B’s range. Note that this is different from any single hand-vs-hand matchup.

The Strategy Adaptation Cycle

The most important insight of distribution vs. distribution play is that strategies are not static. When one player adjusts, the other should counter-adjust, creating a dynamic cycle:

The Adaptation Spiral
  1. Player A plays strategy S1 (e.g., bluffs 30% of the time on the river).
  2. Player B reads S1 and plays maximally exploitive S2 (e.g., calls more often since A bluffs a lot).
  3. Player A reads S2 and adjusts to S3 (e.g., bluffs less since B is calling more).
  4. Player B reads S3 and adjusts to S4 (e.g., folds more since A is now bluffing less).
  5. The cycle continues...
Exploitive Strategy

A strategy designed to maximally exploit a specific opponent’s tendencies. The exploitive response to a player who bluffs too much is to call more. The exploitive response to a player who never bluffs is to fold more. The exploitive response is always the maximally profitable counter-strategy against a fixed opponent strategy.

The Edge Goes to the Faster Adapter

In the adaptation cycle, the player who adjusts more quickly and accurately gains an edge. Strong players constantly revise their model of the opponent’s strategy and adjust accordingly. Weak players play a static strategy, making them easy targets for exploitation.

The Adaptation Advantage If A plays strategy S1 and B plays optimal counter S2:
EV(B) ≥ EV(B against S1 with default strategy)

If A then adjusts to S3 but B is slow to counter:
EV(A with S3) > EV(A with S1) against the now-outdated S2

Limitations of Exploitive Play

Pure exploitive play has a fundamental vulnerability: if your opponent also adapts, your exploitive strategy can itself be exploited.

The Exploitation Trap

Suppose you notice your opponent never bluffs on the river, so you adopt the exploitive counter-strategy of always folding to river bets (since they only bet with strong hands).

But if your opponent notices that you always fold, they can start bluffing 100% of the time on the river — exploiting your exploitation!

Now you are the one being exploited, worse than if you had just played a balanced strategy from the start.

This leads to a crucial question: Is there a strategy that cannot be exploited? A strategy so balanced that no matter what the opponent does, they cannot gain an edge?

The Path to Game Theory

The search for an unexploitable strategy — one that guarantees at least break-even results regardless of the opponent’s counter-strategy — leads directly to game theory and the concept of Nash equilibrium. This is the subject of Part III.

The Nash equilibrium strategy is the “fixed point” of the adaptation cycle: the strategy where neither player can improve by deviating unilaterally.

Exploitive vs. Optimal: The Tradeoff

ApproachStrengthWeakness
Pure ExploitiveMaximizes EV against a static opponentVulnerable to counter-exploitation by adapting opponents

Practical Framework

In reality, perfect distribution vs. distribution calculations are computationally intractable at the poker table. Even a simplified game with 100 possible hands per player requires 10,000 matchup evaluations — and real poker has millions of possible hand combinations across multiple streets.

Skilled players approximate using:

  • Range categories: Grouping hands into “strong,” “medium,” “weak,” and “draw” categories rather than evaluating each combo individually.
  • Heuristics: Rules of thumb developed from off-table study (e.g., “if the board is wet and the opponent checks twice, their range is capped”).
  • Pattern recognition: Experience builds an intuitive sense for how ranges interact, allowing near-instant approximations.
  • Off-table study: Solvers and equity calculators allow players to study distribution vs. distribution scenarios before they arise in play.

The mathematical framework in this chapter — even if we can’t compute it perfectly in real time — tells us what we’re trying to approximate. Understanding the theory improves the approximations.

Key Concepts

  • Distribution vs. distribution is the most realistic poker analysis: both players hold ranges, and EV is computed as a double summation over all possible hand matchups.
  • The strategy adaptation cycle shows how players iteratively adjust strategies to exploit each other’s tendencies.
  • The player who adapts faster and more accurately gains an edge in the adaptation cycle.
  • Pure exploitive play is vulnerable to counter-exploitation when the opponent also adapts, motivating the search for unexploitable strategies.
  • Nash equilibrium (game theory, Part III) represents the fixed point of the adaptation cycle — a strategy no opponent can profitably counter.
  • In practice, players approximate distribution vs. distribution calculations using range categories, heuristics, pattern recognition, and off-table study.
