A Revolution in Strategic Thinking

In the late 1970s and early 1980s, bond and option markets were dominated by traders who learned their craft by experience. They believed their intuition was a renewable edge. By the mid-1990s, a revolution had occurred: the old-school traders had been replaced by quantitative analysts who applied mathematics to the “art” of trading, making of it a science.

A similar transformation happened in backgammon. Programs based on neural network technology and mathematical analysis would have been mocked as “overaggressive and weak” by 1970s experts. Today, computer analyses are considered the final word on backgammon play.

Chen and Ankenman argue that poker is undergoing the same revolution. A new breed of players, applying computer science, mathematics, and Internet-based information sharing, has challenged many assumptions underlying traditional approaches to the game.

The Book’s Mission

To provide an introduction to quantitative techniques as applied to poker, and to demonstrate that a mathematical approach can be applied even in situations where it seems impossible — reading hands, adapting to opponents, and choosing creative plays.

Common Misconceptions About Math and Poker

The authors identify and address several widespread misconceptions:

Misconception 1: “There’s always one mathematically correct play”

In reality, the mathematically correct play depends on the model we use. Different assumptions about an opponent’s strategy lead to different correct actions. Math provides the framework for converting assumptions into optimal responses — it doesn’t always yield a single universal answer.

Misconception 2: “Mathematical play is predictable and lacks creativity”

An optimal strategy incorporates a precisely calibrated mixture of bluffs, semi-bluffs, and value bets that make it appear entirely unpredictable. “Predictable” connotes “exploitable,” but this is not necessarily true. A player who always raises with aces is not exploitable as long as he also raises with some other hands. This concept of balance is central to Part III of the book.

Misconception 3: “Sometimes the numbers go out the window”

This stems from a narrow definition of “mathematical play.” Incorporating new information — reads, tells, betting patterns — into opponent models is mathematical. Bayes’ theorem (Chapter 3) provides the precise tool for quantifying how new information should update our beliefs.

Mathematics and Psychology

The authors do not claim that psychology is without value. Quite the opposite: the psychology of poker is an invaluable tool for exploitive play. Reads, tells, and opponent profiling all feed into the assumptions that drive mathematical models.

The key insight is that once we have characterized an opponent’s tendencies into assumptions, the methods of mathematics take over and intuition fails as a guide to proper play. Mathematics tells us what to do with the information our intuition provides.

The book also addresses what to do when such information is unavailable — the domain of optimal (game-theoretic) play covered in Part III.

How This Course Is Organized

The book (and this course) is divided into five parts:

Part I: Basics

Foundational concepts — probability, expected value, variance, and Bayes’ theorem. These tools apply to all forms of gambling and strategic decision-making.

Part II: Exploitive Play

Using information about opponents to deviate from default strategy and increase profit. Topics include pot odds, hand reading, online tells, and playing accurately against known opponent tendencies.

Part III: Optimal Play

Game theory and its application to poker. Through a series of simplified “toy games,” the book develops principles of bet sizing, bluffing frequency, and multi-street strategy.

Part IV: Risk

Bankroll management, risk of ruin, the Kelly criterion, and backing arrangements. Winning players still go broke if they don’t manage risk properly.

Part V: Other Topics

Tournament strategy (chip value differs from cash value), multiplayer games, and a synthesis chapter on applying everything together.

Who This Course Is For

As Chen and Ankenman write, the book targets a wide range of players:

  • Computer scientists and options traders who have recently taken up poker
  • Solid, winning players who feel existing literature lacks the insight to raise their game
  • World-class players — holders of multiple bracelets who play in the biggest games

The one prerequisite is some poker experience. If you have never played, the authors recommend gaining experience first before diving into the mathematics.

The mathematical level is kept accessible — high school algebra and basic probability are sufficient for most of the material. Where advanced techniques appear, intuitive supplemental arguments are provided.

Key Takeaways

  • Mathematics is the strongest foundation for poker decision-making, just as quantitative methods transformed trading and backgammon
  • There is no single “correct play” independent of assumptions — math provides the framework for converting assumptions into optimal responses
  • Optimal play is not predictable — balanced strategies with calibrated bluffing frequencies are inherently unexploitable
  • Psychology and mathematics complement each other — reads provide the assumptions; math determines the actions
  • Expected value (EV) is the primary metric for evaluating poker decisions
  • Risk management matters — even winning players can go broke without proper bankroll management
