Playing Accurately, Part II: Hand vs. Distribution

Moving from face-up play to real poker — playing your known hand against a range of possible opponent hands

From Exposed Cards to Hidden Information

In Chapter 7, we analyzed play with both hands exposed. In real poker, of course, we don’t see the opponent’s cards. Instead, we estimate what they might hold based on their actions — this estimate is their hand distribution (or range).

The “hand vs. distribution” framework represents the first step toward real poker analysis: we know our own hand exactly, and we model the opponent’s hand as a probability distribution over all possible holdings.

Hand Distribution (Range)

A probability-weighted set of all hands an opponent could hold given the information available. As the hand progresses and the opponent takes actions (betting, checking, raising, folding), we update the distribution using Bayesian reasoning (Chapter 3).

Expected Value Against a Distribution

To evaluate an action (bet, call, check, fold, raise) against an opponent whose hand is unknown, we compute the weighted average EV across all hands in their distribution:

Equation 8.1 — EV Against a Distribution <A, B> = Σi p(handi) × EV(A vs handi)

where A is our action, B is the opponent’s distribution, p(handi) is the probability of each hand in B, and EV(A vs handi) is the expected value of taking action A against that specific hand.

The Process

For each possible action:
1. Enumerate every hand in the opponent’s range.
2. Calculate EV against each hand (as if cards were exposed).
3. Multiply each EV by the probability of that hand.
4. Sum all terms.
Choose the action with the highest total EV.

Example: River Value Bet Decision

Top Pair on the River

You hold A♠K♥ on a board of K♠9♥4♣2♦7♣. The pot is $200.

You estimate your opponent’s distribution as:

Hand CategoryProbabilityYour Result if You Bet $100
Sets (KK, 99, 44)10%They raise; you lose $100
Two pair (K9, K4)15%They call; you lose $100
Top pair, weaker kicker (KQ, KJ, KT)40%They call; you win $100
Missed draws (flush/straight draws)35%They fold; you win $0 extra

EV Calculation: Bet $100

EV(Bet) Breakdown vs Sets (10%): 0.10 × (-$100) = -$10.00
vs Two pair (15%): 0.15 × (-$100) = -$15.00
vs Weaker top pair (40%): 0.40 × (+$100) = +$40.00
vs Missed draws (35%): 0.35 × ($0) = $0.00

EV(Bet) = -$10 - $15 + $40 + $0 = +$15.00

EV Calculation: Check

EV(Check) If you check, you win the $200 pot whenever you have the best hand (75% of the time).
But you would have won those hands anyway. The question is about the marginal EV.
Marginal EV(Check) = $0 (no additional money gained or lost beyond the pot)

Since EV(Bet) = +$15 > EV(Check) = $0, you should bet. This is a thin value bet — you lose to 25% of the opponent’s range but still profit from betting.

Thin Value Betting

Thin Value Bet

A bet made for value when you beat some but not all of the opponent’s range. The bet is correct whenever EV(bet) > EV(check), which depends on:

  • What fraction of the range calls (vs. folds or raises)
  • What fraction of the calling range you beat
  • The bet size relative to the pot

The key mathematical condition for a profitable thin value bet:

Thin Value Bet Condition EV(bet) > 0 when: (% of calling range you beat) > 50%

More precisely: p(call and you win) × betsize > p(call and you lose) × betsize + p(raise and you fold) × betsize

Notice that hands that fold to your bet are irrelevant to the value calculation — you win nothing extra from them whether you bet or check. The entire value-betting decision comes down to how you fare against the portion of the range that calls or raises.

The Importance of Combinatorics

When constructing an opponent’s distribution, it’s critical to account for the number of combinations each holding represents. Not all hand categories are equally likely.

Hand Combinations
Pocket pair (e.g., AA): C(4,2) = 6 combos
Suited hand (e.g., AKs): 4 suits = 4 combos
Offsuit hand (e.g., AKo): 4 × 3 = 12 combos
Total AK combos: 4 + 12 = 16 combos
Why Combinatorics Matter

Suppose you know your opponent holds either AA or AK. Naively, you might think it’s 50-50. But combinatorics say otherwise:

AA: 6 combos   |   AK: 16 combos   |   Total: 22 combos

P(AA) = 6/22 = 27.3%
P(AK) = 16/22 = 72.7%

Your opponent is nearly three times more likely to hold AK than AA. Ignoring combinatorics leads to systematically biased EV calculations.

Card Removal Effects

Combinatorics also interact with card removal (also called “blockers”). If you hold an ace, the number of AA combos your opponent can have drops from 6 to 3, and AK combos drop from 16 to 12. Your own cards directly affect the opponent’s distribution.

Playing Against Unknown Opponents

When facing an opponent with no history, we use a default distribution based on general population tendencies. For example, a default preflop raising range from early position might be approximately {AA-TT, AKs-AJs, AKo-AQo} — roughly the top 8% of hands.

As we observe more actions, we refine the distribution using Bayes’ theorem (Chapter 3). Each action narrows or reshapes the range:

  • Preflop raise → narrows to strong starting hands
  • Continuation bet on flop → maintains most of the preflop range
  • Checks flop → removes strongest hands from the range
  • Raises the turn → heavily weights toward strong made hands and semi-bluffs

The better we model the opponent’s distribution, the more accurately we can calculate EV. This is the essence of hand reading as a mathematical exercise.

Key Concepts

  • In real poker, we play our known hand against the opponent’s hand distribution (range).
  • EV against a distribution is the probability-weighted sum of EVs against each hand in the range.
  • A thin value bet is profitable when you beat enough of the calling range, even if some hands in the total range beat you.
  • Combinatorics determine the relative likelihood of each hand: AA has 6 combos, AKs has 4, AKo has 12.
  • Card removal (blockers) affects the opponent’s distribution — your own cards reduce their possible holdings.
  • Against unknown opponents, start with a default distribution and refine it with Bayesian updates as new information arrives.
