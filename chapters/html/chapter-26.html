Doubling Up: Tournaments, Part I

Why doubling your chips doesn’t double your equity — the fundamental economics of tournament poker

Tournament vs Cash Game Fundamentals

In a cash game, the relationship between chips and money is perfectly linear: if you have $200 in chips, you have $200 in value. You can leave the table at any time and exchange your chips for exactly their face value. This makes chip-EV (expected value measured in chips) and dollar-EV identical.

Tournaments are fundamentally different. When you buy into a tournament, your chips become a medium of competition rather than direct representations of money. The prize pool is fixed — it doesn’t grow because someone accumulates more chips. This creates a profound asymmetry:

  • In a cash game, winning 5,000 chips gains you exactly $5,000 of value.
  • In a tournament, winning 5,000 chips gains you less than the value you’d lose if you lost 5,000 chips.

This is the principle of diminishing marginal value of tournament chips. Each additional chip you accumulate is worth slightly less than the previous one, because having all the chips only wins you first-place prize money — not the entire prize pool.

Diminishing Marginal Chip Value

In tournament poker, each additional chip added to your stack is worth less in real-dollar equity than the previous chip. This occurs because the prize pool is fixed and distributed across multiple finishing positions. Having 100% of the chips earns you only the first-place prize (often 25–35% of the prize pool), not 100%.

Cash Game vs Tournament: A Concrete Example

Consider a 10-player tournament with $100 buy-ins ($1,000 prize pool) paying 50/30/20 to the top 3. Each player starts with 10,000 chips (100,000 total).

Cash game logic: 10,000 chips = $100 of value (1 chip = $0.01)

Tournament reality:

  • 10,000 chips (your starting stack) = $100 equity (your buy-in)
  • If you double to 20,000 chips — your equity might be ~$150, not $200
  • If someone has all 100,000 chips — their equity is $500 (1st prize), not $1,000

The player with all the chips owns $500 of equity, but in a cash game those chips would be worth $1,000. The “missing” $500 is distributed as guaranteed equity to the other finishers.

The Independent Chip Model (ICM)

The Independent Chip Model is the standard mathematical framework for converting tournament chip stacks into real-dollar equity. ICM answers the question: “Given the current chip distribution and prize structure, what is each player’s share of the prize pool worth?”

Independent Chip Model (ICM)

A mathematical model that estimates each player’s tournament equity by calculating the probability of finishing in each position based on chip stacks, then multiplying by the corresponding prize. The model assumes each player’s probability of finishing first is proportional to their chip stack, and subsequent finishing probabilities are computed recursively.

How ICM Works

ICM proceeds in several steps:

  1. Probability of finishing 1st: Each player’s probability of winning is proportional to their chip stack. If you have 20% of the chips, you have a 20% chance of finishing 1st.
  2. Probability of finishing 2nd: For each possible 1st-place finisher, remove that player, recalculate the remaining players’ chip proportions, and compute 2nd-place probabilities. Weight by the probability of each 1st-place scenario.
  3. Continue recursively for 3rd, 4th, etc. through all paid positions.
  4. Compute equity: Multiply each finishing probability by the prize for that position, then sum across all positions.
ICM Equity Formula Equity(player i) = ∑ P(player i finishes in position k) × Prize(k)

where k ranges over all paid positions
ICM Calculation: 3-Player Example

Three players remain. Prize pool: $50 / $30 / $20. Chip stacks:

  • Player A: 5,000 chips (50%)
  • Player B: 3,000 chips (30%)
  • Player C: 2,000 chips (20%)

Step 1 — P(1st): A = 50%, B = 30%, C = 20%

Step 2 — P(2nd):

  • If A wins 1st (50%): B gets 2nd with prob 3/5 = 60%, C gets 2nd with prob 2/5 = 40%
  • If B wins 1st (30%): A gets 2nd with prob 5/7 ≈ 71.4%, C gets 2nd with prob 2/7 ≈ 28.6%
  • If C wins 1st (20%): A gets 2nd with prob 5/8 = 62.5%, B gets 2nd with prob 3/8 = 37.5%

Player A’s 2nd-place probability: 0.30 × 0.714 + 0.20 × 0.625 = 0.214 + 0.125 = 0.339

Player A’s equity: 0.50 × $50 + 0.339 × $30 + 0.161 × $20 = $25.00 + $10.17 + $3.22 = $38.39

Note: In a cash game, 50% of the chips would be worth $50. But ICM gives A only $38.39. The “missing” equity is distributed to the smaller stacks as their guaranteed survival equity.

ICM Implications for Strategy

ICM creates strategic distortions that don’t exist in cash games. Understanding these distortions is essential for optimal tournament play.

The Asymmetry of Gains and Losses

The most important consequence of ICM is that gaining chips is worth less than losing the same number of chips. This asymmetry has profound implications:

ICM Asymmetry ΔEquity(winning X chips) < |ΔEquity(losing X chips)|

The magnitude of equity lost from a chip loss always exceeds
the magnitude of equity gained from an equal chip gain.
  • Risk aversion is rational: Unlike cash games where risk-neutral play is optimal, tournament players should be risk-averse. A coin flip for your entire stack is -$EV even though it’s neutral in chips.
  • Calling all-ins requires a bigger edge: Near the bubble or at final tables with significant pay jumps, you need more than 50% equity to profitably call an all-in. The “ICM tax” on calling can be substantial.
  • Survival has inherent value: Even if you never play a hand, other players busting out increases your equity. This “free equity” from survival has no cash game analog.
The Asymmetry in Action

You have 10,000 chips in a tournament with average stack of 10,000. Your ICM equity is $100.

  • If you win a 10,000-chip pot (doubling to 20,000): Your equity rises to ~$150 (gain of $50)
  • If you lose a 10,000-chip pot (busting out): Your equity drops to $0 (loss of $100)

A “fair” coin flip for your stack costs you $25 in equity: (0.5 × $150 + 0.5 × $0) = $75, vs $100 current equity.

You need approximately 67% chip equity to break even on this all-in — not 50%!

The Bubble Effect

The bubble — the point just before players reach the money — is where ICM distortions reach their maximum. This creates one of the most strategically rich situations in poker.

Why the Bubble Is Special

On the bubble, the difference between busting out (winning nothing) and surviving one more elimination (locking up a minimum cash) is enormous relative to other pay jumps. This makes survival disproportionately valuable:

  • Short stacks have disproportionate value: A player with just 2 big blinds has significant equity because they might fold their way into the money. Every other player who busts before them hands them guaranteed prize money.
  • Medium stacks are most constrained: They have too many chips to freely gamble (they’d sacrifice significant survival equity) but not enough to dominate. Medium stacks should play the most conservatively.
  • Large stacks can pressure others: Since medium stacks can’t afford to play back without a premium hand, large stacks can raise liberally, stealing blinds with impunity. The big stack risks relatively little equity by gambling.
The Bubble Effect

The phenomenon near the money bubble where ICM distortions are maximized. Short stacks gain disproportionate equity from survival, medium stacks become strategically paralyzed, and large stacks gain leverage to exploit the situation. The bubble creates the biggest gap between chip-EV strategy and dollar-EV optimal play.

Bubble Dynamics: Who Benefits?

A 100-player tournament pays top 15. We’re on the bubble with 16 remaining. Consider two players:

  • Player A: 50,000 chips (big stack)
  • Player B: 8,000 chips (medium stack)

Player A raises all-in. Player B has pocket Queens. In a cash game, this is a snap-call against most ranges.

But on the bubble, B must consider: calling and losing means going broke, winning nothing. There are several short stacks who might bust soon, gifting B a guaranteed cash. The ICM cost of calling and losing far exceeds the ICM benefit of calling and winning. B might need to fold hands as strong as QQ here.

Meanwhile, Player A can make this play with almost any two cards — they risk minimal equity compared to what they pressure from the medium stacks.

Chip Accumulation vs Survival

Tournament poker presents a fundamental tension between two competing goals:

  • Chip accumulation: To win the tournament, you need all the chips. This requires taking risks, playing pots, and building your stack.
  • Survival: Simply lasting longer increases your equity. Each elimination that happens before yours pushes you up the pay ladder.

ICM provides the mathematical framework for finding the correct balance. At every decision point, the question is: does the expected chip gain from this play generate enough additional equity to offset the risk of elimination?

When to Prioritize Accumulation

  • Early in the tournament (far from the money)
  • When you already have a big stack (your marginal risk is low)
  • When you have a significant skill edge over the table
  • When the pay structure is top-heavy (first place gets a large share)

When to Prioritize Survival

  • Near the bubble (maximum ICM distortion)
  • When shorter stacks are likely to bust soon
  • When pay jumps are large relative to your stack
  • When the pay structure is flat (many spots get similar prizes)

Doubling Up Math

The chapter’s title refers to a counterintuitive result about what doubling your stack actually means in tournament equity. Let’s work through it carefully.

The Doubling Illusion

Suppose the average stack is 10,000 chips and you have 5,000 (half the average). Your equity before the double-up:

  • In a cash game: exactly half of average equity
  • In a tournament: more than half of average equity (due to concave chip utility)

Now you double to 10,000 (the average). Your equity after:

  • In a cash game: exactly the average equity
  • In a tournament: approximately the average equity

The key insight: you started with more than half the average equity and ended with the average equity. So the gain from doubling is less than your initial equity. You risked $X to gain less than $X.

The Doubling Paradox Let E(s) = tournament equity as a function of stack size s.

Because E is concave (diminishing marginal value):
E(2s) < 2 × E(s)

Therefore: E(2s) − E(s) < E(s) − E(0) = E(s)

The equity GAINED from doubling (Δ+) is less than
the equity LOST from busting (Δ− = E(s)).

This mathematical reality means that early tournament double-ups, while exciting, are worth less than they appear. A player who doubles from 5,000 to 10,000 feels like they’ve doubled their chances, but in equity terms the gain is significantly less than 100%.

This also explains why top tournament professionals are willing to play conservatively early — the cost of busting (losing your entire entry equity) outweighs the benefit of a double-up during a stage when average stacks are deep relative to the blinds.

Key Concepts

  • In tournaments, chips have diminishing marginal value — doubling your chips does NOT double your equity.
  • The Independent Chip Model (ICM) converts chip stacks to dollar equity by computing finishing probabilities weighted by prizes.
  • ICM creates an asymmetry: gaining chips is always worth less than the risk of losing the same number of chips.
  • Risk aversion is rational in tournaments (unlike cash games), because the chip utility function is concave.
  • The bubble effect maximizes ICM distortions: short stacks have disproportionate value, big stacks gain leverage, medium stacks are most constrained.
  • Tournament strategy requires balancing chip accumulation (needed to win) with survival (which generates free equity).
  • Early double-ups are less valuable than they appear — you risk your full equity to gain less than your full equity.
