Part III — Chapter 14

You Don't Have To Guess: No-Limit Bet Sizing

How game theory determines the mathematically optimal bet size and its relationship to bluffing frequency

The Freedom of No-Limit

In limit poker, the bet sizes are fixed by the rules of the game. You can bet exactly one small bet or one big bet, and that is all. No-limit poker removes this constraint entirely: at any point, a player may bet any amount from the minimum (typically one big blind) up to their entire stack.

This freedom creates a rich and fascinating optimization problem. Rather than asking only "should I bet?" we must also ask "how much should I bet?" The answer, as we will see, depends on the relationship between bet sizing, bluffing frequency, and the opponent's optimal response.

In the previous chapters, we developed the half-street framework with fixed bet sizes. Now we allow the bettor to choose the bet size, and we examine how this choice affects the equilibrium strategies of both players.

Optimal Bet Sizing Principles

When choosing a bet size in no-limit poker, there are several competing forces at work. Understanding these forces is essential to finding the optimal bet size in any situation.

Larger Bets Allow More Bluffing

Recall from the half-street game that the optimal bluff-to-value ratio is determined by the pot odds the opponent is getting. When you bet larger, you offer worse pot odds to the caller, which means you can bluff more frequently without being exploited.

Bluff-to-Value Ratio Bluff Ratio = b / (b + P)

where b is the bet size and P is the pot before the bet. As b increases, this ratio approaches 1 — meaning you can bluff nearly as often as you value bet.

Larger Value Bets Extract More

When you hold a strong hand and your opponent calls, a larger bet wins a larger amount. If your hand is strong enough that the opponent will call at some frequency regardless, increasing the bet size directly increases your expected value from value betting.

But Larger Bets Risk More

The counterbalancing force: when you bet larger and get called by a better hand, you lose more. This creates a natural tension. The optimal bet size balances these three forces.

The Bet Sizing Tradeoff

The optimal bet size maximizes expected value by balancing three factors:

  • Bluff profitability: larger bets make bluffs more profitable when they succeed
  • Value extraction: larger bets win more when called by worse hands
  • Risk exposure: larger bets lose more when called by better hands

The Bet Size / Bluff Ratio Relationship

From the half-street framework developed in Chapter 11, we can derive the optimal bluff frequency for any bet size. The key formula connects bet size to the fraction of your betting range that should consist of bluffs.

Optimal Bluff Ratio Formula Bluff Frequency = b / (b + P)

where b = bet size, P = current pot size

Let us examine specific bet sizes relative to the pot:

Bet Size As Fraction of Pot Bluff Ratio Bluffs per Value Bet Caller's MDF
P/3 33% 1/4 = 25% 1 bluff per 3 value bets 75%
P/2 50% 1/3 ≈ 33% 1 bluff per 2 value bets 67%
2P/3 67% 2/5 = 40% 2 bluffs per 3 value bets 60%
P 100% 1/2 = 50% 1 bluff per 1 value bet 50%
2P 200% 2/3 ≈ 67% 2 bluffs per 1 value bet 33%
Reading the Table

When you bet pot-sized (b = P), the bluff ratio is P/(P+P) = 1/2. This means for every value bet you make, you should also include one bluff in your range. Your opponent, facing a pot-sized bet, needs to call with at least 50% of their range to prevent your bluffs from being automatically profitable.

When you bet half-pot (b = P/2), the ratio drops to (P/2)/(P/2 + P) = 1/3. Now you bluff only one-third as often as you value bet — one bluff for every two value bets. Your opponent must defend more frequently (67%) because they are getting better pot odds.

The Caller's Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)

The MDF is the minimum frequency at which the caller must continue to prevent the bettor's bluffs from being automatically profitable. It is the complement of the bluff ratio from the caller's perspective:

Minimum Defense Frequency MDF = P / (P + b) = 1 − Bluff Ratio

If the caller folds more than (1 − MDF), the bettor profits from bluffing regardless of the cards. This is why understanding bet sizing is critical for both the bettor and the caller.

Polarized vs. Linear Ranges

A crucial concept in no-limit bet sizing is the shape of the betting range. There are two fundamental approaches:

Polarized Range

A polarized betting range consists of very strong hands (value bets) and very weak hands (bluffs), while medium-strength hands are checked. The range is "split" into two poles with nothing in between.

Linear (Merged) Range

A linear betting range consists of all hands above a certain threshold. You bet with strong and medium hands, checking only the weakest portion of your range. The range forms a continuous line from strong to moderate.

When to Use Each Strategy

  • Polarized ranges pair with larger bet sizes. When you bet big, you need the clear value/bluff separation. Medium hands do not benefit from a large bet because they are often behind when called.
  • Linear ranges pair with smaller bet sizes. When you bet small, medium hands can extract thin value. The small bet does not need the extreme hand-strength separation.
  • Board texture matters. On dry boards (few draws), smaller bets with linear ranges work well. On wet boards (many draws), polarized ranges with larger bets help protect against draws and maximize value.
Polarized vs. Linear in Practice

Polarized example: On the river with a pot of $100, you hold either the nuts or a busted draw. You bet $100 (pot-sized). Your range is purely strong hands and bluffs — there is no middle ground. You bluff with 50% of your betting range.

Linear example: On a dry flop of K♠7♣2♦, you bet $15 into a $50 pot with all Kings, all pocket pairs above 7, and AQ/AJ. You are not bluffing at all — you are betting a continuous range of hands that can extract value from worse hands that call.

Overbetting: Beyond Pot-Sized Bets

In many poker discussions, betting more than the pot ("overbetting") is considered unusual or aggressive. However, the mathematics reveals that overbetting can be optimal in the right circumstances.

When Overbetting is Correct

Overbetting becomes optimal when:

  • The pot is small relative to the remaining stacks (deep-stacked play)
  • Your range is highly polarized — you hold either very strong hands or complete air
  • The opponent's range is capped — they cannot have the strongest possible hands
Overbetting Bluff Ratio For a bet of 2P (twice the pot):
Bluff Ratio = 2P / (2P + P) = 2/3 ≈ 67%

For a bet of 3P (three times the pot):
Bluff Ratio = 3P / (3P + P) = 3/4 = 75%

The larger the overbet, the higher the allowed bluff frequency. This can be extremely profitable when the opponent's range is weak. The opponent faces a terrible decision: call a huge bet with a mediocre hand, or fold and allow frequent bluffs to succeed.

The Overbet Scenario

The pot is $50 and you have $200 behind. The board is A♥K♠7♦3♣2♥. You can represent a strong Ace or a flush/straight and your opponent likely has a medium-strength hand like KQ or QQ.

A $150 overbet (3x pot) means your bluff ratio is 75%. Out of every 4 bets you make in this spot, 3 can be bluffs and only 1 needs to be a value bet. The opponent must call with only 25% of their range — making it very difficult for them to defend properly with their capped range.

Practical Guidelines

While the mathematical framework provides precise answers for simplified games, real poker adds complexity. Here are the key practical takeaways:

No Universal "Correct" Bet Size

The optimal bet size depends on:

  • Your range: How polarized or linear is your range in this spot?
  • Opponent's range: Is it capped? How elastic is it (how much does their calling frequency change with bet size)?
  • Board texture: Are there draws? Is the board coordinated or dry?
  • Stack depth: How much is left to bet on future streets?
  • Street: On the river (last decision point), polarized strategies with larger bets are more common. On the flop, smaller bets with linear ranges are often preferred.

The Geometric Bet Sizing Principle

When betting across multiple streets, the optimal approach often involves sizing bets so that the pot grows geometrically to reach all-in by the river. This ensures you use the maximum number of betting streets to build the pot.

Geometric Sizing over N Streets Each street's bet as fraction of pot: f = (S/P)1/N − 1

where S = remaining stack, P = current pot, N = streets remaining

For example, if the pot is $100 and you have $700 behind with 3 streets to bet, each bet should be approximately 100% of the pot (f ≈ 1.0), growing the pot from $100 to $300 to $900 to $2700 — but since that exceeds the stack, the geometric solution is to bet roughly 91% of the pot each street.

Key Concepts

  • In no-limit poker, the bet size is a strategic variable that must be optimized alongside the decision to bet at all.
  • The optimal bluff-to-value ratio is b/(b+P), where b is the bet and P is the pot. Larger bets permit more bluffs.
  • The caller's minimum defense frequency is P/(P+b). Folding more than this makes the bettor's bluffs automatically profitable.
  • Polarized ranges (strong hands + bluffs) pair with larger bets; linear ranges (all hands above a threshold) pair with smaller bets.
  • Overbetting (betting more than the pot) is optimal when the pot is small relative to stacks and your range is highly polarized against a capped opponent.
  • No single "correct" bet size exists — the optimal size depends on your range, the opponent's range, board texture, stack depth, and street.
