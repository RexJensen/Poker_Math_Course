Headsup With High Blinds: The Jam-or-Fold Game

Where game theory meets real poker — the solved push/fold strategy for short-stacked play

📄 Download Chapter PDF

From Toy Games to Real Poker

Chapter 11 introduced half-street games using abstract hand distributions. Now Chen and Ankenman bridge the gap between theory and practice with a model that is both mathematically solved and directly applicable to real poker situations.

The jam-or-fold game (also called push/fold) is perhaps the single most practically useful result in the entire book. If you play tournaments, sit-and-gos, or any form of poker where stacks get short, this chapter changes how you play.

The Jam-or-Fold Game

Headsup no-limit hold’em with a simplified action: the button (small blind) either moves all-in or folds. If the button pushes, the big blind either calls or folds. No other actions are permitted — no limping, no raising to a smaller amount, no postflop play.

Game Structure

The setup is normalized as follows:

  • Small blind (button): posts 0.5 units
  • Big blind: posts 1 unit
  • Stack sizes: both players start with S units (measured in big blinds)
  • Button’s choice: fold (lose 0.5) or push all-in (S units)
  • Big blind’s choice (if button pushes): fold (lose 1 unit) or call (S units total)

This simplification eliminates postflop play entirely, making the game tractable to solve exactly. The key question becomes: which hands should the button push, and which hands should the big blind call?

When Does This Apply?

The jam-or-fold model is an excellent approximation of optimal play when:

  • The effective stack is approximately 10 big blinds or less
  • You are on the button (small blind) headsup
  • Play is in a tournament or SNG where stack sizes regularly shrink

Above roughly 10-15 BB, postflop play and smaller raise sizes add enough complexity that the pure push/fold model becomes too restrictive. But below that threshold, the optimal strategy is very close to jam-or-fold.

Real-World Applications

You’ll use push/fold strategy in:

  • SNG endgame — final 2-3 players with short stacks
  • Tournament bubble — approaching the money with a short stack
  • Heads-up play — any time stacks are shallow
  • Blind vs. blind battles — when folded to the small blind
  • Late-stage MTTs — escalating blinds compress stacks

Mathematical Framework

Button’s Decision

The button pushes a hand when the expected value of pushing exceeds the expected value of folding:

Button Push Condition EV(push) > EV(fold) = −0.5

When the button pushes, one of two things happens:

  • BB folds (probability f): Button wins 1.5 units (the blinds)
  • BB calls (probability 1−f): Showdown for 2S units. Button wins with equity E against BB’s calling range.
EV of Pushing EV(push) = f × 1.5 + (1−f) × [E × 2S − S]

Big Blind’s Decision

The big blind calls when the expected value of calling exceeds folding:

Big Blind Call Condition EV(call) > EV(fold) = −1

The big blind has already posted 1 unit. When calling the push:

EV of Calling EV(call) = EBB × 2S − S

where EBB is the big blind’s equity against the button’s pushing range.

Equity vs. a Range

To evaluate whether to push or call, we need to compute the equity of our specific hand against the opponent’s entire range of hands. This is done by averaging our win probability against every hand in the opponent’s range, weighted by the probability of each hand.

Key Results at 8 BB (16 chips)

With 16 chips (8 big blinds), the button should push approximately 61.7% of all hands. This includes many hands that feel “unplayable” to most players:

  • All pairs
  • All aces, all kings, all queens
  • Most jacks, most tens
  • Many suited connectors and one-gappers
  • A huge range of hands most players would consider folding

The big blind’s calling range is significantly tighter — roughly the top 30-40% of hands, depending on exact stack size.

How Pushing Ranges Change with Stack Size

Stack (BB)Approx. Button Push %Approx. BB Call %Notes
1 BB100%~100%Mandatory — almost always correct to get money in
2 BB~95%~70%Push nearly everything, BB calls very wide
3 BB~85%~55%Still extremely wide pushes
5 BB~75%~40%Wide but starting to tighten
8 BB~62%~33%The classic short-stack scenario
10 BB~55%~28%Near the upper limit of jam-or-fold
15 BB~40%~22%Model becomes less accurate

The pattern is clear: as stacks get shorter, pushing ranges widen dramatically. With only 1-2 BB, you should push nearly any two cards.

Why Push So Wide?

Folding the button costs 0.5 BB every hand. At 5 BB, you lose 10% of your stack per orbit (2 hands). The fold equity from pushing — the chance the BB folds and you win 1.5 BB uncontested — makes pushing marginally profitable even with weak hands. The math:

Even a weak push can be profitable If BB folds 60% of the time, pushing any hand gains:
EV = 0.60 × 1.5 + 0.40 × [equity × 2S − S]
Even with 35% equity when called: EV = 0.90 + 0.40 × [0.35 × 10 − 5] = 0.90 + 0.40 × (-1.5) = +0.30

Practical Application

The jam-or-fold game is one of the rare cases in poker where you can play provably optimally. Here is how to use it:

Tournament Scenario

You’re heads-up in a SNG with 7 BB on the button. You look down at 9♠7♥. Most players would fold this. But the Nash equilibrium says push.

Why? If the big blind folds ~65% of the time, you win 1.5 BB uncontested most of the time. When called, you still have ~35-40% equity against typical calling ranges. The combination of fold equity and showdown equity makes this a profitable push.

Key practical insights:

  • Most players push too tight. If you’re only pushing top 30% at 7 BB, you’re bleeding chips.
  • Most players call too loose. Seeing an all-in as “cheap” is a trap — you need strong equity against a wide range.
  • Memorize key stack sizes. Know your approximate push/call ranges for 3, 5, 7, and 10 BB.
  • ICM modifies ranges. In multi-player tournaments, the independent chip model (ICM) adjusts ranges based on payout structure, but the fundamental framework remains.

Key Concepts

  • The jam-or-fold game is a solved model where the button either pushes all-in or folds, and the big blind calls or folds.
  • It is directly applicable to real poker when effective stacks are approximately 10 BB or less.
  • Optimal pushing ranges are far wider than most players expect — at 8 BB, push ~62% of hands.
  • Fold equity is a major component — even weak hands profit when opponents fold often enough.
  • As stacks get shorter, pushing ranges widen because the cost of folding (losing the blind) becomes relatively larger.
  • This is one of the few areas in poker where you can play provably optimal strategy by memorizing charts.
