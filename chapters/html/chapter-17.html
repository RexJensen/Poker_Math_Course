Part III — Chapter 17

Mixing in Bluffs: Finite Pot [0,1] Games

Reintroducing the fold option — how bluffing emerges from strategic necessity in continuous-distribution games

Adding Folding Back

In Chapter 16, we explored [0,1] games where players could only bet and call — no folding allowed. That simplified model let us isolate the value-betting decision: when should you put money in because you expect to be ahead? But real poker is far richer than that.

Now we reintroduce the option to fold. This single addition transforms the game entirely. Once a player can fold, a new strategic weapon appears: the bluff. If your opponent will sometimes fold, then even your worst hands can profit by betting — not because they expect to win at showdown, but because they expect to win the pot uncontested.

With folding restored, the full strategic complexity of poker comes alive. The bettor must decide which hands to bet for value, which to check, and which to bluff. The defender must decide which hands to call and which to surrender. Both decisions are locked in a tense equilibrium where each player's strategy constrains the other's.

The Finite Pot [0,1] Game

The [0,1] Game with Folding

Each player is dealt a hand uniformly at random from the interval [0, 1], where higher numbers are stronger. There is a pot of size P. One player (the bettor) may bet b or check. If the bettor checks, hands are revealed and the higher hand wins the pot. If the bettor bets, the opponent may call b or fold.

Game Setup Hands: x, y ~ Uniform[0, 1]
Pot: P > 0   Bet: b > 0
Bettor: bet b or check
Defender: call b or fold

This model is simple enough to solve analytically, yet complex enough to capture the fundamental tension of poker: the interplay between value betting, bluffing, and calling. Every concept that emerges here — bluff frequencies, calling thresholds, the indifference principle — has a direct analog in real-world poker.

The Three-Region Strategy

The optimal strategy for the bettor in this game divides the [0,1] interval into three distinct regions. This is one of the most beautiful and practical results in poker mathematics.

The Three Regions

The bettor's optimal strategy partitions the hand space [0, 1] into exactly three zones:

  • Top Region (near 1.0): Value Bet — These hands are strong enough to bet expecting to be called by worse hands. They profit from the call.
  • Middle Region: Check — These hands have showdown value (they can often win if checked down) but are not strong enough to bet for value. Betting them would only get called by better hands.
  • Bottom Region (near 0.0): Bluff — These hands have no showdown value. They cannot win at showdown, so they lose nothing by bluffing. If the opponent folds, they win a pot they would otherwise lose.

The exact boundaries depend on P and b. With a larger pot, the value-bet region expands (more hands are worth betting) and the bluff region shrinks (opponent calls more often, making bluffs less profitable).

The elegance of this result is that it mirrors the structure of optimal play in real poker. Strong hands bet for value. Medium hands try to get to showdown cheaply. And bluffs come from the very bottom of the range — a point we will explore in depth below.

The Optimal Bluff Threshold

How much should the bettor bluff? This is determined by the indifference principle: the bettor must bluff at exactly the frequency that makes the defender indifferent between calling and folding with their marginal hand.

Indifference Principle for Bluffing

At equilibrium, the defender's marginal calling hand must have the same EV whether it calls or folds. This pins down the ratio of bluffs to value bets in the bettor's range.

Equation 17.1 — Bluff-to-Value Ratio Bluffs / (Value Bets + Bluffs) = b / (b + P)

Equivalently, the fraction of the bettor's betting range that is bluffs equals:

Equation 17.2 — Bluff Frequency α = b / (b + P)
Numerical Example

Suppose P = 10 (pot) and b = 5 (bet). Then:

Calculation α = 5 / (5 + 10) = 5 / 15 = 1/3 ≈ 33.3%

One-third of the bettor's bets should be bluffs. If the bettor value bets with 20% of hands, they should bluff with 10% of hands, for a total betting frequency of 30%. The ratio of bluffs to total bets is 10/30 = 1/3.

Why 1/3? Because the defender is getting pot odds of 5:15 (1:3) to call. The defender needs to win at least 1/4 of the time to break even calling. If exactly 1/3 of bets are bluffs, the defender wins (by calling a bluff) exactly 1/3 of the time, which is more than the 1/4 needed — but the bettor balances this by choosing the right value-bet threshold to make calling and folding equally attractive for the defender's marginal hand.

Defender's Optimal Response

The defender faces the inverse problem: given that the bettor is betting with a mix of strong hands and bluffs, which hands should the defender call with?

Optimal Calling Threshold

The defender's calling threshold is set to make the bettor indifferent between bluffing and checking with their marginal bluff. The defender calls with hands that beat enough of the bettor's range to justify the call.

Equation 17.3 — Calling Threshold Defender calls when: hand ≥ 1 − (Value Bet Region) − α × (Value Bet Region) / (1 − α)

In practice, the calling threshold is determined by the pot odds offered by the bet. The defender needs to be correct at least b / (P + 2b) fraction of the time to profit from calling.

The symmetry is striking: the bettor bluffs at a rate to make the defender indifferent about calling, and the defender calls at a rate to make the bettor indifferent about bluffing. Neither player can improve their EV by deviating unilaterally — this is the Nash equilibrium.

Key Insight: Bluff with the Worst Hands

This is perhaps the most counterintuitive and important lesson of the [0,1] game: bluffs should come from the absolute worst hands, not from medium-strength hands.

Why Bluffs Come from the Bottom

A medium-strength hand has showdown value — it can win if both players check. Bluffing with such a hand means sacrificing this value. If the opponent calls, you lose both the showdown AND the bet. If the opponent folds, you gain the pot but give up the showdown equity you already had.

By contrast, a truly weak hand (near 0.0) has zero showdown value. It will lose at showdown regardless. Bluffing costs nothing extra — you are turning a hand worth zero into a hand that sometimes wins the pot.

Medium vs. Weak Hand Bluffing

Suppose you hold hand = 0.45 (medium) vs. hand = 0.05 (weak). The pot is 10 and the bet is 5.

Hand 0.45 (medium): If you check, you win the pot ~45% of the time at showdown (EV = 0.45 × 10 = 4.5). If you bluff, and the opponent calls ~60% of the time, your EV from bluffing is negative compared to checking.

Hand 0.05 (weak): If you check, you win the pot ~5% of the time (EV = 0.05 × 10 = 0.5). If you bluff, and the opponent folds ~40% of the time, your EV from bluffing is 0.4 × 10 − 0.6 × 5 = 4.0 − 3.0 = 1.0. That is better than 0.5!

The weak hand gains from bluffing; the medium hand does not. This is why optimal bluffs come from the bottom of the range.

This mirrors the lesson from the AKQ game (Chapter 15): in that game, the optimal strategy was to bluff with the weakest hand (Q) rather than the medium hand (K). The K has showdown value against the Q, so bluffing with it wastes that advantage. The same principle scales to the continuous [0,1] distribution.

Pot Size Effects

The size of the pot profoundly affects the equilibrium strategy. Understanding these effects builds intuition that transfers directly to real poker.

Large Pot vs. Small Pot
FactorLarge Pot (P = 20, b = 5)Small Pot (P = 4, b = 5)
Pot Odds for Defender 5:25 = 1:5 (need 16.7% to call) 5:9 ≈ 1:1.8 (need 35.7% to call)
Defender Calls... More often (great pot odds) Less often (poor pot odds)
Bluff Frequency (α) 5/25 = 20% (less bluffing) 5/9 = 55.6% (more bluffing)
Intuition Opponent calls wide, bluffs rarely work Opponent folds often, bluffs are cheap
Equation 17.4 — Pot Size and Strategy As P → ∞: α → 0 (no bluffing, opponent always calls)
As P → 0: α → b/(b+0) = 1 (all bluffs, opponent never calls)

The practical lesson: in large pots, lean toward value betting. Your opponent is getting excellent odds to call and will do so frequently. In small pots, you can bluff more aggressively because your opponent's calling odds are worse. This is why position and pot manipulation are so important in real poker — controlling the pot size is controlling the game's incentive structure.

Key Concepts

  • Folding reintroduces bluffing. Without the ability to fold, there is no reason to bluff. Once folding is possible, bluffing becomes a powerful and necessary strategic tool.
  • Three-region strategy: Optimal play divides hands into value bets (top), checks (middle), and bluffs (bottom). This tripartite structure is fundamental to poker strategy.
  • Bluff with the worst hands, not medium hands. Medium hands have showdown value that bluffing would sacrifice. The weakest hands have nothing to lose, making them ideal bluff candidates.
  • The bluff frequency α = b/(b+P) makes the defender indifferent between calling and folding with marginal hands. This is a direct application of the indifference principle.
  • Larger pots mean less bluffing. When the pot is large, opponents get better odds to call, making bluffs less profitable. Smaller pots create more bluffing opportunities.
  • Nash equilibrium arises from mutual indifference: the bettor bluffs enough to keep the defender uncertain, and the defender calls enough to keep the bettor from profiting with reckless bluffs.
