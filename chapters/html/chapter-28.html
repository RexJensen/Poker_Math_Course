Poker’s Still Poker: Tournaments, Part III

Beyond ICM — skill edges, tournament stages, and the limits of mathematical models

Beyond ICM

In Chapters 26 and 27, we developed the Independent Chip Model and showed how it transforms tournament decision-making. ICM is a powerful and widely-used tool — but it is a simplification. This chapter examines what ICM gets wrong, when to deviate from its recommendations, and how to think about tournament poker more holistically.

The title “Poker’s Still Poker” reminds us that despite the mathematical overlay of ICM calculations, tournaments are still poker games played by people with different skill levels, tendencies, and psychological states. A purely mechanical application of ICM leaves significant value on the table.

The ICM Paradox

ICM is simultaneously the best available model for tournament equity AND a model with significant blind spots. It provides the foundation for tournament strategy while systematically ignoring several factors that a complete theory would incorporate. Understanding both its power and its limitations is essential for advanced tournament play.

ICM Limitations

ICM makes several simplifying assumptions that don’t hold in real tournament play. Recognizing these limitations helps you know when to trust ICM and when to adjust.

Assumption 1: Equal Skill

ICM assumes every player has an equal probability of finishing in any position, weighted only by chip stack. In reality, skilled players outperform their chip-stack-predicted finishing positions, and weak players underperform.

Equal Skill Assumption in Practice

Two players each have 10,000 chips in a tournament. ICM gives them identical equity. But if Player A is a world-class professional and Player B is a recreational player in their first tournament, their actual equity is vastly different. Player A will convert those chips into higher finishes far more often than ICM predicts.

ICM cannot distinguish between these two players — it sees only the chip counts.

Assumption 2: Static Model

ICM takes a snapshot of current chip stacks and computes equity as if the tournament ended immediately with random outcomes. It doesn’t account for:

  • Blind structure: Rising blinds change the effective stack depths and favor aggressive play. ICM doesn’t model future blind increases.
  • Position at the table: Being on the button vs under-the-gun matters enormously for the next few hands, but ICM ignores seat position entirely.
  • Future play: ICM doesn’t consider that chips won now can be leveraged in future hands. A skilled player’s chips are worth more because they’ll play future hands better.

Assumption 3: Short-Stack Accuracy Bias

ICM is most accurate when stacks are short relative to the blinds (push/fold territory). When stacks are deep, there are many more decisions ahead, and the equal-skill assumption creates larger errors.

ICM Accuracy Spectrum

Most accurate: Short-stacked play (under 10 BB), near the bubble, in push/fold situations where there are few decisions remaining. Least accurate: Deep-stacked play (50+ BB), early in tournaments, when significant postflop skill advantages exist. In deep-stacked situations, ICM should be treated as a rough guide rather than a precise calculator.

Skill Advantage in Tournaments

If you are a significantly better player than your opponents, you should sometimes deviate from ICM-optimal play. The reason is straightforward: ICM assumes you’ll convert chips at an average rate, but a skilled player converts chips at an above-average rate.

Skill-Adjusted Chip Value Effective Chip Value = ICM Chip Value × Skill Multiplier

If you convert chips 20% better than average:
Skill Multiplier ≈ 1.2

Winning 1,000 chips is worth ~20% more to you than ICM suggests,
because you’ll play future hands better than the average opponent.

When to Deviate from ICM

  • Marginal +cEV / −$EV spots: If ICM says a call is slightly −$EV but the cEV is positive and your skill edge is significant, taking the spot may actually be +$EV when skill is factored in. The chips you win will generate above-average returns in future play.
  • Against weak players: When you have a large skill advantage over a specific opponent, slightly −$EV gambles can become +$EV because you’ll exploit that player in future hands if you win chips.
  • Early in the tournament: With many hands remaining, your skill edge has more time to compound. ICM considerations are weakest here, and cash-game-like aggression is more appropriate.
Skill-Adjusted Decision Making

You’re an experienced professional at a final table with 5 recreational players. ICM says a particular all-in call is −$3 (very slightly negative). Pure ICM play says fold.

But consider: if you win this pot, you’ll have a big stack at a table full of weak players. Your ability to exploit them in future hands makes those chips worth significantly more than ICM credits. The −$3 ICM result might actually be +$20 or more when skill edge is factored in.

Key principle: The greater your skill edge, the more you should favor chip accumulation over pure ICM survival.

Skill Discount on Risk Premium

A skilled player’s risk premium should be lower than ICM suggests, because: (1) chips they win are worth more than average due to superior future play, and (2) chips they lose hurt less because they can rebuild more efficiently. The greater the skill edge, the larger the discount. At the extreme, a massively skilled player should play almost like a cash game — maximizing cEV and trusting their edge to convert chips efficiently.

Stack Size Strategy

Your strategic approach should change dramatically based on your stack size relative to the blinds and the average stack. Each stack category has its own optimal approach.

Short Stack (Under 10 BB)

  • Game mode: Push/fold poker (as analyzed in Chapter 12)
  • Key principle: Your decisions are simplified to all-in or fold. No postflop play.
  • ICM consideration: Risk premium is relatively low because you have little equity to protect. You must take opportunities to double up or you’ll be blinded out.
  • Skill edge: Minimal. Push/fold is largely a mathematical exercise where everyone plays similarly. This is where ICM is most accurate.

Medium Stack (15–30 BB)

  • Game mode: The most constrained and challenging stack size
  • Key principle: Avoid marginal situations. You have enough chips that busting is costly (high risk premium) but not enough to freely gamble.
  • ICM consideration: Maximum risk premium, especially on the bubble. Be selective about the spots you play.
  • Skill edge: Hard to leverage. Your stack doesn’t allow for deep postflop play, and ICM constraints limit your aggression.

Big Stack (40+ BB)

  • Game mode: Aggressive pressure and leverage
  • Key principle: Use your stack to put pressure on medium stacks who can’t afford to gamble. Your risk premium is low — losing a portion of your stack barely dents your equity.
  • ICM consideration: You benefit the most from ICM distortions. Other players’ fear of busting gives you leverage.
  • Skill edge: Maximum opportunity to leverage. Deep stacks allow postflop play where skill differences are largest.

Tournament Stages

A tournament is not one long game — it’s a series of phases, each with distinct strategic characteristics. ICM’s influence waxes and wanes throughout.

Stage 1: Early Game (Deep Stacked)

Early Tournament Play

Stack depth: 50–200+ BB. ICM impact: Minimal.

Play is closest to cash game strategy. The money bubble is far away, so survival equity is small. Focus on chip accumulation using solid fundamental poker. Skill edges are at their maximum because deep stacks allow complex postflop play.

Key adjustments from cash: Preserve your stack for future opportunities. Don’t go broke with one-pair hands in marginal spots — you can’t rebuy. But don’t play so conservatively that you miss valuable opportunities.

Stage 2: Middle Game (Approaching the Bubble)

Middle Stage Play

Stack depth: 15–50 BB. ICM impact: Increasing.

As the bubble approaches, ICM pressure begins to affect play. Short stacks become desperate, medium stacks tighten up, and big stacks start leveraging their position. The transition from middle to bubble is gradual — ICM effects intensify with each elimination.

Key adjustments: Start considering pay jumps in your decisions. Avoid marginal spots with medium stacks. Take advantage of ICM-aware opponents who are tightening up too early.

Stage 3: The Bubble

Bubble Play

Stack depth: Varies widely. ICM impact: Maximum.

This is the point of maximum ICM distortion. The jump from “nothing” (bubbling out) to “min-cash” is proportionally the largest pay jump in the tournament. Every decision must be evaluated through the ICM lens.

Key dynamics:

  • Short stacks try to survive one more elimination
  • Medium stacks play extremely tight
  • Big stacks raise relentlessly, exploiting others’ fear
  • The “bubble factor” (risk premium) reaches its peak

Stage 4: In the Money

Post-Bubble Play

Stack depth: Varies. ICM impact: Decreased (temporarily).

Immediately after the bubble bursts, ICM pressure drops significantly. The massive pay jump (0 to min-cash) is behind you, and the next several pay jumps are typically small. This creates a window for more aggressive play.

Key adjustments: Many short stacks who were nursing their chips on the bubble will now push freely. This is a good time to pick up chips if you have a solid hand. The field loosens up considerably right after the bubble.

Stage 5: Final Table

Final Table Play

Stack depth: Varies widely. ICM impact: Renewed (large pay jumps).

Pay jumps at the final table are often enormous. The difference between 9th and 1st might be 10x or more. Each elimination creates a significant equity boost for survivors. This introduces new ICM pressure, creating “mini-bubbles” at each pay jump.

Key dynamics: Stack sizes relative to each other matter as much as absolute stack sizes. Short stacks at the final table have significant equity from potential laddering. Deal-making becomes a viable option.

Long-Term Tournament Success

Tournament poker is characterized by extremely high variance. Understanding the long-term mathematical reality is essential for maintaining both strategy and sanity.

Return on Investment (ROI)

A successful tournament player’s ROI is typically much lower than most people expect:

  • Top professional: 30–100% ROI in small-to-mid-field tournaments
  • Strong player: 15–30% ROI
  • Winning recreational: 5–15% ROI
  • Large-field tournaments (1000+ players): Even top pros may have only 30–50% ROI due to variance
Tournament ROI ROI = (Total Winnings − Total Buy-ins) / Total Buy-ins × 100%

Example: $50,000 in winnings from $40,000 in buy-ins
ROI = ($50,000 − $40,000) / $40,000 × 100% = 25%

Variance and Sample Size

Tournament variance is staggering. Even a highly profitable player can go hundreds of tournaments without a significant cash. This is not a flaw in their game — it’s a mathematical certainty.

The “Tournament of Life”

Chen and Ankenman use the concept of the “tournament of life” to frame long-term thinking. Every tournament you play is one event in a much larger sample. The key insights:

  • Any single tournament is meaningless for evaluating your skill. You can play perfectly and bust first, or play terribly and win.
  • Focus on decisions, not results. Over thousands of tournaments, correct decisions compound into profit.
  • Downswings are inevitable. A player with 50% ROI will still have months-long losing streaks. This is variance, not evidence of poor play.
  • Sample size matters enormously. You need hundreds (often thousands) of tournaments to distinguish skill from luck with statistical confidence.

Field Size and Edge

Your edge per tournament decreases as field size increases, but the potential prize increases. This creates a tradeoff:

The Tournament of Life

The philosophical and mathematical framework that treats a player’s entire tournament career as a single, ongoing sample. Individual tournament results are data points, not conclusions. True skill is revealed only over a large sample — hundreds or thousands of events. This perspective helps maintain optimal decision-making during inevitable downswings and prevents results-oriented thinking.

Key Concepts

  • ICM is a simplification that assumes equal skill, ignores position and blinds, and doesn’t model future play. It is most accurate for short-stacked push/fold decisions.
  • A skilled player should sometimes deviate from ICM by taking marginally −$EV spots, because their skill edge makes accumulated chips worth more than ICM credits.
  • Stack size dictates strategy: short stacks play push/fold, medium stacks are most constrained, big stacks leverage ICM pressure.
  • Tournaments progress through distinct stages: early (cash-like), middle (increasing ICM), bubble (maximum ICM), post-bubble (ICM relief), final table (renewed ICM from pay jumps).
  • Long-term tournament success requires understanding ROI, variance, and sample size. Even strong winners endure long losing stretches.
  • The “tournament of life” framework treats all tournaments as one large sample, emphasizing decision quality over individual results.
  • The skill discount on risk premium means better players should tolerate more variance than ICM alone suggests, especially against weak fields.
