Poker's Still Poker: Tournaments, Part III

Beyond ICM — when theory meets the full complexity of tournament play

ICM's Limitations

ICM is a powerful model, but it makes significant simplifying assumptions:

  • Assumes equal skill: ICM treats all players as equally likely to win each chip. In reality, skill differences are significant.
  • Static model: ICM is a snapshot — it doesn't account for future play, position, or blind increases.
  • No blind structure: Ignores the fact that blinds increase, forcing action and changing stack dynamics.
  • Better for short stacks: ICM is most accurate for push/fold situations, less so for deep-stacked play.

When to Deviate from ICM

Skill Edge Adjustment

A skilled player should sometimes deviate from pure ICM recommendations. If you have a significant skill edge, taking marginally -$EV (by ICM) gambles can be justified because the chips you win will grow faster than ICM predicts — you'll outplay opponents post-flop and in future hands.

When Skill Overrides ICM

Scenario: You're a strong player at a final table with mostly weak opponents. ICM says fold AJs to a short stack's all-in because the risk premium is high.

Pure ICM: Fold (marginally -$EV call)

Skill-adjusted: Call may be correct, because winning those chips gives you a big stack to dominate the weak field. The future edge you gain exceeds the ICM cost.

Stack Size Strategy

Stack SizeStrategyKey Principle
Short (<10 BB)Push/fold (Chapter 12)Every chip is precious; maximize fold equity with all-in moves
Medium (15-30 BB)Most constrainedAvoid marginal spots; let short stacks bust; pick premium spots
Big (40+ BB)Pressure othersUse stack as weapon; put medium stacks in tough spots; ICM leverage

The medium stack is in the most difficult position: too many chips to play push/fold optimally, but not enough to absorb the variance of marginal plays without significant ICM cost.

Tournament Stages

Stage-by-Stage Strategy

Early (Deep Stacked): Play close to cash game strategy. ICM pressure is minimal with many players remaining. Focus on accumulating chips through skill.

Middle (Approaching Bubble): ICM pressure increases. Tighten up with medium stacks. Widen aggression with big stacks. Short stacks play push/fold.

Bubble: Maximum ICM distortion. Survival becomes critical for medium/short stacks. Big stacks exploit the fear of others. Single most important stage for ICM understanding.

In the Money: ICM pressure decreases somewhat as guaranteed min cash is secured. Players can loosen up slightly.

Final Table: New ICM pressure from large pay jumps. Every elimination is worth significant money. Play becomes extremely strategic.

Long-Term Tournament Success

Tournament poker has extraordinarily high variance. A player's ROI (Return on Investment) is measured over hundreds or thousands of events:

  • A 20% ROI is exceptional for MTTs (multi-table tournaments)
  • Even the best players have long stretches without significant cashes
  • Field size matters: larger fields mean higher variance but potentially higher ROI for skilled players
  • All your tournaments are "one big session" — the tournament of life

Key Concepts

  • ICM has significant limitations: assumes equal skill, ignores future play and blind structure.
  • Skilled players should deviate from ICM when their edge makes chip accumulation more valuable than the model suggests.
  • Stack size dictates strategy: short stacks push/fold, medium stacks avoid marginal spots, big stacks apply pressure.
  • Tournament stages create different strategic environments: early play resembles cash, bubble play is ICM-dominated.
  • Tournament success requires high volume due to extreme variance; results should be evaluated over hundreds of events.
