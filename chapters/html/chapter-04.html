Part II — Chapter 4

Playing the Odds: Pot Odds and Implied Odds

When to call, when to fold, and why future bets change everything

Games and Strategies

Before diving into pot odds, Chen and Ankenman establish fundamental definitions. A game consists of two or more players, each of whom must choose among available actions, with outcomes that depend on the choices of all players. A strategy is a complete specification of which action a player takes at every possible decision point — every path through the game tree.

Exploitive Play

Exploitive play seeks to maximize expected value by choosing the highest-EV action at each decision point, given our beliefs about the opponent's strategy. This is the foundation of poker decision-making.

Maximally Exploitive Strategy

The strategy with the highest expectation against a specific opponent strategy S. If we know exactly how our opponent plays, the maximally exploitive strategy tells us the best possible counter.

The Bluffing Threshold — Example 4.1

Consider this toy game that illustrates a critical concept:

Example 4.1 — Toy Bluffing Game

Player A holds the nuts 20% of the time and a dead hand 80% of the time. The pot contains 4 big bets. Player A always bets with the nuts, and may also bluff with some fraction x of the total hands dealt.

B's EV of calling:

EVcall = 5x − 0.2

B's EV of folding: 0

B should call when EVcall > 0, i.e., when x > 0.04 (4%).

The critical insight: a tiny change in A's bluffing frequency — crossing the 4% threshold — causes B's optimal response to shift entirely from always-fold to always-call. This threshold effect is fundamental to poker strategy.

  • If A bluffs less than 4%: B should always fold
  • If A bluffs more than 4%: B should always call
  • If A bluffs exactly 4%: B is indifferent

Pot Odds

Pot odds form the backbone of correct calling decisions. A draw should call when the expected value of calling exceeds the cost:

Pot Odds Rule

A call is correct when:

Pot Odds Inequality p(win) × (pot + call) > call

Equivalently, the minimum win probability required is:

x > cost / (pot + cost)

Two key principles from the book:

  • "In made hand vs. draw situations, the made hand usually bets."
  • "The draw usually calls if it has positive equity after calling."
Example 4.2 — AA vs Flush Draw

Player A has A♠A♦. Player B has 9♥8♥ with a flush draw. The pot is $400 in a $30-60 limit game.

B has 9 outs out of 44 unseen cards.

Probability of hitting: 9/44 ≈ 20.5%

Pot odds needed: 60 / (400 + 60) = 13.0%

Since 20.5% > 13.0%, calling is clearly correct. B's EV of calling is positive.

Pot Odds on Multiple Streets

A common mistake is evaluating pot odds one street at a time without considering the total cost across all remaining streets. Sometimes it is correct to call on the flop even without immediate odds if the combined odds across flop and river work out.

Example 4.5 — Multi-Street Calling

The pot is $135. A draw can call the flop and, if it misses, face another bet on the turn.

Key insight: B may be correct to call the flop (taking into account the probability of hitting on both the turn and river) but must fold the turn if the draw misses — because the turn call alone does not have the right odds with only one card to come.

The total cost of seeing both cards must be weighed against the total probability of hitting on either street.

Why "Raising to Give Yourself Odds" Doesn't Work

A persistent poker myth says you should raise the flop with a draw to "buy" a cheaper turn card. Chen and Ankenman dismantle this argument:

Example 4.6 — Raising vs. Calling on the Flop

Raising the flop to try to create better pot odds on the turn actually produces $20 less EV than the simpler line of calling the flop and folding the turn when you miss.

The flaw in the "raise for odds" argument: it ignores that you are putting in extra money on the flop at bad odds. The turn "free card" is not free — you paid for it with the flop raise.

This is a critical example of why analyzing total expected value over multiple streets is essential, rather than optimizing each street in isolation.

Implied Odds

When hands are not exposed (as in real poker), a draw that hits may collect additional bets from an opponent who doesn't know the draw came in. This makes the effective pot larger than the current pot.

Implied Odds

The ratio between the amount you expect to win (including future bets) and the amount you must call now. Implied odds account for the money you expect to win on later streets when your draw completes.

Implied Odds — Three-Case Breakdown

Consider a flush draw where we track the three possible outcomes:

ScenarioResult
Turn flush (hit immediately)+$285
River flush (miss turn, hit river)+$285
No flush (miss both streets)−$90

Without implied odds, the EV of calling is just $4.67.

With implied odds (collecting future bets when the flush hits), the EV jumps to $32.73.

Implied odds nearly seven times the basic EV — this is why draws are often far more profitable than simple pot odds suggest.

Bluffing, Semi-Bluffing, and Value Betting

Types of Bets
  • Pure bluff: A bet with no chance of winning if called. The only way to profit is by getting the opponent to fold.
  • Semi-bluff: A bet with a hand that is probably behind but might improve on later streets (e.g., betting a flush draw).
  • Value bet: A bet with a hand that has positive expectation even when called — you expect to win more than you lose.
  • Snowing: Bluffing with no outs on early streets, planning to continue bluffing on later streets to push the opponent off their hand.
7-Card Stud — Bluffing Thresholds

The pot is $655. Player A has open aces. Player B has a flush draw.

Let x = A's calling frequency, y = B's bluffing frequency.

  • A should call when B bluffs more than 2.2% of the time
  • B should bluff when A calls less than 89.1% of the time

This creates two player archetypes:

TypeTendencyExploitive Counter
SkepticsCall too oftenNever bluff, value bet thinly
BelieversFold too oftenBluff frequently, bet for folds

Key Concepts

  • A game requires 2+ players choosing actions whose outcomes depend on all players' choices; a strategy specifies actions at every decision point.
  • Exploitive play maximizes EV by choosing the best action against the opponent's specific strategy.
  • Pot odds compare the cost of calling to the total pot: call when p(win) > cost / (pot + cost).
  • On multiple streets, evaluate total cost vs. total probability — don't optimize each street in isolation.
  • Raising to "give yourself odds" is a fallacy — the extra flop money goes in at bad odds, costing more EV than it saves.
  • Implied odds account for future bets won when your draw hits, often making calls profitable that raw pot odds don't justify.
  • Semi-bluffs combine fold equity with draw equity; pure bluffs rely solely on fold equity.
  • Small changes in bluffing frequency can cross thresholds that completely flip the opponent's optimal response — the exploitive dynamic is discontinuous.
  • Opponents can be classified as Skeptics (call too much) or Believers (fold too much), each requiring opposite exploitive adjustments.
